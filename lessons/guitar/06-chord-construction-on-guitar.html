Building chords on the guitar

You already know what a chord is — the Theory Core built it from the ground up. A triad is a root, a third and a fifth stacked in thirds; a seventh chord adds one more third on top. This lesson does something narrower and more useful: it takes that theory and pins it to the fretboard. Once you see that every chord shape is just a fingering of root–3–5 (or root–3–5–7), the grids stop being arbitrary finger-pictures and become something you can read, alter, and rebuild anywhere on the neck.

  • Read any chord grid as a map of root, 3rd, 5th (and 7th) tones.
  • Play a triad as a compact three-string shape and move it through its inversions.
  • Build dominant-7th, major-7th and minor-7th barre chords from the E- and A-shapes.
  • Add a 7th or 9th to a shape by altering one tone — not learning a new chord.
  • Turn a lead-sheet symbol into a playable voicing, and know why one symbol has many.

1. Every shape is root–3–5 in disguise

On the staff a C major triad is three notes — C, E, G — stacked in thirds. On the guitar those same three pitches get scattered across the strings, sometimes doubled, sometimes in a different vertical order, because the instrument is tuned in fourths (mostly) rather than thirds. The notes don't change; only their arrangement does.

Now the open C chord. It is fingered x 3 2 0 1 0 (low to high). Read what each string actually sounds and you find the very same three notes — C, E and G — with the root and third doubled so all five sounding strings belong to the triad:

The open C chord, decoded

String 5 / fret 3 = C (root); string 4 / fret 2 = E (3rd); string 3 open = G (5th); string 2 / fret 1 = C (root again); string 1 open = E (3rd again). Low E (string 6) is muted because its open E would put the 3rd in the bass. Five strings, three chord tones.

Reading the labels

From now on we label every dot with its function — R for root, 3 for the third, 5 for the fifth, b7 for a minor seventh — not the letter name. That is the whole trick: a shape that you understand as “R on string 5, 3 on string 4, 5 on string 3” is the same shape for every chord; slide it and the functions travel with it. Letter names change, the geometry doesn't.

Why guitarists double notes

A triad has only three notes but the guitar offers six strings. To make a chord ring full you double tones — usually the root and the fifth, the two most stable, least colour-bearing members. The third is the colour (major vs. minor), so it is doubled more sparingly. Doubling never adds a new chord tone; it just thickens the ones you have.

2. Triads as three-string shapes

The fastest way to see root–3–5 is to strip the chord down to exactly three strings — one note per string, no doubling. These compact triad shapes live on each set of three adjacent strings (1–2–3, 2–3–4, 3–4–5, 4–5–6) and they are the cleanest map of the theory you'll find on the neck.

Here is C major on the top three strings, in root position — the root is the lowest of the three:

C major triad, top three strings

String 3 / fret 5 = C (root); string 2 / fret 5 = E (3rd); string 1 / fret 3 = G (5th). Three strings, three notes, exactly the C-E-G of the staff above — no doubling to hide the structure.

What an inversion does here

An inversion (Theory Lesson 10) just changes which chord tone is lowest. On a three-string set the three inversions are three different shapes stacked up the neck, all spelling C-E-G:

  • Root position — R on the lowest string (the shape above).
  • 1st inversion — 3rd on the lowest string.
  • 2nd inversion — 5th on the lowest string.

Same three notes, reordered. The staff shows the three inversions as the root, then the third, then the fifth taking the bottom voice:

Targets, not shapes

The point of three-string triads is not to memorise more grids — it is to stop thinking in grids at all. If you know that the 3rd of C is E and you know where every E sits, you can build a C triad anywhere by aiming at its tones. The shape is just the by-product of three targets landing on three strings.

3. Seventh chords: add one more third

A seventh chord is a triad with a fourth note a seventh above the root (Theory Lesson 11). On the guitar you almost never need a sixth string for that extra note — you convert a tone you're already fretting, or free up a string, so the shape barely moves. The most efficient example is the movable E-shape.

Take an E-shape barre major chord and lower the doubled root on the fourth string by two frets: it becomes the minor seventh (b7), and the whole grip is now a dominant-7th barre chord. Here it is rooted at the first fret — an F7 (F–A–C–E♭):

F7 — the E-shape dominant 7th

String 6 / fret 1 = F (root); string 5 / fret 3 = C (5th); string 4 / fret 1 = E♭ (the b7 — this is the one note that turns the major barre into a dom7); string 3 / fret 2 = A (3rd); string 2 / fret 1 = C (5th); string 1 / fret 1 = F (root). Barre fret 1, and slide the whole thing to play any dominant 7th.

Three sevenths, one shape

The same barre frame gives you all three common sevenths by moving one or two tones a single fret each — the chord tones are right under your fingers:

ChordTonesFrom the major barre…
Dominant 7 (7)R 3 5 b7lower the doubled root to b7
Major 7 (maj7)R 3 5 7lower that root only one fret (to the major 7th)
Minor 7 (m7)R b3 5 b7also lower the 3rd a fret to the b3

The A-shape works the same way but roots on string 5 instead of string 6 — handy for getting a different range out of the same chord. CAGED (Lesson 3) is what lets you pick which root string, and therefore which voicing, you want.

4. Extensions: keep stacking thirds

A ninth chord carries the stack one third further — R–3–5–b7–9 (the 9th is the same letter as the 2nd, an octave up; Theory Lesson 5 covered compound intervals, Lesson 21 the extended chords). On the guitar you rarely play all five notes; you swap a doubled or less-essential tone for the extension. That is the everyday move:

  • Take the F7 above and raise a doubled root a whole step (F → G) to land on the 9th — now it's an F9 colour, with no new fingering vocabulary, just one tone re-aimed.
  • The 5th is the most expendable tone in a dominant chord — drop or alter it freely to make room for the 7th, 9th, or a b5/#5 alteration. The 3rd and 7th are the guide tones that define the chord's quality, so they stay.
Why the 3rd and 7th are sacred

The third tells you major or minor; the seventh tells you major-7, dominant, or (with a lowered third) minor-7. Together they are the chord's identity — in a dominant seventh they even form the internal tritone that drives the chord home (Theory Lesson 11). The root is often supplied by the bass and the fifth adds little colour, so when strings run out, those are the two you sacrifice. Jazz guitarists voice whole progressions on just the 3rd and 7th for exactly this reason.

5. One symbol, many voicings

A lead sheet writes C, or Cmaj7, or G7 — a single symbol that names a set of notes, not a fingering. Theory fixes the notes; the guitar offers a dozen legal ways to lay them out. The open C chord, the C triad on the top three strings, an A-shape C barre at the third fret, a C on the middle strings — all of them are spelled C-E-G, so all of them are “C.” The symbol underdetermines the shape on purpose.

To go from symbol to shape, read it in two halves:

Symbol partTells youOn the neck
Letter (root)which note is the rootpick the root string & fret (use CAGED)
Quality (m, maj7, 7, dim…)which 3rd / 5th / 7th to usechoose the matching shape (major, m7, dom7…)

So G7 = root G + dominant-7th quality → put an E-shape dom7 root on the G of string 6 (fret 3), or an A-shape dom7 on the G of string 5 (fret 10), or the open G7 grip. Choose by range, voice-leading, and what your fingers can reach — the theory is satisfied by any of them.

The practical payoff

When a chart throws a chord you've “never played,” you don't need to look it up. Read the symbol → know the tones (R, 3, 5, and maybe 7/9) → find those tones on a string set you can reach. Chord construction on the guitar is just this loop, run fast.
