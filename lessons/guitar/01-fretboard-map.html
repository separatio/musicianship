The fretboard as a map

The guitar neck looks like a thicket of dots and lines, but it is laid out with total regularity: six strings, each fret a single half step, the whole thing just the chromatic scale printed on a grid. Learn how that grid is built and you stop memorising 72 unrelated note positions and start deriving them. This lesson hands you the map — the anchor strings, the octave shapes, and the one quirk (the B string) that trips everyone up — so the rest of the neck becomes legible.

  • Read the neck's layout: six strings E-A-D-G-B-E, one fret = one half step.
  • Find any natural note on the low-E and A strings without counting from the nut every time.
  • Use the “+2 frets, +2 strings” octave shape to copy a note all over the neck.
  • Know why the B string shifts the shape by one fret — and adjust automatically.
  • See the fretboard as one chromatic scale repeated, so one known string derives the rest.

1. The layout: strings, frets, and one octave to the 12th

In standard tuning the six open strings, from the thickest (lowest in pitch) to the thinnest (highest), are E A D G B E. We number them the way guitarists do: the high E is string 1 and the low E is string 6. (The numbering runs opposite to the pitch, which is its own small hurdle — string 1 is the thinnest, highest-sounding string.)

String 1 2 3 4 5 6
Open note E (high) B G D A E (low)

Moving along a string toward the body, each fret raises the pitch by one half step — the smallest step in Western music, the same half step you met in Theory 1. Two frets make a whole step. So a single string is nothing more than the chromatic scale walking upward, one fret at a time.

Because an octave is 12 half steps (Theory 5), the 12th fret is the octave of the open string — the same letter name, one register higher — and from fret 12 onward the entire pattern of notes repeats. The neck therefore holds only one octave of distinct positions per string; learn frets 0–12 and you have learned the whole string. That doubled dot at the 12th fret on most guitars is marking exactly this octave.

Why one fret is exactly one half step

Guitar frets are placed so each one shortens the vibrating string by the ratio that produces a half step (the twelfth root of two in pitch). Twelve of those equal-tempered steps multiply back to a doubling of frequency — a perfect octave — which is precisely why the 12th fret lands on the octave and the markings reset there. You don't need the maths to play; you only need the consequence: fret = half step, 12 frets = octave, then repeat.

2. The anchor strings: naturals on low E and A

You don't have to memorise all six strings cold. Two of them carry most of the navigational load: the low-E (6th) and A (5th) strings. These are the strings whose notes name your barre-chord roots — the E-shape barre takes its root from the low-E string, the A-shape barre from the A string — so knowing their natural notes lets you put a chord anywhere. Master these two and the rest of the neck follows from the octave shapes in section 3.

Start with the seven natural notes climbing the low-E string. Notice the rhythm of the gaps: most natural notes are a whole step (two frets) apart, but B up to C and E up to F are only a half step (one fret) apart — the two natural half steps you learned in Theory 1, now visible as the two single-fret jumps on the string.

Natural notes climbing the low-E string (the rightmost string here; string 6, the thickest). Open = E, then F(1), G(3), A(5), B(7), C(8), D(10), and E again at the 12th fret. The two one-fret hops — B→C (7→8) and E→F (open→1) — are the natural half steps; every other gap is two frets.

The A string works identically — same letter sequence, same two half-step gaps (B-C and E-F), just starting from A:

A string (5th) fret 0235781012
Natural note ABCDEFGA

Two useful checkpoints fall straight out of this: A-string fret 5 is D (the same pitch as the open D string, which is how you tune by ear), and A-string fret 7 is E (an octave above the open low E). The sharps and flats simply live in the wider gaps — fret 1 of the A string is A♯/B♭, and so on — but you can always locate them from the naturals you now know.

3. Octave shapes: copy a note across the neck

Here is the trick that turns two known strings into the whole fretboard. Take any note on the low-E, A, D, or G string and you find its octave by going up 2 frets and over 2 strings (toward the thinner strings). The shape is a small, fixed L — same fingering everywhere on those four strings.

Example: A is on the low-E string at fret 5. Go +2 frets, +2 strings and you land on the D string at fret 7 — the A an octave higher. Same note name, instantly, with no counting.

The “+2 and +2” octave: root A on the low-E string (string 6, fret 5) and its octave A on the D string (string 4, fret 7). The same shape moves anywhere on the E/A/D/G strings — slide it to fret 3 and it spells G→G, slide it to fret 8 and it spells C→C.

Why the shape is “+2 and +2”

Each adjacent string in standard tuning is a perfect fourth higher than the one below it (5 half steps — Theory 5). Skip a string and you stack two fourths: 5 + 5 = 10 half steps. An octave is 12, so you are 2 half steps short — and you make up exactly those 2 half steps by moving 2 frets along the string. That is the whole reason the shape is two-and-two: two fourths plus two frets equals one octave.

4. Why the B string breaks the pattern

Standard tuning has one irregularity, and it is the single most important thing to internalise about the neck. Every adjacent pair of strings is a perfect fourth apart — except the G string up to the B string, which is a major third (only 4 half steps instead of 5). It is the lone major-third gap in an otherwise all-fourths tuning.

The consequence: any shape that crosses from the G string onto the B or high-E string shifts up one extra fret to keep its sound. The +2/+2 octave becomes a +3 frets, +2 strings shape whenever the higher note lands on the B or top-E string.

The B-string octave shape: root G on the D string (string 4, fret 5) and its octave G on the B string (string 2, fret 8). Because the octave crosses onto the B string, it sits three frets up, not two — the extra fret pays for the major-third gap below it.

Why one extra fret, exactly

Walk the same maths as before but cross the G-B boundary. From the D string, the next string up (G) is a fourth (5 half steps), and G up to B is only a major third (4 half steps): 5 + 4 = 9 half steps. To reach the 12 half steps of an octave you must add 3 frets, not 2. The B string's major-third tuning eats one half step of horizontal distance, so every shape that crosses it must hand one fret back. Learn this single offset and the rest of the neck's geometry — barre chords, scale boxes, arpeggios — stops surprising you.

Quick rule

Counting strings from low to high — E A D G | B E — put the imaginary line between G and B. Any shape staying on one side keeps its frets. Any shape stepping across that line, toward the thin strings, slides up one fret; stepping back across it, toward the thick strings, slides down one fret.

5. The whole neck is one chromatic scale on a grid

Step back and the picture is simple. A string is the chromatic scale laid end to end. The next string up is the same chromatic scale, started a perfect fourth higher (a major third higher across the B-string gap). Stack six of these offset copies and you have the entire fretboard — a grid where the same pitch shows up in several places, and where knowing one string lets you derive every other by counting the tuning offset.

This is the engine behind everything that comes next on guitar: barre chords are octave shapes wearing a chord, the CAGED system is five views of this same grid, and reading standard notation on the neck (next lesson) is just mapping the staff's pitches onto these coordinates. You are not memorising the fretboard so much as learning its one repeating rule and the one exception to it.
