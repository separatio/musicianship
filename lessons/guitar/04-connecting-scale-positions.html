Connecting scale positions

Almost every guitarist learns one box of the minor pentatonic — the shape around the 5th fret — gets stuck there, and spends years feeling boxed in. This lesson dissolves the box. The single most important idea on the whole instrument is this: the neck is one scale repeated, not five separate things. The same five notes — A, C, D, E, G — recur over and over up the strings, and the “five boxes” are simply five camera angles on the same landscape. Once you see how they overlap and hand off to each other, you can play A minor pentatonic anywhere and move freely across the entire fretboard.

  • Play all five A-minor-pentatonic boxes and know where each sits.
  • See that adjacent boxes share a whole column of notes — the pivot.
  • Connect box 1 to box 2 with a slide or a position shift, smoothly.
  • Recognise the CAGED chord shape living inside each box.
  • Add the two “extra” notes to get natural minor, Dorian, or the blues scale.

1. One scale, five windows

A minor pentatonic is just five notes: A · C · D · E · G — the A natural-minor scale with its 2nd (B) and 6th (F) removed, which is exactly why it sounds so consonant and never lands a wrong note over an A-minor groove (Theory 8 covers the why). Those five pitch-classes repeat in every octave, so they appear all over the neck. Read out as a single octave they ascend A–C–D–E–G–A:

A “box” (or position) is one playable, four-fret-wide window onto these notes — a shape your hand covers without shifting. Because there are five notes, there are five boxes, and they tile the neck end to end, each one starting where the previous one is still in progress. The crucial fact, which we will see in section 3, is that consecutive boxes overlap: the last notes of box 1 are literally the first notes of box 2.

Why exactly five boxes?

Each box is defined by which scale note you start on with your first finger on the low string. There are five notes, so there are five possible starting notes, hence five boxes. After the fifth you are back to the first, an octave higher — the pattern loops. This is the same five-fold loop you met in CAGED (Guitar 3): five shapes that cycle forever up the neck. That is not a coincidence, and section 4 shows why.

2. Box 1 and Box 2

Start with the two boxes every lead player lives in. Read the diagrams as a guitar neck held up in front of you: the leftmost vertical line is the low E string (6th) and the rightmost is the high E (1st); fret numbers run down the left edge. Every dot is a real A-minor-pentatonic note, and each is labelled with its letter name so you can see the five notes recurring.

Box 1 — around frets 5–8 (the “home” box)

The root A sits under your first finger on the low E string, 5th fret. This is the shape almost everyone learns first.

A minor pentatonic, Box 1 (Position 1). Low E on the left, high E on the right. Roots (A) are the anchor notes.

Two notes per string. The roots (A) fall on the low E 5th fret, D string 7th fret, and high E 5th fret — learn those three anchors and the box hangs off them.

Box 2 — around frets 7–10

Slide your hand up so the index finger sits at the 7th–8th fret. Same five notes, new window. Notice the root A now appears on the A string (7th fret) and the D string (7th fret).

A minor pentatonic, Box 2 (Position 2), one box higher up the neck.

Box 2 begins on C (the ♭3) on the low E string, 8th fret — the note that was the top of box 1 on that string. That shared note is the whole secret, and the next section makes it visible.

3. The overlap: where box 1 hands off to box 2

Lay the two boxes on the same stretch of neck and a column lights up in the middle: the highest notes of box 1 are the exact same notes as the lowest notes of box 2. They share a whole vertical column — here the notes at frets 7 and 8 (red below). That shared column is the pivot: the bridge you walk across to move from one box to the next.

Boxes 1 and 2 on one map (frets 5–10). Red dots are the shared pivot column where box 1 becomes box 2.

The red column — low E 8, A 7, D 7, G 7, B 8, high E 8 — belongs to both boxes at once. Play up to a red note inside box 1, and you are already standing in box 2. There is no gap to jump; the boxes interlock.

This is the mental shift the whole track is built around. You are not memorising five unrelated diagrams. You are learning one continuous map in which the boxes are just convenient hand positions, glued together at their shared edges. Two ways to cross the pivot in real playing:

  • Position shift: when you reach a pivot note, let your whole hand jump up so your index finger covers box 2. The pivot note is the note you re-finger under your new index finger — the hand “teleports” on a note you were already on.
  • Slide: play a pivot note with a finger, then slide that same finger up the string into the next box's note on the same string. The slide is audible and bluesy, and it physically carries your hand into the new position. Sliding on the B string from G (8th fret) up to A (10th fret) is a classic box-1-to-box-2 move.
Practice the seam, not the shapes

Beginners drill each box in isolation and then can't join them. Flip it: drill the seam. Pick one pair of adjacent strings, play up box 1 to the pivot note, cross into box 2, come back. Loop just that crossing until the shift is invisible. Boxes you already know; it is the handoff that needs the reps.

4. The CAGED connection

Why exactly five boxes, looping forever? Because each pentatonic box is wrapped around one of the five CAGED chord shapes you met in Guitar 3. The chord is the skeleton; the pentatonic box is the flesh around it. Their roots are the same notes, and they cycle in the same five-fold loop up the neck — that is why both systems have five shapes and why they line up.

Box 1 of A minor pentatonic wraps the E-shape barre chord (an A-minor barre at the 5th fret — root on the low E, exactly where box 1's root is). Box 2 wraps the D-shape, the next CAGED shape toward the bridge. Move up through the boxes and you are walking the CAGED word backwards along the neck: E → D → C → A → G, each chord shape carrying its pentatonic box with it.

Why this is worth the effort

When the box and the chord are the same object in your mind, your scale lines automatically spell the chord: the strong notes of any lick fall on chord tones because the chord is sitting right there inside the box. You stop running shapes and start outlining harmony — which is the difference between noodling and playing music. Anchor each pentatonic box to its barre chord and you also instantly know where you are on the neck.

5. From five notes to seven: pentatonic + diatonic

The minor pentatonic is A natural minor with two notes missing. Put them back and the same boxes become full seven-note scales — no new shapes to learn, just two extra dots per octave. Which two notes you add decides the flavour:

Scale Notes (from A) Added to the pentatonic Colour
A minor pentatonic A C D E G — (the five-note base) safe, neutral
A natural minor (Aeolian) A B C D E F G + B (2nd) and F (♭6) dark, sad
A Dorian A B C D E F♯ G + B (2nd) and F♯ (natural 6th) minor but brighter, jazzy

The only difference between natural minor and Dorian is that one note — the 6th: F versus F♯. That single half step is the whole mood swing. Hearing it is a Theory 8 skill; here you just learn where the extra dots sit inside the box you already own.

The blue note

There is one more note worth its own name: the blue note, the ♭5. In A that is E♭ (= D♯), and it lives between the 4th (D) and the 5th (E). Slip it in as a passing tone and the pentatonic becomes the A blues scale (A–C–D–E♭–E–G). The blue note is tension you pass through, not a note you sit on — it pulls up into E or down into D. (Theory 8 explains why this borrowed chromatic note sounds so right.)

6. How to practise connecting two boxes

A concrete routine for welding box 1 to box 2. Slow, looped, daily — ten focused minutes beats an hour of aimless noodling.

  1. Anchor the roots. Play only the A roots of box 1 (low E 5, D 7, high E 5), then box 2's roots (A 7, D 7). Knowing where “home” is in each box keeps you from getting lost mid-shift.
  2. Ascend box 1, cross at the pivot, descend box 2. Go up box 1 string by string; when you hit a red pivot note, continue straight into box 2 and come back down. The boxes should feel like one long shape.
  3. Drill the slide. On the B string, play G (8th fret) and slide up to A (10th fret) — box 1 to box 2 in one gesture. Repeat until the slide is smooth and in time.
  4. Improvise across the seam. Over an A-minor backing loop, solo using both boxes, forcing yourself to cross the pivot at least once per phrase. The goal: stop noticing the boundary.
  5. Then add the colour notes. Once the seam is invisible, drop in the natural-minor F, the Dorian F♯, or the blue-note E♭ and hear how each recolours the same lines.
The whole point, restated

When this clicks, you will stop seeing “box 1” and “box 2” as places and start seeing the neck as one field of A-minor notes you can enter and leave anywhere. Connect boxes 2–3, 3–4, 4–5 the same way and the entire fretboard opens up — five windows onto one scale.
