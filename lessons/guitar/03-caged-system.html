The CAGED system & movable shapes

You already know a handful of open chords — C, A, G, E, D. Here is the secret the guitar has been keeping from you: those five shapes are not five random grips, they are the only five major-chord templates the instrument has, and every one of them can be picked up and slid up the neck to spell any major chord, anywhere. Learn how the five interlock and the whole fretboard stops being a maze and becomes a map.

  • Name the five CAGED shapes and see each as a voicing of root–3–5.
  • Understand why a barre chord works — the barring finger as a movable nut.
  • Slide an open shape up the neck and know exactly which chord you land on.
  • See the five shapes interlock to cover the whole neck for one chord, spelling C–A–G–E–D as you ascend.
  • Find any major chord anywhere: locate the root, grab the nearest shape.

1. Five shapes, and only five

On a six-string guitar in standard tuning there are exactly five ways to grab a major chord that uses open strings: the familiar C, A, G, E, and D open chords. Spell them in order and you get the word that names the system: CAGED. Every other major-chord grip on the neck is one of these five shapes moved to a new fret — there is no sixth template hiding anywhere.

Why exactly these five and no more? Because they are the major chords whose root happens to fall on an open string or within reach of the nut. The remaining open-chord letters (B and F) need a barre even in first position, so they are not open shapes — they are already movable versions of A and E. Five it is.

Why this matters before anything else

The guitar is laid out so the same chord can be fingered in many physical places. Without an organising idea you would have to memorise dozens of unrelated grips. CAGED collapses all of them into five shapes plus the rule “slide it up.” You learn five things and get the whole neck.

The same three notes on the keyboard: root C, major third E, fifth G. Five guitar shapes, one chord — this is the through-line of the whole lesson.

2. Why a barre chord works: the movable nut

An open chord borrows its lowest notes from the open strings — the nut is, in effect, a zero-th fret that stops every string at its open pitch. A barre chord just replaces the nut with your index finger. Lay that finger flat across a fret and it becomes a movable nut: every string that used to ring open now rings stopped at that fret instead, and the rest of your fingers keep the original shape on top.

Because every string rises by the same number of frets, the whole chord is transposed up by that many half steps — exactly what a capo does, except your finger is the capo. Move the open E shape up one fret and every note climbs a semitone: E becomes F. That is the famous F-major barre chord.

Why “up one fret” means “up a semitone”

One fret is one half step on every string (you proved this in Guitar 1). Barring fret 1 pushes all six open pitches — E A D G B E — up one half step to F A♯ D♯ G♯ C F, and the fingered E-shape on top moves up with them. The chord keeps its internal intervals (it is still major), only its pitch level changes. Transposition by barre is the single most useful trick on the instrument.

Open E (the template)

Open E major: low E (string 6) open, B→E on string 5/4 (the 2nd-fret dots), and G♯ fretted on string 3 (1st fret)... in first position the shape sounds E B E G♯ B E. The open strings are the nut doing the work.

E-shape barre at fret 1 = F

Index finger barres all six strings at fret 1 (the movable nut); the E-shape sits on top. Sounding F C F A C F — an F-major chord. Root on strings 6, 4 and 1.

3. One chord, five shapes, the whole neck

Here is the second big idea. Take a single chord — say C major — and you can voice it in five places up the neck, one for each CAGED shape, and they interlock: where one shape ends, the next begins, so together they tile the entire fretboard with C major. Read the shapes from the nut upward and they appear in the order C → A → G → E → D — the word CAGED, ascending. (Past the D shape it wraps back to C an octave higher.)

For C major the five positions land like this. Each diagram is a different voicing of the same C–E–G triad; verify every dot against the open strings E A D G B E if you like — the arithmetic always returns C, E, or G.

C shape — open position

The plain open C chord: root C on string 5/fret 3, third E on string 4/fret 2, fifth G open on string 3, root C on string 2/fret 1, third E open on string 1. Low E is usually left out.

A shape — barre at fret 3

The open-A grip made movable and slid up three frets (A → C). Root C now sits on string 5/fret 3; the barre supplies the fifth on string 1. Voicing C–G–C–E–G.

G shape — around fret 5–8

The open-G grip moved up five frets (G → C). It is the widest, most awkward of the five and is often played as a partial, but the full shape puts a low root C on string 6/fret 8 and a high C on string 1/fret 8.

E shape — barre at fret 8

The same E-shape barre from section 2, but eight frets up (E → C). Roots on strings 6, 4 and 1; the most useful full-neck C voicing. Voicing C–G–C–E–G–C.

D shape — around fret 10–13

The open-D grip moved up ten frets to C an octave above the C-shape we started on. Top four strings only; root C on string 4/fret 10. Past here the cycle returns to the C shape at the 12th-fret octave.

How the shapes interlock

Look at the roots. The C-shape root on string 5 (fret 3) is the same note that anchors the A-shape directly above it; the A-shape's upper root becomes the G-shape's lower one, and so on. Each shape shares an edge with its neighbour — the top of one is the bottom of the next. That shared boundary is why they connect seamlessly instead of leaving gaps, and why the order never changes: C, then A, then G, then E, then D, climbing the neck.

4. Finding any chord anywhere

You do not need all five shapes to start. One reliable method:

  1. Pick a root note on a low string (string 6 or string 5) — you can find any note there from Guitar 1.
  2. Choose the shape whose root sits on that string. The E shape has its root on string 6; the A shape has its root on string 5. These two are the workhorse barre chords for exactly this reason.
  3. Place the shape so its root lands on your note. Want G major? Put the E-shape root (string 6) on G — fret 3 — and barre. Want D major? Put the A-shape root (string 5) on D — fret 5 — and barre.
Two shapes get you started

Master just the E-shape and A-shape barres and you can play every major chord in twelve keys without learning a single new grip — only sliding. The C, G and D shapes then fill in the gaps between them when you want a voicing in a different register. That is the whole practical payoff of CAGED.

And remember what is underneath all of it: a CAGED shape is not a mysterious pattern, it is a voicing of root–3–5 (Theory 10). Once you can see the root, the third, and the fifth inside each shape, you can alter them deliberately — flatten the third for a minor chord, drop a note for a power chord — instead of memorising yet another grip. Building chords from their formulas on the neck is the next step (Guitar 6).
