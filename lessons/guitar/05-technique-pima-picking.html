Technique: fingerstyle, picking & legato

Earlier lessons taught you where the notes live on the fretboard. This one is about your two hands — how the right hand sets a string moving (fingers or pick) and how the left hand connects notes smoothly (legato). The same fretboard sounds completely different depending on the touch you bring to it, and since you play both classical and electric, you need both vocabularies fluently.

  • Use the PIMA fingers and assign them to strings in an arpeggio.
  • Tell rest stroke from free stroke, and know when each one earns its keep.
  • Pick with strict alternate picking, and know what economy picking trades for it.
  • Connect notes with hammer-ons, pull-offs, and slides — the legato toolkit.
  • Adjust posture and touch between a classical and an electric guitar.

1. The right hand: fingerstyle (PIMA)

In fingerstyle playing each finger of the right hand has a name and a job. The names are Spanish, because the modern classical tradition was codified in Spain:

Letter Spanish Finger Usual job
ppulgarthumbbass strings (6, 5, 4)
iíndiceindextreble strings
mmediomiddletreble strings
aanularringtreble strings

The little finger (meñique) is essentially unused in standard classical technique. The basic division of labour is simple: the thumb takes the bass, the fingers take the treble. In the most common arpeggio assignment the thumb plays whichever of the three bass strings is the chord root, and i m a map one-to-one onto the three treble strings: i → string 3 (G), m → string 2 (B), a → string 1 (high E).

Why one finger per string?

Assigning i m a to strings 3–2–1 means no single finger has to jump between strings inside an arpeggio. Each finger lives over its own string, so it can plant (rest lightly on the string, ready) and then release in a steady relay. That removes the largest source of right-hand timing error — a finger travelling sideways looking for its next string — and is why the assignment is taught as a default, not a rule to break for fun.

The arpeggio

Rest stroke and free stroke

Every plucked note is produced one of two ways. Free stroke (tirando, “pulling”): the finger plucks and follows through into the palm without touching the next string — the light, clean motion you use for arpeggios and chords, because it leaves the neighbouring strings ringing. Rest stroke (apoyando, “supporting”): the finger plucks and comes to rest against the adjacent string, giving a rounder, punchier, louder note — used to make a single melodic line or a scale sing out above everything else.

2. The right hand: pick technique

Swap the fingers for a flatpick and the question becomes which direction the pick travels. Alternate picking is the foundation: strictly alternating down–up strokes in a continuous motion, regardless of which string you are on. Because the pick is always either falling from the last note or rising toward the next, no motion is wasted and the hand stays in a steady oscillation — which is exactly what lets it run fast and even.

Economy picking bends the strict rule to save motion: you alternate while staying on one string, but when you change strings you keep the pick moving in the same direction (a small sweep) instead of reversing it. It is more efficient across strings, at the cost of the metronomic evenness that strict alternate picking gives you — which is why most players learn alternate picking solidly first.

Pick-hand anchor

The pick angle and a relaxed wrist matter more than speed. If notes come out uneven, you are almost always rushing the upstroke or digging the downstroke deeper — fix that at slow tempo before adding BPM, because the metronome will faithfully amplify whatever imbalance you practise in.

3. The left hand: legato

Legato means smoothly connected — notes that flow without a fresh attack on each one. On guitar the left hand makes notes without the right hand re-plucking, using three moves:

  • Hammer-on: pluck a note, then strike a higher fret on the same string with a fretting finger, hard enough to sound the new note from the finger’s impact alone.
  • Pull-off: the reverse — a fretted finger plucks the string sideways as it lifts off, sounding a lower note already fretted (or the open string) beneath it.
  • Slide: pluck a note and, keeping the string pressed, glide the finger along to another fret — the smoothest connection of all, and the one that covers the largest distance.
Why “minimal motion, finger-per-fret”?

Legato is only as clean as your left-hand economy. Keep fingers close to the strings — a finger hovering a centimetre off the board has to travel and accelerate before it can hammer, which costs time and accuracy. The finger-per-fret habit (index, middle, ring, pinky each owning one of four consecutive frets) means the right finger is already over its target, so a hammer-on is a short, controlled drop rather than a lunge. Minimal motion = less to go wrong = faster and cleaner, not one or the other.

4. Classical vs electric: posture & touch

You play both, and they ask for different bodies. These are tendencies, not laws — but they explain why a piece feels wrong if you bring the other instrument’s habits to it.

Classical guitar Electric guitar
Guitar position On the left leg (often on a footstool); neck angled up, headstock near eye level (~45° or steeper). On the right leg or on a strap, slung lower; neck closer to horizontal.
Right hand Fingers and nails (PIMA); free and rest strokes. Usually a pick; alternate/economy picking and strumming.
Signature move Tone shaping through nail angle and stroke choice. Palm muting — the side of the picking hand rests on the strings near the bridge for a tight, chugging damp.
Why the steep classical angle?

Raising the neck brings the whole fretboard toward the left hand and squares the fingers to the strings, so they press straight down with the fingertip — essential for the wide stretches and clean stops of classical repertoire. The electric’s lower, flatter carry favours the picking hand and strap mobility instead. Neither is “correct”; each optimises for the hand that does the most work in that style.

5. How to practise this

Technique is built by slow, accurate repetition, not by straining for speed. A few rules that apply to every drill above:

  • Slow first. Play at a tempo where every note is clean and even. Speed is a by-product of accuracy, never a substitute for it.
  • Use a metronome. Lock the PIMA arpeggio and the alternate-picking exercise to a click; raise the tempo only after a passage is clean several times in a row. (We give this a whole session in the Practice track — Metronome work, linked below once published.)
  • Stay relaxed. Tension is the enemy of both speed and tone. If your forearm aches, you are gripping; stop, shake it out, slow down.
  • Short and often beats one long cramming session — the hands consolidate motor patterns between sessions, not within one.
