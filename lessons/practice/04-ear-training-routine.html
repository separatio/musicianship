An ear-training routine

You already know what the ear-training categories are — intervals, chord qualities, dictation, rhythm, sight-singing. This lesson is the opposite of theory: it is a schedule. A repeatable week of short drills, each tied to a free trainer, each with a way to measure whether you are getting better. The ear is a muscle that grows on frequency, not on heroic single sessions, so the whole design here is “a little, most days, forever.” This is also the final lesson of the course, so it closes by tying the four threads — theory, instruments, repertoire, and the practice system — back together.

  • Run a fixed weekly rotation that touches every ear skill.
  • Set the right difficulty on each free trainer and track accuracy.
  • Turn mistakes into the next session's target, not noise.
  • Connect ear to instrument: transcribe by ear, then sing what you play.
  • Leave the course with a sustainable loop you can run for years.

1. Why a fixed weekly rotation

Ear training rewards interleaving — rotating between related skills — far more than blocking one skill for an hour. Hearing a third today, a chord quality tomorrow, and a dictation the day after keeps each category just effortful enough to force real retrieval, and it spaces your repetitions so they stick. That is the same spacing-and-retrieval logic behind every good practice plan; here we apply it across the five ear skills at once.

Why short and daily beats long and rare

After a focused session your brain keeps consolidating the pattern in the background — sleep and time do part of the work for you. Ten honest minutes a day reaches you on six or seven separate consolidation cycles a week; one seventy-minute Sunday session reaches you on only one. The trainers below are deliberately tiny so the bar to starting is near zero: the hardest rep is opening the page, and a ten-minute target keeps that bar low.

One principle runs through all of it, and it is the single most important idea in modern ear training: listen in a key, not in a vacuum. Establish a tonic first (play a I–IV–V–I or just hum “do”), then hear every interval and chord relative to that tonic. This functional approach transfers to real music in a way that abstract, contextless interval drilling never quite does. Most trainers below have a functional mode — use it.

2. The weekly routine

Here is one full week. Each day is one short focus block plus a standing two-minute sight-singing warm-up (because singing trains the ear faster than passive listening — you can't sing what you can't hear). Swap the days to suit your life; keep the rotation.

Make it concrete

Put the seven trainer links on your phone's home screen or bookmark bar in this order. The routine then runs itself: open today's link, drill ten minutes, log your accuracy, done. Decision removed.

3. How to practise each trainer well

Every trainer below shares the same three-step loop: set difficulty → drill → review mistakes. The skill is in tuning the difficulty so you score roughly 75–85% — high enough to feel fluent, low enough that real learning is happening. Too easy is a waste; below about 60% you are guessing, not hearing.

Set difficulty

  • Start narrow. Intervals: begin with just the perfect 5th, octave, and major/minor 3rd; add one new interval only when the current set sits above ~85%. Chords: major vs minor triads first, then add diminished/augmented, then sevenths.
  • Turn on the functional mode where it exists (play a cadence to fix the key first). It is harder at first and far more useful.
  • Fix the key, then unfix it. Drill in C until steady, then let the trainer randomise the key so you are hearing relationships, not absolute pitches.

Track accuracy

Most of these trainers show a live percentage and a per-item breakdown. Write the number down — a one-line log (date, drill, score) is enough. You are looking for two things: the trend over weeks, and which specific item drags the average down. The number is not for grading you; it is for choosing tomorrow's target.

Review mistakes

Why the mistake log is the whole game

Recognition errors are almost never random. You will find you reliably confuse one specific pair — major 3rd vs perfect 4th, or minor 7th vs major 6th, or dominant 7th vs minor 7th chord. Those confusions are the actual lesson. When the trainer marks you wrong, stop and replay both the answer you gave and the correct one back to back until the difference is obvious, then sing each. Next session, build a tiny custom set of only that confused pair and drill it for two minutes. Targeted reps on your three worst pairs will move your overall score more than any amount of broad practice.

4. Connecting the ear to the instrument

Trainers build recognition; transcription turns recognition into music. Once a week, pick a very short fragment — two bars of a riff, a vocal hook, a bass line — and work it out by ear, without the sheet music. This is where every ear skill you have been drilling finally pays rent.

  1. Find the tonic. Hum along until you find “home” (do). Everything else is measured from there.
  2. Sing the fragment before you touch the instrument. If you can sing it, you have heard it.
  3. Find the first note on your instrument, then move by the interval your ear tells you — up a step, down a third — note by note.
  4. Check against the recording and fix what's wrong.
  5. Sing what you play, and play what you sing. Close the loop in both directions — voice to hand, hand to voice — until they agree.
Keep the fragments tiny

Two bars is plenty. A short fragment you finish builds the habit; a whole song you abandon halfway builds nothing. Transcribe many small things, not one big thing.

5. The daily sight-singing warm-up

Two minutes, every day, with movable-do solfège (the syllables and technique are taught in Lesson 9 — we just put them on a clock here). Sound a C, sing the major scale up and down on do re mi fa sol la ti do, then sing the little phrase below from the notation before you play it, and only then check yourself on your instrument. Singing first is what wires hearing to reading.

The home triad you are singing against: do (C4), mi (E4), sol (G4). Anchor your ear to these three and most of a major key falls into place around them.

6. Closing the course — keep cycling

This is the last lesson, so step back and look at the whole shape of what you have built. The course gave you four things that are only powerful together:

  • Theory — the grammar: intervals, scales, chords, harmony, form. It explains why music does what it does.
  • The instruments — guitar and piano: where theory becomes sound under your hands.
  • The repertoire approach — how to take a real piece apart and put it back together as something you can play.
  • The practice system — routines, the metronome, sight-reading, and now the ear: the machinery that turns study into skill over time.
Why these four feed each other

Theory tells your ear what to listen for; the ear lets you hear the theory in real music; the instrument lets you produce it; the repertoire gives it somewhere to go; and the practice system keeps the whole loop turning. Train your ear and a chord symbol stops being a label and becomes a sound you expect. Learn a piece and the harmony lessons stop being abstract. None of the four is finished — they deepen each other for as long as you keep cycling through them. So that is the parting instruction: keep the loop running — theory → instrument → ear → repertoire → theory — a little, most days, and the music keeps opening up.

You have the map and you have the routine. There is nothing left to teach — only to do.
