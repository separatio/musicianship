Building practice routines

Talent is mostly a story we tell about people who practised well. The difference between an hour that moves you forward and an hour that just passes is almost entirely how you spend it. This lesson is the method behind the whole suite: how to turn study into skill with focused, structured, well-aimed practice — and a schedule you will actually keep. Everything else in the Practice System track (the metronome, sight-reading, ear-training) plugs into the routine you build here.

  • Practise deliberately — slow, focused, aimed at your weakest spot, with feedback.
  • Lay out a session in blocks (warm-up → technique → new material → repertoire → theory/ear) and know why each is there.
  • Use the practice science: spacing, interleaving, slow-before-fast, short frequent sessions.
  • Set goals that are testable and keep a practice log that proves progress.
  • Build a personal weekly routine from a worked sample.

Start here — no prerequisites

This is the front door to the Practice System. You need no theory or technique first; you need a routine to hang them on. Once you have a routine, this is where the technique work goes to live:

1. Deliberate practice: the only kind that compounds

Most people play their instrument and call it practice. Playing is running through what you can already do. Deliberate practice is the opposite: a focused, structured activity aimed squarely at something you can't do yet, repeated at the edge of your ability, with immediate feedback telling you whether each attempt got closer. The research behind this — K. Anders Ericsson's studies of expert performers — found that how good you get depends far more on how you practise than on the raw number of hours.

The four ingredients, every time you sit down:

  • A clear, narrow goal — not “practise the piece” but “play bars 9–12 cleanly at 70 bpm.”
  • Full focus — phone away, one thing at a time; deliberate practice is effortful and you can't fake it while distracted.
  • The edge of your ability — work the spot that fails, not the parts that already shine.
  • Immediate feedback — a metronome, a recording, a teacher, or just your own honest ear telling you what went wrong, why, and how to fix it.
Why “practice makes permanent,” not perfect

Every repetition strengthens whatever you actually do — including the mistakes. Mindless run-throughs at full speed bake errors and tension into the very neural pathways you're trying to build, so they become harder to remove later. The goal of a repetition is not to get through the passage; it is to groove the correct motion so deeply that the wrong one never surfaces. That is why the rest of this lesson is mostly about slowing down and aiming carefully — speed and fluency are the reward for accuracy, never the route to it.

The weak-spot habit

When something breaks down, don't restart from the top — that just rehearses the easy beginning and the failure. Isolate the smallest unit that's failing (often two beats), loop that slowly until it's solid five times in a row, then stitch it back into the bar around it. Practise the hard 10%, not the comfortable 90%.

2. A session that works: five blocks

A good session has a shape. Each block has a job, and the order is deliberate: physical readiness first, then your hardest learning while you're fresh, then consolidation, then the lighter ear/theory work at the end. Treat the table as a frame, not a cage — scale the minutes to the time you have (the percentages are what matter).

Block Share What you do Why it's here / in this order
1. Warm-up ~10% Slow scales, long tones, easy stretches, gentle technical figures. Wakes up the hands and ears and lowers injury risk before any forcing. Never start cold on hard material.
2. Technique ~20% Scales, arpeggios, picking/finger patterns, isolated mechanics. Builds the raw motor vocabulary the music will draw on. Done early, while you're fresh and patient enough to be exact.
3. New / hard material (slow) ~35% The newest, hardest passages — small chunks, very slow, looped, weak-spot first. Your peak-focus block. New learning needs the most attention and the slowest tempo, so it gets the prime real estate.
4. Repertoire ~25% Play pieces you mostly know; refine musicality, run sections, perform-rehearse. Consolidates earlier learning and keeps motivation high — you end the hard work by making real music.
5. Theory / ear ~10% A few minutes of ear-training or theory tied to what you're playing. Lower-intensity, so it survives at the end when hands tire. Connects the dots between the notes and the why.

Sources put the blocks in this rough order for the same reason: warm up to protect the body, spend peak attention on the hardest new material, then consolidate with repertoire and finish with lighter ear/theory work. The exact split is yours to tune.

3. The practice science you can actually use

Four findings from learning research change how a routine should be built. None of them are intuitive — most feel worse in the moment, which is exactly why most people skip them.

Spaced (distributed) practice beats cramming

Spreading the same total practice time across several short sessions produces far better long-term retention than one long massed session. The “spacing effect” is one of the most robust results in all of learning science: students who space their study forget dramatically less than those who cram. Five 20-minute sessions across the week beat one 100-minute marathon — every time.

Why spacing works (and why it feels worse)

Memories consolidate during the gaps — especially during sleep. When you return after a break and have to partly reconstruct yesterday's progress, that little effort of re-retrieval is what burns it in for good. Cramming feels more productive because performance is smooth while you cram; but smooth-now and remembered-next-week are different things, and spacing trades a little fluency today for a lot of retention later.

Interleaving beats blocking

Blocked practice drills one thing to exhaustion before moving on (AAA BBB CCC). Interleaved practice mixes them up (ABC ACB BCA). Blocking feels better and looks better during the session, but studies of musicians find interleaved (“random”) practice produces clearly superior retention a day or more later. Rotate among a few passages or skills instead of grinding one into the ground.

The contextual-interference trick

The very thing that makes interleaving feel awkward — having to reload a different passage each time instead of riding momentum — is the mechanism that makes it stick. Forcing your brain to retrieve the skill afresh each repetition is harder work, and harder work that you can still complete is what builds durable learning. If a practice block feels too smooth, mix it up.

Slow practice builds accuracy before speed

Speed is a by-product of accuracy, never a substitute for it. Practising slowly lets you place every note and motion exactly right, so the correct pattern is the one that gets grooved. Then raise the tempo only as fast as you can stay clean — the metronome lesson next door is the engine for this. If errors creep in, you've gone too fast; drop back down. Slow is smooth, and smooth becomes fast.

Short, frequent sessions beat long, rare ones

Focus and working memory have a ceiling — attention and retention start to drop after roughly 20–30 minutes of concentrated effort. Several short, fully-focused sessions outperform one long session of equal total length, both because each one stays inside your high-focus window and because the gaps between them give you free spacing. Twenty honest minutes a day beats three lazy hours on a Sunday.

4. Goals you can test, and a log that proves it

“Get better at guitar” is a wish, not a goal — there's no way to tell if you've done it. Borrow the SMART frame from goal-setting: make each goal Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. The magic words are measurable and time-bound: a goal you can't check off can't tell you whether your practice is working.

Vague wishSMART goal
“Learn the C major scale.”“Play C major two octaves, even and clean, at 100 bpm by Friday.”
“Get the chord changes down.”“Switch G↔C cleanly in time at 70 bpm, 10 times in a row, this week.”
“Practise more.”“Five 25-minute sessions this week, logged.”

Pair goals with a practice log — even one line per session. It does three things: it makes spacing visible (you can see the gaps), it turns vague effort into evidence of progress (great for motivation on the days it feels flat), and it tells you where the time actually goes. A log can be a notebook, a notes app, or a simple table like this:

DateMinFocus / goalTempoWin & next problem
Mon25Piece A, bars 9–12 (slow)60→70Clean at 70; bar 11 shift still buzzes.
Tue20Scales + bar 11 isolated—Shift fixed slow; speed it up tomorrow.
……………
End each session by writing the next one

The single most useful log entry is the next problem: the thing that was still shaky when you stopped. Tomorrow you open the log and you already know exactly where to begin — no warm-up spent deciding what to do. The session plans itself.

5. Slow practice, made concrete

Here's what “slow then fast” looks like on a single, trivial line. Don't play it once and move on — play it at a tempo where every note is clean, loop it, and only nudge the tempo up when it's effortless. The notes don't matter; the discipline does. (The metronome lesson turns this into a system.)

6. A sample weekly routine

Here is one full week built from every principle above: short daily sessions (spacing), the five blocks rotated so skills interleave across days rather than blocking on one, a lighter day, and one genuine rest day for consolidation. Treat it as a starting template — copy it, then change the minutes and emphasis to fit your life and your goals.

Day Min Focus blocks
Mon25Warm-up · Technique (scales) · New material slow
Tue25Warm-up · New material slow · Repertoire · Ear (5 min)
Wed20Warm-up · Technique (arpeggios) · Repertoire
Thu25Warm-up · New material slow · Theory tied to the piece
Fri25Warm-up · Technique · Repertoire run-through · Ear (5 min)
Sat15Light: play for fun · revisit the week's weak spot once
Sun0Rest — sleep consolidates the week's gains.

Notice the design: ~20–25 minutes a day (inside the focus window), technique and new-material blocks interleaved across days rather than all crammed into one, and a rest day so the spacing effect and sleep do their work. Six short sessions > one long one.

Why a written routine beats willpower

A fixed time and a written plan remove the two biggest leaks in practice: deciding whether to practise and deciding what to practise. Both decisions drain the same willpower you need for the hard slow work — so spend them once, in advance. Attach practice to an existing daily anchor (after breakfast, before dinner), and the routine starts running on autopilot instead of motivation, which is fickle. The plan is the discipline; you just follow it.
