Figured-bass realization & an introduction to the fugue

Two Baroque crafts, one lesson. First, figured bass (thoroughbass, basso continuo): the shorthand where a single bass note plus a few numbers tells a keyboardist exactly which chord to build above it — the GPS of Baroque harmony. Second, the fugue: the high art of imitative counterpoint, where one tune chases itself through every voice. Figured bass teaches you to read harmony vertically from the bass up; the fugue teaches you to hear it horizontally, as independent lines. Both are the living application of everything in this track so far.

  • Read the common figures (5/3, 6, 6/4, 7, 6/5, 4/3, 4/2) off a bass line.
  • Know how an accidental raises, lowers, or stands in for a figure.
  • Realize a short figured bass into spelled chords with smooth voice-leading.
  • Name the parts of a fugue: subject, answer, countersubject, exposition, episode, stretto, pedal.
  • Tell a real answer from a tonal one, and a fugue from a two-voice invention.

1. What figured bass is

In the Baroque, much music was published as a bass line plus figures and nothing else. A continuo player — harpsichord, organ, or lute, usually with a cello or bassoon doubling the bass — read that line and improvised the full harmony on the spot. The figures are simply interval numbers above the written bass note: a “6” means “play a sixth above this bass,” a “3” means a third, and so on. The bass note itself is given; the figures fill in the chord.

Why shorthand at all?

Writing out every inner voice for a fast-moving continuo part would be enormous and would also over-specify it — the exact voicing, doublings, and ornaments were the player's to choose. Figures capture only what is fixed (the harmony) and leave free what is taste (the realization). It is the same instinct as a modern jazz chart's chord symbols: enough to lock the harmony, not so much that it strangles the player. The crucial difference is that figures count intervals above the bass, so they describe the chord's inversion automatically — which is exactly why figured-bass symbols are still used today to label inversions under Roman numerals.

3. Accidentals in the figures

Figures fix intervals above the bass, but they say nothing about chromatic colour until an accidental is added. Three conventions cover almost everything you will meet:

  • An accidental beside a figure raises or lowers that interval. ♯6 means the sixth above the bass is sharpened; ♭7 flattens the seventh.
  • An accidental standing alone (no number) always applies to the third above the bass. A bare ♯ under a bass note sharps that third — the single most common figure of all, because it is how a raised leading tone is signalled in a minor key.
  • A slash through a figure, or a + beside it, raises that interval by a half step. A slashed 6 and ♯6 mean the same thing.
The accidental does not change the number

♯6 is still a sixth above the bass — just a chromatically larger one. The figure names the letter distance (the generic interval); the accidental tunes its quality, exactly as number-then-quality worked back in Lesson 5. Read the number first, then apply the accidental.

5. Realizing it

To realize a figured bass is to write out the chords the figures specify — and to do it with the voice-leading you learned in Lessons 13 and 22: keep common tones, move each upper voice the smallest distance, and resolve tendency tones. The figures fix which notes; good voice-leading fixes where they go. Below, the same bass now carries a realized right hand on the upper staff.

The “6” chord on the keyboard

First inversion of C major: the bass plays E, and the figure “6” reaches up to C (a sixth above E) with G in between. The chord's root, C, is now on top, not in the bass.

The “7” chord on the keyboard

The figure “7” over a bass G stacks a third (B), a fifth (D), and a seventh (F): a dominant seventh, all in root position because the bass is the root.

6. From the bass up to the lines across — imitative counterpoint

Figured bass reads harmony from the bottom note up. The fugue asks the opposite question: what if every voice is an independent melody, and they all sing the same tune, entering one after another? That is imitative counterpoint, and the fugue is its grandest form. Everything it does rests on the species-counterpoint rules from Lesson 22 — it simply organises them around a single recurring theme.

The subject and the answer

A fugue is built on a subject: a short, memorable tune stated alone by the first voice, in the tonic. When the second voice enters, it restates the subject transposed to the dominant — a fifth higher (or, equivalently, a fourth lower). That dominant restatement is the answer.

Real answer vs. tonal answer

A real answer transposes the subject exactly — every interval preserved, as above. A tonal answer adjusts one or two intervals slightly so the answer stays anchored in the key instead of pulling too hard toward the dominant's own dominant. The classic trigger: when a subject leaps from tonic up to dominant (a fifth), a tonal answer often replies dominant up to tonic (a fourth) rather than the literal dominant-to-supertonic, keeping the music gravitationally tied to the home key. Why bother? An exact transposition of a tonic-emphasising subject would over-stress the dominant region and start modulating before the exposition has even finished introducing the voices. The tonal tweak buys harmonic stability at the cost of a literal copy — a tiny, deliberate “wrong” note in service of the key.

The countersubject

When a voice has finished stating the subject and the next voice begins the answer, the first voice does not fall silent — it continues with a complementary line called the countersubject. A true countersubject is reusable: it is written in invertible counterpoint so it sounds equally well above or below the subject (the principle from Lesson 22), and it returns, consistently, every time the subject does.

7. The architecture: exposition, episode, stretto, pedal

A fugue's large shape is assembled from a handful of named events:

SectionWhat happens
Exposition The opening. Each voice enters in turn — subject, then answer, then subject, then answer — until every voice has stated the theme once. After this, all voices are “in.”
Episode A transitional passage with no full statement of the subject. Built from fragments of the subject or countersubject, usually in sequence, it modulates between key areas and sets up the next entry.
Middle entries Statements of the subject in related keys, separated by episodes — the fugue's developmental core.
Stretto Overlapping entries: a voice begins the subject before the previous voice has finished it, so the statements pile up. Often saved for late in the piece, where it ratchets up tension.
Pedal point & close A sustained bass note — usually the dominant, then the tonic — under shifting harmony, gathering tension and discharging it into a final tonic statement.
How to follow a fugue by ear

Lock the subject into your memory from its first solo statement, then simply count the entries: each time the subject re-enters in a fresh voice, that voice has “arrived.” The stretches between entries — where you hear the tune broken into fragments and spun in sequence — are the episodes. When entries start tumbling over each other before they finish, you have reached the stretto, and the end is near.

8. The two-voice invention — the fugue's cousin

If a full fugue feels like a cathedral, the two-part invention is the chapel: the same imitative idea on a smaller, two-voice frame. One voice states a short idea, the other imitates it — but the imitation usually comes at the octave rather than the dominant fifth, which is why an invention sounds less harmonically charged than a fugue. By convention its recurring idea is called a motive rather than a subject, and its companion line a counter-motive rather than a countersubject. Inventions were written as study pieces — the most direct way to get imitative counterpoint into your hands before tackling a fugue.
