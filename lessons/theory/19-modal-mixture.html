Modal mixture (borrowed chords)

Secondary dominants (Lesson 17) coloured a key by pointing the dominant pull at a chord that wasn’t the tonic. This lesson colours a key a different way — not by adding a new leading tone, but by reaching across to the parallel mode and borrowing a chord from it. A piece in C major can, for a moment, sound a chord built from C minor — the minor subdominant, the bright-to-dark hinge you have heard a thousand times — without ever leaving C major. That is modal mixture, and it is one of the most expressive tools in tonal harmony.

  • Define modal mixture as borrowing from the parallel mode, and say why it is not modulation.
  • Spell the common borrowed chords in a major key: minor iv, ii°, ♭VI, ♭III, ♭VII, minor i.
  • Know which three scale degrees the borrowing lowers (♭3, ♭6, ♭7) and why that is the whole story.
  • Explain why a borrowed chord keeps the function of the chord it replaces.
  • Recognise and use the Picardy third — a minor piece ending on a major tonic.

1. Parallel modes, and what “borrowing” means

Two keys are parallel when they share the same tonic but differ in mode: C major and C minor are parallel keys (both centred on C). They are not the same as relative keys (C major and A minor), which share a key signature but have different tonics. Modal mixture borrows from the parallel mode, not the relative one — the tonic never moves.

Modal mixture (also called mode mixture, modal interchange, or simply borrowing) is the technique of using chords whose notes come from the parallel mode. In practice this almost always means a major key borrowing from its parallel minor — pulling in the darker colour of the minor mode for a chord or two, then carrying on in major.

Why it darkens without modulating

A borrowed chord changes the quality of a passing harmony, not the key. The tonic is never abandoned or replaced — the borrowing is brief enough that your ear keeps hearing C as home. That is the line between mixture and modulation (Lesson 18): modulation establishes a new tonal centre; mixture tints the current one. You get the minor mode’s shadow without paying the price of leaving the key.

2. Three lowered notes do all the work

The parallel minor differs from the parallel major in exactly three scale degrees: the 3rd, 6th, and 7th are each a half step lower. In C, that is E→E♭ (♭3), A→A♭ (♭6), and B→B♭ (♭7). Every borrowed chord is simply a diatonic chord that has had one or more of these three notes flatted. Learn the three notes and you can derive every mixture chord from scratch.

Of the three, ♭6 (A♭ in C) is the workhorse — it is the note inside the most common borrowed chords, the minor iv and the ♭VI. Watch for it.

Read the Roman numerals carefully: capital letters are major triads, lower-case are minor, and a flat sign before the numeral (♭VI, ♭III, ♭VII) means the root itself is a lowered scale degree, not just an inner note. The ♭II — a major triad on the lowered 2nd — is closely related but belongs to the Neapolitan chord, which gets its own treatment in Lesson 20.

3. The minor subdominant: mixture’s signature sound

The most-used borrowed chord by far is the minor iv. Take the ordinary IV chord (F–A–C in C major) and lower its third from A to A♭. That single half step turns a bright major subdominant into the wistful minor subdominant — the sound of countless cadences, film scores, and the “and I…” in a hundred ballads. Here it is framed by the tonic on both sides so you hear the colour appear and dissolve.

On the piano

F minor (iv): F and C are white keys, but the third sits on the black key A♭ — one half step below the A you would play for a normal F-major IV. That black key is the borrowing.

On the guitar

An F-minor voicing: F on the D string (4th string, 3rd fret), A♭ on the G string (3rd, 1st fret), C on the B string (2nd, 1st fret). Raise that A♭ one fret to A and you are back to a major IV.

The minor-iv plagal cadence

The progression IV–I is a plagal (“amen”) cadence. Swap in the borrowed iv–I and you get the famous “minor plagal” cadence — the same gesture with a darker, more poignant inner voice falling A♭→G. It is the single most common way you will meet modal mixture in real music.

4. Borrowed chords keep their function

This is the idea that makes mixture easy to use: changing a chord’s quality does not change its function. A borrowed chord slots into a progression in exactly the place its diatonic twin would have. The minor iv is still a predominant — it leads to the dominant just as the major IV did. The ♭III and ♭VII behave like the mediant and subtonic regions they replace. (The one chord that shifts its habits is ♭VI — see below.)

So you do not have to relearn voice-leading. If you know IV–V–I, then iv–V–I works the same way; the ear simply hears a moodier predominant. Function is carried by a chord’s position in the progression, not by whether its third happens to be major or minor.

5. ♭VI: the bright outlier

♭VI (A♭–C–E♭ in C) is a major triad built on the lowered 6th degree. It is striking because both its outer notes are borrowed (A♭ = ♭6, E♭ = ♭3), giving it a warm, faraway glow against a major key. Unlike most borrowed chords, ♭VI does not simply inherit the function of the chord it replaces (vi); in common practice it most often moves to the dominant, often as a predominant that sets up a strong V–I to follow. It can also resolve straight to I for a sudden, cinematic lift.

♭VI on the keyboard: A♭ and E♭ are both black keys (the two borrowed notes), with C in the middle. Seeing two black keys flanking the natural C is a good visual cue that you are deep in mixture territory.

7. The Picardy third

Mixture runs the other direction too. A Picardy third (French tierce de Picardie) is a passage in a minor key that ends on a major tonic chord — the final triad’s third is raised a half step. In C minor, instead of closing on C minor (C–E♭–G), the piece resolves to C major (C–E♮–G). It is a minor key borrowing its tonic chord from the parallel major: the same tonic, a brighter, more conclusive ending.

Why composers reached for it

In Renaissance and Baroque tuning, a major triad sounds more acoustically stable and resonant than a minor one — its overtones line up more cleanly — so a final major chord felt like a firmer place to rest. The raised third gives a piece a bright, consoling close after a whole movement of minor-mode tension. Bach uses it constantly to end minor-key chorales. It is the same parallel-borrowing principle as the minor iv, just applied to the very last chord and pointing toward the light instead of the dark.
