Four-part (SATB) writing & voice-leading

So far a chord has been a single object — a triad, a Roman numeral. Now we split it into four independent singing lines: Soprano, Alto, Tenor, Bass. Four-part (SATB) writing is the laboratory where every later harmony skill is forged, because it forces you to answer the real question: not just which chords, but how each voice gets from one chord to the next. The rules below are not arbitrary etiquette — each one protects something audible, and the lesson is mostly about the why.

  • Lay out the four voices on a grand staff with correct clefs and stems.
  • Double and space chord tones the way the common-practice tradition does — and know why.
  • Lead each voice smoothly: smallest motion, common tones, resolve tendency tones.
  • Recognise and avoid parallel fifths and octaves — and explain what they destroy.
  • Spot direct (hidden) fifths/octaves, voice crossing, and overlap.

1. The four voices and the grand staff

SATB labels four lines by register, highest to lowest: Soprano, Alto, Tenor, Bass. The names come from choral music, but in written theory they are simply four independent parts, each kept inside a comfortable range:

VoiceUsual rangeClef / staffStem
SopranoC4 (middle C) – A5Treble (upper)Up
AltoF3 – D5Treble (upper)Down
TenorC3 – A4Bass (lower)Up
BassF2 – E4Bass (lower)Down

Two voices share each staff: Soprano and Alto on the treble staff, Tenor and Bass on the bass staff. To keep the two voices on a staff legible, the upper of the pair (Soprano, Tenor) gets stems up and the lower (Alto, Bass) gets stems down. That single convention lets you read four lines off two staves without colour or labels.

Why fixed ranges and stems?

The ranges keep each part singable and keep the four lines from piling into the same register, where they would stop sounding like four voices. The stem rule is pure readability: with two notes on one staff, the only unambiguous way to say “this note belongs to the alto, that one to the soprano” is the stem direction — so it is treated as law, even when both voices happen to share a note.

2. Doubling: which note to repeat

A triad has three notes; SATB has four voices. One note must be doubled — sounded by two voices (usually an octave apart). The default rule in root-position triads is the simplest one to remember and the safest:

  • Double the root. The root is the most stable, least directional tone, so doubling it never creates a problem.
  • Never double the leading tone. Scale degree 7 (the leading tone, B in C major) is a strong tendency tone that pulls up to the tonic. Two leading tones would both want to rise to the same tonic — and two voices moving to the same note in the same direction is a parallel octave (section 5).
  • Never double the chordal seventh. In a seventh chord the seventh must fall by step to resolve; doubling it forces the same parallel-octave problem on the way down.
Why the leading tone is off-limits

A doubling rule is really a voice-leading rule in disguise. The leading tone has only one good destination — up a half step to the tonic. Give it to two voices and both are obligated to walk to the tonic together, producing forbidden parallel octaves (or forcing one of them to resolve wrongly, downward). Doubling the root sidesteps the trap because the root has no such obligation; it can stay, rise, or fall freely.

3. Spacing: how far apart the voices sit

Once you know which notes, you choose how far apart to stack them. The governing rule concerns the upper three voices:

  • Keep no more than an octave between adjacent upper voices — that is, between Soprano–Alto and between Alto–Tenor.
  • The Tenor–Bass gap is allowed to be wider (up to a twelfth is normal). The bass is the harmonic foundation and naturally sits apart from the choir above it.

This also names two textures. When the three upper voices fit inside one octave (Soprano to Tenor), the chord is in close structure (close position); when they spread past an octave, it is open structure (open position). Both are correct; composers alternate them for colour.

Why cap the upper voices but not the bass

A gap larger than an octave between two upper voices leaves an audible hole in the middle of the chord — the harmony sounds thin and the inner voice loses contact with its neighbour. The bass is different: ears expect a foundation set apart from the chord, so a wide tenor-to-bass gap reads as “solid”, not “hollow”. The rule protects the blend of the choir, which lives in the upper three voices.

4. Voice-leading: getting from chord to chord

Voice-leading is the heart of the craft: connect each chord to the next so that every line moves well. The classic principles, in priority order:

  • Keep common tones. If a note belongs to both chords, hold it in the same voice. (G is in both V and I in the final two chords of our score — the tenor simply stays on G3.)
  • Move each voice the smallest distance. Step or stay rather than leap; small motion is smooth and singable.
  • Resolve the leading tone up to the tonic (scale degree 7 → 8), especially when it is in the soprano — this is what makes a cadence sound finished.
  • Resolve a chordal seventh down by step. A seventh is a dissonance that leans downward; let it fall to the note below.
  • Prefer contrary and oblique motion. When voices move in opposite directions (contrary) or one holds while another moves (oblique), each line keeps its own identity. Save parallel and similar motion for the consonant thirds and sixths.

The four kinds of motion between any two voices are worth naming once, because the next section bans one specific case of parallel motion:

MotionWhat the two voices do
ContraryMove in opposite directions (one up, one down).
ObliqueOne voice holds; the other moves.
SimilarMove the same direction by different intervals.
ParallelMove the same direction by the same interval.

5. The great prohibition: parallel fifths & octaves

The single most famous rule of common-practice harmony: do not write consecutive (parallel) perfect fifths or perfect octaves between the same pair of voices. If two voices are a perfect fifth apart and both move so they are a perfect fifth apart again — in the same direction — that is a parallel fifth. The same with octaves (and unisons) is a parallel octave.

Why they are forbidden — the real reason

The whole point of four-part writing is four independent voices. The octave and the perfect fifth are the two most fused, most blended intervals there are. When two voices glide in parallel octaves they momentarily sound like one voice doubled — the texture loses a part. Parallel fifths fuse almost as strongly and, because the fifth defines the key, a chain of them blurs the harmony as well. The ban is not superstition: it is the mechanical consequence of wanting voices that stay distinct. (Parallel thirds and sixths are fine — those intervals don’t fuse, so the voices keep their independence.)

Below is a deliberately wrong V–I, then a corrected one. In the bad version the Tenor and Bass are a perfect fifth apart on chord 1 (Tenor D3 over Bass G2) and again a perfect fifth apart on chord 2 (Tenor G3 over Bass C3), both rising — consecutive perfect fifths between that pair.

The fix keeps the same chords but holds the common tone: the Tenor stays on G3 (G belongs to both V and I), so it no longer marches in fifths with the bass. The bass still leaps up a fourth (G2→C3) and the soprano carries the leading tone B4 up to C5, but the offending Tenor–Bass parallel is dissolved by giving the tenor oblique motion — it simply holds while the bass moves.

6. The near-misses: direct fifths, crossing, overlap

Three subtler faults round out the list. They matter most between the outer voices (Soprano and Bass), the pair the ear tracks most closely.

  • Direct (hidden) fifths and octaves. Even without a true parallel, arriving at a perfect fifth or octave by similar motion in the outer voices is weak — the ear hears the approaching parallel. The usual licence: it is acceptable if the soprano moves by step into the perfect interval.
  • Voice crossing. Don’t let a lower voice rise above the voice that should be over it (e.g. the alto going higher than the soprano). The listener loses track of which line is which.
  • Voice overlap. Don’t let a voice move past where its neighbour just was — e.g. the alto leaping above the note the soprano held a beat ago. Like crossing, it muddies the independence of the parts.
A working order of operations

When you realise a progression: (1) write the bass from the Roman numerals; (2) write the soprano as a singable melody; (3) fill alto and tenor, holding common tones and moving by the smallest steps; (4) then check every adjacent pair of chords for parallel fifths and octaves, and the outer voices for direct fifths/octaves. Doubling and spacing fall out almost automatically if you keep the inner voices lazy.
