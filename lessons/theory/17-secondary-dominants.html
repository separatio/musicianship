Tonicization & secondary dominants

Up to now every chord you have used belongs to one key — it was diatonic. This lesson is your first step off that path and into chromatic harmony. The idea is small and powerful: borrow the strongest pull in tonal music — the dominant→tonic resolution — and aim it at a chord that isn’t the tonic. For one moment that chord gets spotlit as a little local “home.” This is tonicization, and the chord that does the pointing is a secondary dominant.

  • Say what tonicization is, and how it differs from changing key.
  • Spell any secondary dominant V/x and V7/x from scratch.
  • Spell a secondary leading-tone chord vii°/x and vii°7/x.
  • Find the chromatic note that signals the tonicization — and know why it’s there.
  • Read and write the “slash” Roman-numeral labels (“five of five”).

1. The one fact that makes this work

A dominant chord — V, and even more so V7 — wants to resolve to its tonic, I. That pull is the engine of tonal music, and you met it as the authentic cadence in Lessons 12 and 16. The whole trick of this lesson is to notice that the pull is relative, not absolute: a dominant doesn’t know which key it’s in. It only knows it wants to drop a fifth (or rise a fourth) to the chord a perfect fifth below it.

So if you put the dominant of some other diatonic chord right before that chord, the ear briefly hears the target as “home.” You have tonicized it — made a non-tonic chord sound, for a beat or two, like a temporary tonic.

Why it convinces the ear

What sells a tonic is its leading tone — the note a half step below the root that strains upward into it (Lesson 6). C major already has B→C. To make G sound like home you need its leading tone, F♯→G — and F♯ is not in C major. Introducing that one chromatic note is the whole signal: the ear reads “new leading tone ⇒ new temporary tonic.” No leading tone of the target, no tonicization.

2. The secondary dominant V/x — “five of x”

Write it with a slash and read it aloud as “five of”: V/V is “five of five,” V/vi is “five of six.” The chord after the slash is the target you are tonicizing; the V (or V7) before it is that target’s own dominant.

To spell a secondary dominant, do exactly what you’d do for a real V:

  1. Find the root of the target chord (the x after the slash).
  2. Go up a perfect fifth from it — that root is your secondary dominant.
  3. Build a major triad on it (add a minor 7th on top for V7/x).

In C major, to tonicize V (which is G), go up a fifth from G to D and build D major: D–F♯–A. That is V/V, and the F♯ is the borrowed leading tone of G.

V/V on the keyboard: D–F♯–A. The black key F♯ (the borrowed leading tone) wants to slide up the half step to G, the root of the chord it tonicizes.

The seventh makes the pull sharper: V7/V

Adding a minor seventh turns the major triad into a dominant-7th — the strongest pull of all, because now you have both the new leading tone and a tritone that must resolve. In C major, V7/V is D7: D–F♯–A–C. That C is the seventh; it leans down to B as the chord resolves to G.

3. A target for every diatonic chord

Any major or minor chord in the key can be tonicized (the diminished vii° is the usual exception — a diminished triad makes a poor temporary tonic). The procedure never changes: root of target, up a perfect fifth, build a major triad / dominant 7th. The quality of the target is irrelevant to building the dominant — a dominant is always major, whether it resolves to a major or a minor chord.

Watch V/IV — it’s the sneaky one

The dominant of IV (F) is built up a fifth from F, which lands on C — and C major is already the tonic triad! As a plain triad V/IV looks identical to I, so the ear hears no tonicization. The fix is the seventh: V7/IV = C7 adds B♭, which is not in C major. That B♭ is the chord’s seventh and it falls to A as C7 resolves down a fifth to F. So for IV you almost always need the 7th to hear the tonicization at all.

On the piano

V/vi = E–G♯–B. G♯ (a black key) is the borrowed leading tone; it pulls up to A, root of the A-minor chord it tonicizes.

On the guitar

The same E major triad as a guitar shape: E on the D string (open-position, 4th string 2nd fret), G♯ on the G string (3rd string 1st fret), B on the open B string (2nd string). The G♯ is the colour note.

4. Secondary leading-tone chords — vii°/x

There is a second way to tonicize a chord, and it leans on the same idea from a different angle. Instead of borrowing the target’s dominant, borrow its leading-tone chord — the diminished triad (or seventh chord) built a half step below the target’s root. Write it vii°/x (“seven-diminished of x”).

To spell it, again pretend the target is momentarily tonic and build its vii°:

  1. Find the root of the target chord.
  2. Go down a half step — that note is the secondary leading tone.
  3. Build a diminished triad on it (vii°/x), or a diminished/half-diminished seventh (vii°7/x).

To tonicize V (G) in C major, drop a half step from G to F♯ and build a diminished triad: vii°/V = F♯° — F♯–A–C. Stack one more minor third for the fully-diminished seventh: vii°7/V = F♯–A–C–E♭.

Why V/x and vii°/x are cousins

Notice the overlap: V7/V is D–F♯–A–C and vii°/V is F♯–A–C. The leading-tone chord is just the top three notes of the dominant seventh — the dominant with its root left off. Both carry the same crucial F♯, the tritone, and the same urge to resolve to G. So they tonicize the same chord; the dominant-seventh version is heavier (it has a root in the bass to drop a fifth), the leading-tone version lighter and smoother in the voice-leading.

5. This is colour, not a new key

Here is the line that separates this lesson from the next one. A secondary dominant does not change the key. You do not write a new key signature; the chromatic note appears as an accidental and the music stays anchored in the original tonic. The tonicization lasts a chord or two and then the home key reasserts itself — the borrowed dominant is a momentary splash of colour, like a guest who is announced grandly and then leaves.

Tonicization vs. modulation

When the emphasis on the new chord persists — several phrases, a confirmed cadence, the ear genuinely resettling — it stops being a passing tonicization and becomes a modulation: a real change of key. The boundary is one of degree, not kind. A single V/V is tonicization; an entire section that lives in G is modulation. You will work that threshold in the next lesson.

One more habit worth forming now: a secondary dominant usually resolves to its target (V/V→V), but composers love to set it up and then dodge — resolving somewhere unexpected instead. That is a deceptive use of the applied chord, and it works precisely because you primed the ear to expect the target. The expectation is the whole point; honouring it or cheating it are both available colours.
