Notation I: the staff, clefs & the grand staff

Lesson 1 gave you the twelve pitches. This lesson teaches you to see them — to read a pitch off the page. Everything rests on one idea: a clef is an anchor. It pins one named note to one line of the staff, and every other note is read by stepping out from that anchor. Learn the anchor logic and the page stops being a wall of dots; learn only the mnemonics and you will forever be reciting rhymes. We do the anchor logic first.

  • Read the five-line staff: higher on the staff means higher in pitch.
  • Explain why the treble clef is the G clef and the bass clef is the F clef.
  • Read the grand staff and locate middle C (C4) between its two staves.
  • Use ledger lines to reach notes above and below the staff.
  • Name the notes of a C-major scale in both treble and bass clef.
  • Apply the rule that an accidental lasts to the end of the bar.
  • Recognise the 8va / 8vb octave signs.

1. The staff: a graph of pitch

Western notation is written on a staff (or stave): five horizontal lines with four spaces between them. A note is drawn on a line (the line passes through the notehead) or in a space (the notehead sits between two lines). The staff is really a graph with one axis: vertical position is pitch. A note placed higher on the staff sounds higher; a note placed lower sounds lower. Moving up one position — line to the next space, space to the next line — moves up one letter (a step), regardless of any accidental.

But a bare staff is ambiguous. The third line could be any pitch until something fixes it. That something is the clef.

2. The clef is an anchor

A clef is the symbol at the far left of every staff. Its job is to nail one named note to one specific line. Once that single note is fixed, every other line and space follows by stepping up or down the musical alphabet. The two clefs you will live in are named after the very note they anchor — and the shape of each clef literally points at its anchor line. This is the one insight that makes reading make sense.

Why “G clef” and “F clef”? Read the shape.

The treble clef is a stylised letter G. Its inner curl spirals around — and centres on — the second line from the bottom, and that curl declares that line to be G4, the G just above middle C. That is why it is called the G clef: the curl wraps the G line.

The bass clef is a stylised letter F. It is drawn with two dots, and those dots sit one above and one below the fourth line from the bottom — they straddle it, pointing it out — and that line is declared to be F3, the F below middle C. That is why it is the F clef: the two dots frame the F line.

So you never have to memorise where the anchor is — the clef draws it for you. Find the curl (G), find the two dots (F), and count outward from there.

Mnemonics are a fine secondary aid once the anchor is in place. In the treble clef the lines from the bottom (E G B D F) are often remembered as “Every Good Boy Does Fine,” and the spaces (F A C E) spell FACE. In the bass clef the lines (G B D F A) are “Good Boys Do Fine Always” and the spaces (A C E G) are “All Cows Eat Grass.” Useful — but they are scaffolding on top of the anchor, not a substitute for it.

3. The grand staff and middle C

A single staff covers only so much range before notes pile up on ledger lines and become hard to read. Piano music — and any music spanning a wide range — joins a treble-clef staff (upper, usually the right hand) and a bass-clef staff (lower, usually the left hand) with a brace on the left. Together they form the grand staff (British: great stave).

Why two staves — and where middle C lives

The two clefs are positioned so the staves meet almost exactly at middle C (C4). Middle C falls in the gap between the staves: it is written on the first ledger line above the bass staff or, identically, the first ledger line below the treble staff — the same pitch, reachable from either side. That shared note is the hinge of the grand staff and the reason a pianist’s two hands read as one continuous range: the bass clef covers the notes below middle C, the treble clef the notes above, and middle C bridges them.

Middle C (C4) on the keyboard — the C nearest the centre of a full piano. It is the note that sits on the ledger line between the two staves of the grand staff, equally the lowest comfortable treble note and the highest comfortable bass note.

4. Ledger lines: extending the staff

Five lines cannot hold every pitch. When a note runs higher or lower than the staff, we draw short ledger lines (also spelled leger) — tiny line segments, just wider than the notehead, stacked above or below the staff to extend it. Each ledger line continues the same line-then-space alphabet: the first ledger line below the treble staff is middle C, the next space down is B3, the next ledger line A3, and so on. They are simply the staff carried on past its printed edge.

5. Reading note names in both clefs

Put the anchor logic to work. To name any note: find the clef’s anchor (G4 on the treble second line, F3 on the bass fourth line), then step up or down the alphabet — line, space, line, space — to the note you want. With practice the common notes become instant and you only count for the outliers.

Notice that the same printed position means different pitches in different clefs. A note in the bottom space of the treble staff is F4; a note in the bottom space of the bass staff is A2. The clef is what decides — which is the whole point of having one.

6. Accidentals on the staff — and the end-of-bar rule

An accidental — a sharp (♯), flat (♭) or natural (♮) — is written immediately to the left of the notehead it alters, on the same line or space. A sharp raises the note a half step, a flat lowers it a half step, and a natural cancels a previous sharp or flat. (These are the same twelve pitches from Lesson 1 — here we are only learning how to write them.)

An accidental lasts to the end of the bar

The crucial rule: an accidental applies not just to the note it touches but to every later note of the same pitch (same line or space) for the rest of that measure, unless a different accidental overrides it. The barline cancels it: once you cross into the next measure, the note is back to whatever the key signature says, and an accidental from the previous bar no longer applies. (The one exception is a note tied across the barline, which keeps its accidental for the duration of the tie.) A natural sign is how you cancel an accidental before the bar is over.

So in the score below, the sharp on the first F makes the second F in that bar sharp as well — it carries — even though no second sharp is printed. To get an F natural again before the bar ends, you must write an explicit natural. The barline then resets everything.

7. Octave signs: 8va and 8vb

When a passage sits far above or below the staff, stacks of ledger lines get hard to read. The octave signs fix this without changing the pitch. An 8va (ottava alta) marking with a dashed line over a passage means “play this one octave higher than written”; an 8vb (ottava bassa), usually written below a passage, means “play this one octave lower than written.” They let the composer write a high or low line in an easy-to-read register while keeping the real octave clear, cutting the ledger-line clutter. We will keep these light for now and return to them when we read fuller scores.
