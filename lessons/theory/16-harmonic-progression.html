Harmonic progression, root motion & harmonizing a melody

You already know how to name the seven diatonic chords and sort them into functions (Lesson 12), and how a phrase ends with a cadence (Lesson 14). This lesson joins those ideas into motion: it explains why one chord tends to follow another, which root movements feel strong and which feel like backtracking, and how to turn a bare melody into a chord progression. Harmony stops being a list of chords and becomes a current that flows from home, builds tension, and returns.

  • Explain the T → PD → D → T functional cycle as the engine of progression.
  • Rank root motion by strength: descending fifth, descending third, ascending second — and spot a retrogression.
  • Read the diatonic “traffic-flow” map: which chords lead where.
  • Recognise the workhorse progressions: I–IV–V–I, the 50s loop, ii–V–I, the four-chord loop, and the 12-bar blues.
  • Harmonize a simple melody: plan the cadence, pick chords that contain each note, prefer strong root motion.

1. Why chords move: the functional cycle

A progression is not a random shuffle of the diatonic chords. The chords sort into three functions — you met them in Lesson 12 — and those functions flow in a strong default order:

Tonic (T) → Predominant (PD) → Dominant (D) → Tonic (T)

Tonic (I, and its stand-ins vi and iii) is home — stable, at rest. Predominant (ii and IV) leaves home and sets up the dominant. Dominant (V and vii°) holds the leading tone, the most tense, least stable function, and it pulls hard back to the tonic. The cycle is a small drama: departure, rising tension, release. Every progression below is a variation on it.

Why this exact order, and not the reverse?

The dominant contains the leading tone (scale degree 7), a half step below tonic, and the chordal seventh when it is a V7. Both are dissonances that the ear hears as needing to resolve up and down respectively, straight into tonic chord-tones. So D → T feels inevitable — the tension manufactured by the dominant gets paid off by the tonic. Going the other way (T → D directly) is fine, but D → PD undoes the build-up and sounds like the music is reversing — a retrogression (section 2). The predominant exists precisely to delay and strengthen the arrival of the dominant, which is why it sits between tonic and dominant.

2. Root motion: which moves feel strong

Function tells you what role a chord plays; root motion — the interval between the roots of two adjacent chords — tells you how strong the move sounds. Three kinds of motion drive most tonal music, and their strength is not a matter of taste: it follows from how many tones the two chords share and how the leading tone behaves.

  • Descending fifth (equivalently, ascending fourth — the same motion named two ways) is the strongest and most frequent of all. The two chords share exactly one common tone, and the root of the first becomes the fifth of the second, giving a firm, directed pull. This is the motion of V → I, ii → V, and the whole circle of fifths.
  • Descending third (e.g. I → vi, vi → IV) is smooth: the two triads share two common tones, so the harmony shifts gently without a strong jolt. Ideal for prolonging a function and easing between areas.
  • Ascending second (e.g. IV → V, I → ii) is also strong, even though the chords share no common tone — precisely because there is no shared note, every voice has to move, and the change registers clearly. This is the standard way a predominant pushes into the dominant.

Run the descending-fifth motion all the way around the diatonic circle and you pass through every chord in the key before landing back on tonic: I – IV – vii° – iii – vi – ii – V – I. Each step is a falling fifth (roots C–F–B–E–A–D–G–C).

The strongest single link — V → I — is a descending fifth from G down to C. Seen on the keyboard, the root simply drops from the fifth degree to home:

The root of the dominant (G) falls a fifth to the root of the tonic (C) — the single most decisive move in tonal harmony. Every other descending-fifth link (ii→V, vi→ii, …) has the same shape.

Retrogressions: motion that feels backward

Reverse a strong move and you usually get a weaker one. An ascending fifth (= descending fourth), such as V → IV, runs against the functional current — the dominant should resolve to tonic, not fall back to a predominant. In common-practice classical style this retrogression is used sparingly and was often marked wrong in counterpoint classes. But it is completely idiomatic in blues, rock and pop, where V–IV–I is a signature sound (you will hear it in the 12-bar blues below).

Quick test for strength

When in doubt, ask: does the root fall a fifth, fall a third, or rise a step? If yes, the move is strong or smooth and will sound “right.” If the root rises a fifth or falls a step (other than into a cadence), you are probably retrogressing — fine as a deliberate colour, suspect as an accident.

Root motion Diatonic example Strength & character
Down a 5th (= up a 4th) V→I, ii→V, vi→ii Strongest, most common — one shared tone, firm pull
Down a 3rd I→vi, vi→IV, iii→I Smooth — two shared tones, gentle shift; good for prolonging
Up a 2nd IV→V, I→ii, V→vi Strong — no shared tone, every voice moves, change is clear
Up a 3rd I→iii, IV→vi Weak — two shared tones, near-static; used as prolongation
Up a 5th / down a 2nd V→IV (retrogression) Backward in classical style; idiomatic in blues / rock / pop

3. The diatonic traffic-flow map

Put function and root motion together and the seven chords behave like traffic obeying a one-way system. This map captures the strong default tendencies (you can always break them for effect):

  • I (tonic) — may go anywhere. It is home; every door is open from here.
  • ii and IV (predominant) — head to V or vii° (the dominant). Their job is to set up the dominant.
  • V and vii° (dominant) — resolve to I. The one common swerve is to vi instead — the deceptive move you met in Lesson 14, which dodges the expected tonic.
  • vi and iii (tonic-substitutes) — typically move toward a predominant (vi→ii or vi→IV; iii→vi or iii→IV), prolonging the journey before the real push to the dominant begins.

Notice this is just the functional cycle of section 1 spelled out chord by chord: tonic chords flow to predominants, predominants to dominants, dominants back to tonic — and the descending-fifth links (ii→V, V→I) are the strongest stitches holding it together.

Why is vi the deceptive substitute?

vi shares two of its three notes with I (in C major, vi = A–C–E, I = C–E–G — they share C and E). So when the dominant resolves to vi instead of I, the ear gets most of the tonic it was promised — enough to feel a resolution — but the bass lands a third too high and the leading tone resolves to a non-tonic root, so the phrase feels gently cheated and pressed to continue. That is the whole effect of the deceptive cadence: resolution withheld at the last second.

4. The workhorse progressions

A handful of progressions recur across centuries and genres because each one threads the functional cycle cleanly. Learn to hear and play these and you can navigate most tonal music.

I–IV–V–I — the primary triads

The bedrock: tonic, predominant, dominant, tonic, using only the three major triads of the key. IV→V is an ascending second (strong); V→I a descending fifth (strongest). On guitar these three roots line up vividly — in C they all sit at the 3rd fret.

The three primary roots in C, all at the 3rd fret: G on the low E string (6th), C on the A string (5th), F on the D string (4th). The I–IV–V chords are built on these.

I–vi–IV–V — the 50s / doo-wop loop

Add the tonic-substitute vi to soften the start and you get the 50s progression, also called the doo-wop progression (heard in Heart and Soul, Stand by Me, and a thousand others). I→vi is a smooth descending third; vi→IV another descending third; IV→V the strong step up; V loops back to I. It can cycle indefinitely.

ii–V–I — the jazz cadence

The most important cadential formula in jazz, and ubiquitous in classical music too. It is two descending fifths back to back: ii falls a fifth to V, V falls a fifth to I — the strongest possible approach to the tonic. Predominant, dominant, tonic, with the cleanest root motion there is.

I–V–vi–IV — the four-chord pop loop

The famous “four-chord song” loop behind countless pop hits. It keeps the tonic feeling fresh by passing through the dominant and the deceptive vi before the predominant IV cycles it round again. Same four chords as the 50s loop, reordered — proof that order (root motion), not just chord choice, makes the character.

5. Harmonizing a melody

Now the practical payoff: given a bare tune, choose a chord for each note so the result is a real progression, not a pile-up of clashes. There is a reliable order of operations.

  1. Find the key and plan the cadence first. Decide where phrases end and what cadence each gets (Lesson 14). A final perfect authentic cadence almost always wants V–I under the last two melody notes — lock that in before anything else, and work backward from it.
  2. For each melody note, list the chords that contain it. A melody note can be the root, third, or fifth of several diatonic chords. In C, the note C belongs to I (C E G), IV (F A C) and vi (A C E) — three candidates.
  3. Choose among the candidates by root motion. Prefer the option that gives a strong move (descending fifth, ascending second) and that fits the functional flow toward your planned cadence. This is where the traffic-map of section 3 earns its keep.
  4. Check the voice leading — avoid parallels. When you voice the chords, do not let two voices move in parallel perfect fifths or octaves (the rule from Lesson 13). Keep common tones, move the other voices by the smallest step, and the harmonization will sound smooth.

The cadence (the last two chords, V→I) was fixed first; every melody note is a chord-tone of the chord beneath it; and the root motion is all strong descending-fifth (V→I) or static-tonic moves.

Two practical shortcuts

(1) Strong beats matter most. Harmonize the notes on strong beats (beats 1 and 3) with chord-tones; notes on weak beats can be passing or neighbour tones (Lesson 15) that need no chord of their own. (2) One chord per beat is plenty — often one or two chords per bar gives a more musical harmonic rhythm than a new chord on every note.
