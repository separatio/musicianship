Diatonic harmony, Roman numerals & function

Once you can build a triad (Lesson 10) and a seventh chord (Lesson 11), the next question is: which chords belong to a key, and how do we name them so an analysis reads at a glance? This lesson stacks a triad on every degree of the scale to get the seven diatonic chords, labels them with Roman numerals, and then sorts them into three functions — the engine that makes harmony feel like it leaves home and comes back.

  • Build a triad on each scale degree and know its quality by ear and on paper.
  • Recite the diatonic triads of any major key (I ii iii IV V vi vii°) and minor key.
  • Read and write Roman numerals: case, the ° and + symbols, inversion figures.
  • Group chords into tonic, predominant, and dominant function.
  • Explain why the T → PD → D → T cycle creates tension and release.

1. Building a triad on every scale degree

A diatonic chord uses only the notes of one scale — no borrowed accidentals. To find all the diatonic triads of a key, stand on each scale degree in turn and stack two thirds above it (root + 3rd + 5th), drawing every note from the scale. Because the scale's pattern of whole and half steps is uneven, the thirds you stack come out different sizes from degree to degree — and that is what gives each chord its quality.

Why the qualities differ within one key

A major triad is a major third (4 half steps) with a minor third (3) on top; a minor triad reverses that order; a diminished triad is two minor thirds. Since the major scale spaces its notes unevenly, the third above each degree is sometimes major and sometimes minor. Stack them and you get a fixed recipe that is the same in every major key: build on the tonic and you always get major; build on the second degree and you always get minor; and so on. The key changes the letters, never the pattern.

2. The diatonic triads of a minor key

A natural-minor key reuses the same seven letters as its relative major but starts on a different degree, so the qualities land in a new order. Building triads up A natural minor (again, white keys only — A minor is the relative of C major) gives:

So the natural-minor pattern is minor, diminished, major, minor, minor, major, major: i, ii°, III, iv, v, VI, VII. Notice it is C major's pattern rotated to start on the sixth degree — the relative-minor relationship from Lesson 7 at work.

The raised seventh: V and vii° in minor

The natural-minor v (Em) is a weak dominant — it has no leading tone pulling up to the tonic. In practice, composers raise the seventh degree (G → G♯ in A minor) to restore that pull, which is exactly the harmonic minor scale of Lesson 7. That single raised note turns the v into a major V (E–G♯–B) and the III into an augmented III+, and it gives a genuine diminished vii° (G♯–B–D). These raised-seventh forms are so common that a V or vii° in a minor key is simply assumed to use the raised leading tone.

3. Roman-numeral notation

A Roman numeral names two things at once: the scale degree of the chord's root (the numeral itself, 1 through 7) and the chord's quality (how the numeral is dressed). The conventions are tidy and worth memorising exactly:

  • UPPERCASE (I, IV, V) = a major triad.
  • lowercase (ii, iii, vi) = a minor triad.
  • lowercase + ° (vii°, ii°) = a diminished triad.
  • UPPERCASE + + (III+, V+) = an augmented triad.

That is the whole system for triads. So in C major, reading “V” you instantly know: root on the 5th degree (G), and major quality (G–B–D). Reading “vii°”: root on the 7th degree (B), diminished quality (B–D–F).

Why case carries the quality

Letter-name chord symbols (C, Dm, G7) tell you the absolute pitch but say nothing about the key. Roman numerals are relative to the key, so the same analysis fits any transposition — a I–IV–V–I reads identically in C, in E♭, or in B. Letting the case do the quality work keeps the symbol compact: one glyph encodes degree and major/minor, so an entire progression can be written in a single line a musician parses at sight.

Inversion figures

When a chord is not in root position, a figured-bass number is appended to show which chord tone is in the bass (the idea comes from Baroque figured bass; the full system is in Lesson 14). For triads there are just two cases:

Seventh chords take three-digit figures (e.g. ii6/5 is a first-inversion ii7). You do not need the full set yet — just recognise that a small number after a numeral, like V6 or ii6/5, is telling you the bass note, not changing the chord's root or function.

4. Functional harmony: tonic, predominant, dominant

The seven chords do not behave equally. Centuries of common-practice music sort them into three functions — families of chords that play the same dramatic role:

  • Tonic (T) — home, rest, arrival: I, and its quieter relatives vi and iii (which share notes with I).
  • Predominant / subdominant (PD) — departure, the step away from home that sets up the dominant: IV and ii.
  • Dominant (D) — maximum tension, the strong pull back home: V and vii° (both contain the leading tone).

Healthy harmony tends to flow in a cycle: T → PD → D → T — leave home, prepare, build tension, resolve. You can shorten it (T → D → T, the bare I–V–I) but you rarely run it backwards: a dominant falling straight to a predominant (V → IV) sounds like a deflation, which is exactly why it is used for effect rather than as a default.

Why the cycle feels like departure and return

Function is really about shared notes and tendency tones. The dominant V contains the leading tone (in C major, B), a half step that strains upward to the tonic (C); V also holds the 4th degree's neighbour, and a V7 adds F, which leans down to E. Those two voices form a tritone that wants to resolve outward into the tonic triad. The predominant (IV or ii) sits between rest and tension: it shares notes with both, so it smooths the move from home to the dominant. The result is a narrative — stability, motion, tension, release — and that arc, not the individual chords, is what your ear hears as “a progression.” This is the seed of everything in Lesson 16, harmonic progression.
