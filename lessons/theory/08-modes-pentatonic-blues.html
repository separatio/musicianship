Modes, pentatonic & blues scales

You already own the major scale and the three minor scales. This lesson squeezes far more music out of them for almost no new effort. The seven modes are just the major scale started from each of its own notes; the pentatonic scales are the major and minor scales with their two most troublesome notes removed; and the blues scale is the minor pentatonic with one spicy note added back. Together they are the raw material of most folk, rock, pop, jazz and — obviously — blues, and they are what you actually solo with on the guitar.

  • Build all seven diatonic modes by rotating one parent major scale.
  • Name each mode's characteristic note versus major or minor.
  • Order the modes on a bright-to-dark continuum and know why.
  • Spell the major (1 2 3 5 6) and minor (1 b3 4 5 b7) pentatonics.
  • Build the blues scale and find it under your fingers on the neck.

1. Modes: one scale, seven starting points

Take the white keys of the piano — the C major scale — and play them straight up from C to C. That is the mode called Ionian; it is the major scale. Now play the very same white keys but start and end on D instead. The notes are identical, yet it sounds different, because the pattern of whole and half steps now falls in a new place relative to your new home note. That rotation is the mode Dorian. Keep shifting the starting note and you get all seven diatonic modes.

Why a reshuffle changes the colour

A scale's character comes from where its half steps sit relative to the tonic — you learned this building major versus minor. C major's half steps fall between degrees 3–4 and 7–8. Start the same notes from D and those half steps now land between your degrees 2–3 and 6–7 — a different interval pattern above the tonic, so a different mood, even though not a single pitch changed. The modes are seven ways to hear one collection of notes, each with a different note crowned as “home.”

Each mode is named after a degree of the parent major scale. Starting from C major, the “white-key trick” gives every mode for free:

Mode Start degree (in C major) White-key root Bright / dark Characteristic alteration
Ionian 1 C Bright The major scale itself (1 2 3 4 5 6 7)
Dorian 2 D Dark (minor) Natural minor with a raised 6 (♮6)
Phrygian 3 E Dark (minor) Natural minor with a lowered 2 (♭2)
Lydian 4 F Bright (major) Major with a raised 4 (♯4)
Mixolydian 5 G Bright (major) Major with a lowered 7 (♭7)
Aeolian 6 A Dark (minor) The natural minor scale itself (1 2 ♭3 4 5 ♭6 ♭7)
Locrian 7 B Darkest (diminished) Minor with a lowered 2 and lowered 5 (♭2, ♭5)
The bright-to-dark continuum

There is a tidy way to order the modes. Three modes have a major third above the tonic — they sound bright: Lydian (brightest), Ionian, Mixolydian. Four have a minor third — they sound dark: Dorian, Aeolian, Phrygian, Locrian (darkest). Walk from brightest to darkest and you flatten exactly one more scale degree at each step: Lydian → Ionian (♭4) → Mixolydian (♭7) → Dorian (♭3) → Aeolian (♭6) → Phrygian (♭2) → Locrian (♭5). One note darker each time.

Two modes on the white keys

Here are two of the rotations spelled out — both use only natural notes, both start in the octave above middle C. Listen for how different they feel from a plain major scale even though no black keys appear.

Both share C major's exact seven notes — what changes is which note you treat as home, and therefore where the half steps land. (You will return to modes as a compositional resource, not just a label, in Lesson 19, Modal mixture.)

2. The pentatonic scales: five safe notes

A pentatonic scale has five notes per octave instead of seven. The two that matter most come straight from scales you already know, each by deleting two degrees.

  • Major pentatonic = 1 2 3 5 6. Take the major scale and drop the 4th and 7th. C major pentatonic is C D E G A.
  • Minor pentatonic = 1 ♭3 4 5 ♭7. Take the natural minor scale and drop the 2nd and ♭6. A minor pentatonic is A C D E G.

Notice those are the same five notes (A C D E G = C D E G A re-ordered): A minor pentatonic is the relative of C major pentatonic, exactly as A minor is the relative of C major. One shape, two tonal centres.

Why drop the 4th and the 7th?

Because those two are the restless notes. In a major key the 7th (the leading tone) strains upward to the tonic, the 4th leans down to the 3rd, and 4 against 7 forms the tritone — the most dissonant interval in the scale (you met it in Lesson 5). Players call notes that clash with the underlying chord avoid notes. Strip out the 4th and 7th and the tritone goes with them: what remains is five notes with no half steps and no tritone, so almost any one of them sounds consonant over almost any chord in the key. That forgiving quality is exactly why pentatonics are the first scales handed to a beginning improviser.

A minor pentatonic on the keyboard, A4 to A5: the five lit keys are A, C, D, E, G. They are all white because A minor pentatonic, like C major, uses no sharps or flats.

3. A minor pentatonic on the guitar — “box 1”

On the guitar the minor pentatonic lives in five repeating box shapes. The first and most-used one for A minor sits at the 5th fret, anchored by the root A on the low E string. This is the shape under nearly every rock and blues solo you have ever heard. Two notes per string, straight across; the diagram reads like a chord chart, with the low E string (string 6) on the left and the high E string (string 1) on the right.

A minor pentatonic, box 1 (frets 5–8). String by string, low to high: E (6th) A·5, C·8 — A (5th) D·5, E·7 — D (4th) G·5, A·7 — G (3rd) C·5, D·7 — B (2nd) E·5, G·8 — e (1st) A·5, C·8. Only the five notes A C D E G appear; the three A’s (low-E fret 5, D-string fret 7, high-E fret 5) are the roots.

4. The blues scale: add the blue note

The blues scale is the minor pentatonic with one extra note slipped in: the ♭5, the “blue note.” That makes it six notes: 1 ♭3 4 ♭5 5 ♭7. For A that is A C D E♭ E G — the E♭ sits as a passing tone squeezed between D and the natural E.

Use the blue note in passing

The ♭5 is deliberately dissonant — it is what makes a lick sound “bluesy” rather than merely minor. Treat it as a chromatic passing note: slide through it on the way between the 4th (D) and the 5th (E), or bend up to it, rather than landing and sitting on it. Held too long it sours; flicked through, it cries.

On the guitar the blues scale is simply box 1 with the blue notes (E♭) dropped in. Inside the 5th–8th-fret window the new note shows up in two spots: the A string at fret 6 and the G string at fret 8. Everything else is the minor-pentatonic shape you just learned.

A blues scale, box 1. Same shape as the minor pentatonic above, plus the two blue notes (E♭, ♭5) marked in blue: A string fret 6 and G string fret 8. Reach the E♭ on the A string with a quick chromatic step from D (fret 5) up to E (fret 7).

5. A glance at the symmetrical scales

Most scales so far are built from an uneven pattern of steps, which is what gives each note a distinct job. A couple of scales instead repeat one interval pattern over and over, so they sound deliberately rootless and floating — useful for dreamlike or unstable passages. You will meet them properly later; for now just know they exist.

  • Whole-tone scale: six notes, every step a whole step (e.g. C D E F♯ G♯ A♯). No half steps at all, so no leading tone and no sense of a tonic — think Debussy’s shimmer.
  • Octatonic (diminished) scale: eight notes built by alternating whole and half steps. Highly symmetrical and a staple of jazz over diminished and dominant chords.

These belong to the family of symmetrical “collections” that twentieth-century composers leaned on; the pentatonic itself is counted among them.
