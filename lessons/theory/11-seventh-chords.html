Seventh chords: types, inversions & figured bass

A seventh chord is a triad with one more third stacked on top — a fourth note a seventh above the root. That single added note is what turns the plain, settled sound of a triad into something richer and, more importantly, directional: most seventh chords lean somewhere and want to move. This lesson names the five seventh chords you meet first, spells each one exactly from C, shows why the dominant seventh in particular drives so much music forward, and then turns each chord on its head through its inversions and the figured-bass shorthand that labels them.

  • Build a seventh chord by adding one more third above a triad.
  • Spell the five common types from C — and know which seventh each takes.
  • Hear why the dominant seventh's internal tritone pulls home.
  • Put any chord tone in the bass: root, 3rd, 5th, or 7th inversion.
  • Read and write the figures 7, 6/5, 4/3, and 4/2 (or 2).

Before this lesson

  • Lesson 10 — Triads. A seventh chord is a triad plus one note, so you need major, minor, diminished and augmented triads — and their inversions — already in hand.

1. Adding a seventh: one more third on the stack

A triad is three notes stacked in thirds: root, third, fifth. Keep going — add one more third above the fifth — and you reach a note a seventh above the root. That is a seventh chord: root, 3rd, 5th, 7th, four notes, all a third apart. From C the four letters are C E G B before any accidental decides the qualities.

Why the seventh changes everything

A triad is a closed, stable object — it can simply be the chord at rest. The seventh is a dissonance against the root (a seventh is one of the two harsh interval classes, from Lesson 5), and a dissonance is a question that wants an answer. So adding a seventh doesn't just colour the chord — it gives it momentum. The chord now points away from itself, and the ear expects the dissonant seventh to step down to a consonance. That single pull is the engine of most functional harmony you will analyse in Lesson 12.

Two ingredients fix a seventh chord's type: the triad underneath (major, minor, or diminished) and the quality of the seventh above the root (major, minor, or diminished). The five combinations below are the ones that occur constantly in tonal music; you can name any of them aloud as “triad + seventh.”

2. The five common types, spelled from C

Here are the five built on the same root, C, so you can see exactly which notes change as the chord darkens. Read each row as a recipe: the triad gives the lower three notes, the seventh's quality gives the top one.

Type Symbol Triad + seventh Notes from C Seventh interval
Major seventh Cmaj7 major triad + major 7th C E G B M7 (11 half steps)
Dominant (major-minor) seventh C7 major triad + minor 7th C E G B♭ m7 (10 half steps)
Minor seventh Cm7 minor triad + minor 7th C E♭ G B♭ m7 (10 half steps)
Half-diminished seventh Cø7 diminished triad + minor 7th C E♭ G♭ B♭ m7 (10 half steps)
Fully diminished seventh C°7 diminished triad + diminished 7th C E♭ G♭ B♭♭ (=A) d7 (9 half steps)

Notice the staircase: each step down the table lowers one more note. The major seventh is the brightest; lower the seventh and you get the dominant; lower the third as well and you get the minor seventh; lower the fifth too and you reach half-diminished; lower the seventh again and the chord is fully diminished — four notes, each a minor third apart.

Why we write B♭♭, not A

On the piano the top note of C°7 is the white key A — so why not just call it A? Because a seventh chord is, by definition, a stack of thirds: C (1) up to the seventh must span the seven letters C-D-E-F-G-A-B. The top note has to be some kind of B for the chord to be a seventh at all. A diminished seventh above C is therefore B♭♭, even though it sounds identical to A. Spell it A and the interval C–A reads as a sixth, and the chord stops being analysable. This is the same spelling-carries-function rule from Lesson 5: the notation must show what the note is doing, not just how it sounds. (Our score writes the pitch as A for the engine; read it as B♭♭.)

3. The dominant seventh's tritone — the engine of resolution

Of the five, the dominant seventh (C7) is the one with somewhere to go, and the reason hides inside the chord. Take its third and its seventh — in C7 that is E and B♭ — and measure the gap: six half steps, a tritone (the most unstable interval, Lesson 5). That tritone is what makes a dominant seventh sound unfinished.

Why the tritone resolves the way it does

A tritone is wedged between two stabler intervals and “wants out” in both directions at once. In C7 the third E is the leading tone of F major and strains up a half step to F; the seventh B♭ is a dissonance that sags down a half step to A. Resolve both and the tritone collapses outward — wait, inward — onto F and A, the third and root of an F-major chord. That is exactly why C7 sounds like “the dominant of F” and pulls there: the two voices of the tritone are each a half step from a note of the target chord. No other interval in the chord does this; the tritone is the whole mechanism.

You can hear it in two beats: C7 leaning, then released onto F major, with E→F rising and B♭→A falling.

A bonus from the fully diminished seventh

The fully diminished seventh (C°7) contains two tritones, not one — C–G♭ and E♭–B♭♭(A). That is why it sounds so unsettled and why it can pivot to many different keys: it has two engines, not one. You will use this property in later chromatic-harmony lessons.

4. Inversions: which chord tone is in the bass

Just like a triad, a seventh chord can be turned so that a note other than the root sits at the bottom. A four-note chord has four positions — one for each chord tone in the bass. The notes are the same; only the lowest one changes, and with it the chord's colour and name.

Position Bass note C7 from the bass up
Root position Root (C) C E G B♭
First inversion 3rd (E) E G B♭ C
Second inversion 5th (G) G B♭ C E
Third inversion 7th (B♭) B♭ C E G

The third inversion is the one a triad never has — it only exists because a seventh chord has a fourth note to put in the bass. Here are all four positions of C7 in a row, written in the bass clef so you read the bass note plainly each time.

5. Figured bass: naming the inversions

Figured bass is a centuries-old shorthand: under a bass note you write the intervals (counting by letter, above that bass note) of the other chord tones. For seventh chords the full stack would be cumbersome, so the tradition uses an abbreviated set of figures — one per inversion. Learn these four; they are the labels you will attach to Roman numerals in Lesson 12.

Position Bass Figure Where the figure comes from
Root position Root 7 The seventh above the bass is the defining note (the 3 and 5 are assumed).
First inversion 3rd 6/5 Above the 3rd, the other tones sit a 6th and a 5th away.
Second inversion 5th 4/3 Above the 5th, the other tones sit a 4th and a 3rd away.
Third inversion 7th 4/2 (or just 2) Above the 7th, the root sits a 2nd away — the giveaway interval.
Why these particular numbers

Every figure is just the interval, by letter, from the bass up to the other chord tones — exactly the inclusive counting from Lesson 5. Take C7 in first inversion, bass E: up to C is a sixth (E-F-G-A-B-C), up to B♭ is a fifth (E-F-G-A-B), up to G is a third. The convention keeps the distinctive figures — 6 and 5 — and drops the obvious 3, so it reads 6/5. The same logic gives 4/3 and 4/2. The numbers aren't arbitrary at all: they are a snapshot of the chord measured from whatever note happens to be in the bass, which is why a single figure tells a trained reader both the chord and its inversion at a glance.

Triad figures vs. seventh-chord figures

From Lesson 10 you already met the triad figures — root position (no figure, or 5/3), first inversion 6, second inversion 6/4. Seventh chords add the seventh into the count, which is why their figures (7, 6/5, 4/3, 4/2) all carry an extra number compared with the triad's. Same system, one more note.
