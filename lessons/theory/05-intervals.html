Intervals

An interval is the distance in pitch between two notes. It is the first idea in this course that you can hear, measure, and build with — every scale, chord, melody, and harmony below is just intervals stacked and strung together. Get intervals into your ears and your hands and most of the “why” of music opens up.

  • Measure any interval as a number + a quality.
  • Know the exact size in half steps (semitones) of every interval.
  • Spell intervals correctly — and see why spelling is not optional.
  • Invert an interval and find compound intervals.
  • Tell consonance from dissonance, and know the one famous exception.

1. Melodic and harmonic intervals

The two notes of an interval can be played one after the other — a melodic interval, the stuff of melodies — or at the same time — a harmonic interval, the stuff of chords. The name of the interval is identical either way; only the presentation differs.

2. The number: counting the distance

Every interval has a number (its generic size). You find it by counting letter names — equivalently staff lines and spaces — inclusively: the bottom note is 1, and you count up to and including the top note.

From C up to E, count C (1) — D (2) — E (3): a third. From C up to the next C, count all eight letters C-D-E-F-G-A-B-C: an octave (an 8th).

Why inclusive counting?

Because the number names the span of letters covered, not the number of steps between them. Two adjacent letters (C up to D) already cover two letter names, so they make a 2nd, not a 1st. The same note repeated covers one letter — a 1st, called a unison. This is also why, later, inversion sizes add up to nine rather than eight: the shared note gets counted in both halves.

The number ignores accidentals. C, C♯, and C♭ all count as “C” when you find the number. So C-E, C♯-E, and C-E♭ are all thirds — they just differ in quality, which is the next idea.

The same notes on the keyboard: C is the reference (scale degree 1), E the third, G the fifth — the bones of a C-major chord.

3. The quality: how big, exactly

The number alone is generic — “a third” could be wider or narrower by a half step. The quality makes it specific. There are five quality labels: Perfect (P), Major (M), minor (m), Augmented (A), and diminished (d).

Which qualities a number can take is fixed and absolute:

These numbers are Perfect These numbers are Major / minor
Unison (1), 4th, 5th, Octave (8) 2nd, 3rd, 6th, 7th

A 4th or 5th is never “major”; a 3rd or 6th is never “perfect.” But any interval can be pushed to Augmented (a half step wider) or diminished (a half step narrower).

Move a half step at a time and the quality slides along a ladder:

  • Imperfect numbers (2, 3, 6, 7): diminished — minor — Major — Augmented
  • Perfect numbers (1, 4, 5, 8): diminished — Perfect — Augmented (no “major/minor” rung exists here)
Rule of thumb

Augmented = one half step wider than Perfect or Major. diminished = one half step narrower than Perfect or minor. Note the asymmetry: from Major, “diminished” is two half steps down (you pass through minor first); from Perfect it is only one half step down.

Major third vs. minor third

The clearest place to hear quality is the third. C up to E is a major third (bright); lower the top note a half step to E♭ and you get a minor third (darker). Same number, different quality — and the whole major/minor colour of music rides on this one half step.

On the piano

From C, the major third lands on E (white), the minor third on E♭ (the black key just below it) — you can see the one-half-step difference.

On the guitar

C on the A string (5th string, 3rd fret) up to E on the D string (4th string, 2nd fret): a major third. Lower that E by one fret to E♭ (1st fret) and it becomes a minor third.

5. Spelling matters: enharmonics and the tritone

Two intervals can be the same number of half steps — they sound identical on a piano — yet be named differently, because they span a different number of letters. These are enharmonic equivalents. The classic case is the tritone (6 half steps): C up to F♯ is an Augmented fourth (C-D-E-F = four letters), while C up to G♭ is a diminished fifth (C-D-E-F-G = five letters). Same sound, different spelling, different name.

Why spelling is not optional

The number is fixed by the letters first; only then does the accidental set the quality. F♯ up to D♭ is 7 half steps, the size of a perfect fifth — but F and D are only six letters apart, so it is a diminished sixth, not a fifth. A real perfect fifth from F♯ must be spelled F♯-C♯. Spelling carries the musical function; the same sound can mean different things, and the notation tells you which.

6. Inversion

Invert an interval by flipping its notes — move the bottom note up an octave (or the top note down one) so they swap roles. Two patterns make inversion easy:

  • The Rule of 9: the number and its inversion always add up to 9 (a 3rd inverts to a 6th, a 2nd to a 7th, a 4th to a 5th, a unison to an octave).
  • Quality flips in pairs: Major ↔ minor, Augmented ↔ diminished, and Perfect stays Perfect.

So a major third (C-E) inverts to a minor sixth (E-C). Check it: the numbers 3 + 6 = 9, the quality Major → minor, and the half steps 4 + 8 = 12 (a full octave).

7. Compound intervals

Intervals up to an octave are simple. Anything wider is compound — a simple interval plus one or more octaves. To name a compound interval, add 7 to the simple number (again, because the shared octave note is counted twice). The quality carries straight over.

Compound=Simple + octave
9th (e.g. M9)=2nd + octave (M2)
10th=3rd + octave
11th=4th + octave (P4)
13th=6th + octave

You will meet these constantly in chord names — a “C major 9” is a chord that reaches up a ninth. (More in Lesson 21, Extended & altered chords.)

8. Consonance and dissonance

Some intervals sound stable and at rest — consonant; others sound tense and want to move — dissonant. This drives almost all harmonic motion: dissonance creates the pull, consonance the release.

  • Perfect consonances: unison (P1), perfect fifth (P5), octave (P8).
  • Imperfect consonances: major and minor thirds (M3, m3), major and minor sixths (M6, m6).
  • Dissonances: the seconds (m2, M2), the sevenths (m7, M7), the tritone (A4/d5), and all augmented and diminished intervals.
The perfect fourth — the famous exception

The perfect fourth is the one interval the tradition can't agree on. Acoustically it is a near-perfect consonance (a simple 4:3 frequency ratio). But in strict voice-leading and species counterpoint it is treated as a dissonance when it sits directly above the bass with nothing under it — the species-counterpoint tradition codified by Fux, and inherited by Haydn, Mozart and Beethoven, treated the bare fourth as something that needs resolving. Put a note below the fourth and the tension evaporates. So: consonant on its own, but dissonant against the bass. You will return to exactly this in Lesson 22 (species counterpoint).
