Score-reading conventions

Pitch and rhythm tell you which notes and when. But a page of correct pitches and rhythms is still not music — it is a skeleton. The conventions in this lesson are the muscle and breath: how loud (dynamics), how the notes are joined or detached (articulation), how fast and in what mood (tempo and expression), and in what order you actually travel through the page (the repeat-and- jump road map). Composers write these in because the same notes can be a lullaby or a war-cry depending on them.

  • Read the dynamic ladder from ppp to fff, plus hairpins and accents.
  • Recognise the core articulations — staccato, slur, accent, tenuto, marcato, fermata — and know what each does.
  • Translate the common tempo words, with rough BPM, and know they describe character, not just speed.
  • Read expression terms like dolce, cantabile and espressivo.
  • Navigate a score with repeats, 1st/2nd endings, D.C., D.S., Coda and Fine — including D.S. al Coda.

1. Dynamics: how loud

Dynamics tell you the volume, and almost always in relative terms — there is no decibel meter, only louder-than and softer-than. The whole range runs on a ladder of Italian words, built from two roots: piano (soft) and forte (loud). Stack the same word for “more” (pp = very soft), and use mezzo (“half”) for the two middle rungs.

Symbol Italian Meaning
ppppianississimoas soft as possible
pppianissimovery soft
ppianosoft
mpmezzo-pianomedium-soft
mfmezzo-fortemedium-loud
fforteloud
fffortissimovery loud
ffffortississimoas loud as possible

The order softest → loudest is ppp < pp < p < mp < mf < f < ff < fff. The trap to remember: mp is louder than p, and mf louder than mp — “mezzo” pulls a soft marking up toward the middle, not further down. Scores occasionally push past the ends (pppp, ffff); Tchaikovsky famously wrote pppppp. These are emphasis, not new units.

Why Italian?

Italy dominated European music through the Baroque era — opera was born there, Venice's Petrucci printed the first polyphony from movable type in 1501, and new musical developments originated in Italy and spread outward over the following decades. By the time a shared vocabulary for performance markings settled, Italian was already the trade language of printed music, and it stuck. That is why a German, a Russian and a Brazilian score all say forte, not a local word.

Gradual change: hairpins, crescendo, decrescendo

A fixed letter sets a level; to change level gradually you use a crescendo (grow louder) or a decrescendo / diminuendo (grow softer — the two words mean the same thing). These appear either as the spelled-out words cresc. and dim., or as hairpins: an opening wedge < for crescendo, a closing wedge > for diminuendo. The hairpin shows the shape at a glance — it literally widens as the sound swells.

One more: sforzando (sf or sfz) is not a level but a jolt — a sudden strong accent on one note or chord. Related is fp (forte-piano): hit it loud, then drop instantly to soft.

2. Articulation: how the notes are joined

Articulation governs the connection or separation between notes and the attack at the front of each one. It is the difference between a phrase sung in one smooth breath and one spat out in clipped points — same pitches, opposite character. These are the everyday marks:

Mark Symbol What it does
Staccato dot above/below the note Detached and shortened — release early, leaving silence before the next note.
Legato / slur curved line over the notes Smooth and connected — no gap between notes; one bow-stroke on strings, one breath when singing.
Accent > (horizontal wedge) A stressed attack — lean into the start of the note, then let it taper.
Tenuto – (short horizontal line) Hold the note its full value (or a touch longer), with a slight stress — the opposite of clipping it.
Marcato ^ (vertical wedge / “hat”) Marked and forceful — a heavier, more emphatic attack than a plain accent.
Fermata 𝄐 (eye / bird's-eye) Hold the note (or rest) beyond its written value, for as long as the performer or conductor wants.
Staccato vs. tenuto — the two poles

Think of articulation as a spectrum of note-length. At one end, staccato shrinks a note to a point and surrounds it with silence. At the other, tenuto stretches it to its full written value with no gap at all. Slur goes further still, fusing several notes into one gesture. Everything else (accent, marcato) is mostly about the attack, not the length.

Sources differ slightly on marcato: most pedagogy treats it as “stronger than an accent,” while some references describe it as as loud as an accent but also as short as staccato. Either way, read it as the most emphatic of the attack marks. Here are the main articulations on one staff:

3. Tempo: how fast — and in what spirit

A tempo marking, usually at the top-left of the first staff, sets the speed. Most are Italian words rather than exact numbers, because they were never meant to be only a speed — each one also carries a character. Allegro does not just mean “fast”; it literally means cheerful. Largo means broad. Adagio means at ease. Play the mood, not only the metronome.

Term Character Rough BPM
Largobroad, very slow~40–60
Adagioslow, at ease~66–76
Andantea walking pace~76–108
Moderatomoderate~108–120
Allegrofast, cheerful~120–168
Vivacelively, brisk~156–176
Prestovery fast~168–200

Read those numbers as ballparks, not law — sources genuinely disagree, especially at the slow end (some start Adagio at 44, others at 66) and around the Allegro/Vivace overlap. The reliable fact is the order: Largo < Adagio < Andante < Moderato < Allegro < Vivace < Presto, with Vivace sitting just above Allegro on the way to Presto.

Why the words beat the numbers

A metronome number is a single fact; a tempo word is an instruction about feeling. Two pieces at 120 BPM marked Allegro and Moderato are asking for different things — one buoyant and forward, one settled and even. The Italian tells the player which. This is why composers kept the words even after the metronome existed.

Changing the tempo

Tempo is not fixed for a whole piece. Four common change-markings:

  • ritardando (rit.) — gradually slowing down.
  • accelerando (accel.) — gradually speeding up.
  • a tempo — return to the previous, steady tempo (cancels a rit. or accel.).
  • rubato — “stolen time”: a flexible, expressive give-and-take, robbing a little time here and paying it back there.

4. Expression terms

Beyond loudness and speed, composers add words of character — how a passage should feel. You will meet these constantly, usually beside a tempo or dynamic mark:

Term Meaning
dolcesweetly
cantabilein a singing style
espressivoexpressively
legatosmoothly, connected
sostenutosustained, lengthened
graziosogracefully
con briowith spirit and vigour

These combine freely with everything above: a line might be marked Andante cantabile (a singing walking pace) or Allegro con brio (fast, with fire — the opening of Beethoven's Fifth). Read every word; each one narrows the performance.

5. The road map of a score

The last convention is the most practical: scores rarely play straight from top to bottom. To save paper and ink, composers reuse sections with repeat-and-jump markings. Reading them wrong means playing the wrong music in the wrong order, so learn the map.

  • Repeat signs — a section bounded by 𝄆…𝄇 (|: … :|) is played, then played again. A lone :| with no opening sign means repeat from the very start.
  • 1st & 2nd endings (volta brackets) — a bracket marked 1. is played the first time only; on the repeat you skip it and play the 2. bracket instead. This lets a repeated section end two different ways.
  • D.C. (da capo, “from the head”) — go back to the very beginning of the piece.
  • D.S. (dal segno, “from the sign”) — go back to the segno (𝄋), not the start.
  • Coda (𝄌) — a separate closing section. A To Coda mark tells you when to jump out to it.
  • Fine — the end. The piece stops here, even though the page may continue.

These combine into instructions like D.C. al Fine (go back to the start, play until Fine) and D.S. al Coda (go back to the sign, play until To Coda, then jump to the Coda).

Walking through “D.S. al Coda”

Say you reach the words D.S. al Coda at the end of bar 16. Step by step: (1) jump back to the segno 𝄋 — say it sits at bar 5. (2) Play forward from bar 5. (3) When you hit the To Coda mark — say at bar 9 — stop going straight on. (4) Jump to the Coda 𝄌 section. (5) Play the Coda to the end. The difference from al Fine: that one has no jump — you simply play through and stop at the word Fine.
