Triads

A triad is the smallest complete chord: three notes stacked in thirds. It is where the lessons on intervals and scales cash out — almost every chord you will ever play is a triad, or a triad with notes added on top. Here we build the four qualities, turn each one upside down through its inversions, and learn the shorthand — figured bass — that classical musicians use to name which note is in the bass.

  • Build a triad as root + third + fifth, stacked in thirds.
  • Spell and hear all four qualities: major, minor, diminished, augmented.
  • Know why the third sets the colour and the fifth sets the tension.
  • Put any chord member in the bass: root position, 1st and 2nd inversion.
  • Read and write the figured bass for triads: 5/3, 6, and 6/4.

1. What a triad is: thirds stacked

Take a note — the root. Stack a third above it — the third. Stack another third above that — the fifth. Three notes, two stacked thirds, and the outer span from root to fifth is some kind of fifth. That is a triad. Built on C, the plainest triad is C–E–G.

The names root, third, and fifth are just the interval of each note measured up from the root (a 3rd above, a 5th above). They stay attached to those notes no matter how you later rearrange the chord — that is the idea that makes inversions in section 3 work.

The same C-major triad on the keyboard: C is the root, E the third, G the fifth. Notice the gaps — root to third skips D, third to fifth skips F. Every triad has this “every-other-key” look when it is packed into one octave.

2. The four qualities

The two thirds can each be major (M3, 4 half steps) or minor (m3, 3 half steps), and the choice of each third gives four combinations — the four triad qualities. Built on C they are:

Quality Lower third Upper third Root→5th On C
Major M3 m3 Perfect 5th C–E–G
minor m3 M3 Perfect 5th C–E♭–G
diminished m3 m3 diminished 5th C–E♭–G♭
augmented M3 M3 Augmented 5th C–E–G♯

Read the pattern: major is “big-then-small” (M3 + m3), minor is “small-then-big” (m3 + M3); both keep a perfect fifth. diminished is two small thirds (m3 + m3), which squeezes the outer interval down to a diminished fifth; augmented is two big thirds (M3 + M3), which stretches it up to an augmented fifth.

Why the third sets the colour, the fifth sets the tension

The quality of the lower third alone decides major-vs-minor — the bright/dark colour the ear hears instantly. That is why “major” and “minor” both keep a perfect fifth: they differ only in that one third (E vs. E♭). The fifth is a separate matter: a perfect fifth is consonant and stable, so major and minor triads sound settled. Shrink or stretch the fifth to a diminished or augmented fifth — both dissonant intervals (see Lesson 5) — and the triad itself becomes unstable and wants to resolve. So the third colours the chord; the fifth tells you whether it can stand still.

Here are all four on the staff. They share the same root and the same letter names (C–E–G) — only the accidentals on the third and fifth change.

On the keyboard the one-half-step moves are easy to see. In each diagram below the root C never moves; watch only the third and the fifth slide.

Major — C E G

M3 + m3; perfect fifth. Bright and stable.

minor — C E♭ G

The third drops to E♭ (m3 + M3); fifth still perfect. Dark but stable.

diminished — C E♭ G♭

Both thirds minor; the fifth drops to G♭ (a diminished fifth, the tritone). Tense, wants to resolve.

augmented — C E G♯

Both thirds major; the fifth rises to G♯ (an augmented fifth). Tense and restless.

3. Inversions: which note is in the bass

So far the root has been the lowest note. But a triad is the same chord — same three pitch classes, same name — no matter which member you put at the bottom. The lowest sounding note is the bass, and which chord member sits there names the inversion:

  • Root position — the root is in the bass (C–E–G with C lowest).
  • First inversion — the third is in the bass (E–G–C with E lowest).
  • Second inversion — the fifth is in the bass (G–C–E with G lowest).

To invert, move the bottom note up an octave; the remaining notes re-stack above the new bass. It is still a C-major triad each time — only the bass, and so the chord’s flavour and function, change.

Naming an inverted chord you find in the wild

Re-stack the notes into thirds (the “snowman” shape) to find the root. Given G–C–E, the only way they stack in thirds is C–E–G — so the root is C and the chord is C major, here with its fifth (G) in the bass: second inversion. The root is not the lowest note; it is the bottom of the stacked-thirds form.

A quick word on spacing. When all three notes are packed into the smallest space (within an octave, as above) the voicing is close; when they are spread out over more than an octave — common when the chord is shared between two hands or several instruments — the voicing is open. Spacing does not change the chord’s name or inversion, only its sound and feel.

4. Figured bass for triads

Figured bass is a centuries-old shorthand: write the bass note, then stack numbers under or over it for the intervals above the bass that complete the chord. For triads, only three figures matter, one per inversion:

Inversion Bass note Intervals above bass Full figure Usual shorthand
Root position root a 3rd and a 5th 5/3 (none) — left unfigured
First inversion third a 3rd and a 6th 6/3 6
Second inversion fifth a 4th and a 6th 6/4 6/4
Why these particular numbers — and why the abbreviations

The figures are literally the interval sizes measured up from the bass. In root position, the third and fifth sit a 3rd and a 5th above the bass — that is 5/3. Because a plain root-position triad is overwhelmingly the default, players leave it unfigured: no number means “5/3.” In first inversion the third is in the bass, so the other two notes lie a 3rd and a 6th above it — 6/3 — and since the 6 is the telling interval, it is shortened to just 6. In second inversion the fifth is in the bass; the others lie a 4th and a 6th above — 6/4 — and this one keeps both digits, because dropping to a bare “6” would collide with first inversion. So the rule is: omit what is predictable, keep what disambiguates.

You will say these out loud all the time: a “six-three” (or just “six”) chord is in first inversion; a “six-four” chord is in second inversion. We meet figured bass again — combined with Roman numerals — in Lesson 12 (diatonic harmony), and the figures grow a fourth member for seventh chords in Lesson 11.

5. The C major triad on the guitar

The open C chord every guitarist learns first is just a C-major triad — C, E, and G — voiced across the strings. It is in root position because the lowest sounding note (the A string, 3rd fret) is C, the root. Reading the strings high to low: the open high E and the open G string supply the chord’s third (E) and fifth (G); the rest spell C and E.

Open C major (x32010)

Low E (6th string) is muted; A string (5) fret 3 = C, the root (lowest note, so root position); D string (4) fret 2 = E, the third; G string (3) is open = G, the fifth; B string (2) fret 1 = C; high E (1) is open = E. Three pitch classes, C–E–G, doubled across six strings.

The same chord, named on the keyboard

Strip the guitar voicing down to its three distinct notes and you are back at C–E–G — the plain major triad from section 1.
