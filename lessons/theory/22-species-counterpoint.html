Species counterpoint

Counterpoint is the art of combining two or more melodic lines that are each satisfying on their own yet fit together as harmony — independent in shape, interdependent in sound. The 18th-century teacher Johann Joseph Fux laid out a ladder of five graded exercises (the species), climbing from the strictest note-against-note writing up to free melody, all measured against a fixed melody called the cantus firmus. Haydn, Mozart and Beethoven all learned harmony this way. We do too, because nothing else makes the why of voice-leading so concrete.

  • Say what makes two lines genuinely contrapuntal, not just stacked.
  • Name all five species and what each one adds.
  • Write a first-species line: consonances only, contrary motion, no parallels.
  • Build a fourth-species suspension — prepare, suspend, resolve down by step.
  • See the through-line from these exercises to free two-voice counterpoint.

1. What counterpoint actually is

A texture is contrapuntal when each voice is a real melody you could sing alone, and the voices keep their separate identities even as their pitches agree harmonically. The two demands pull against each other: harmony wants the voices to lock together, independence wants them to stay distinct. Every rule below exists to hold that balance — to keep the lines consonant without letting them collapse into one another.

Why a cantus firmus, and why "species"?

Fux's pedagogy fixes one voice — the cantus firmus (Latin for “fixed melody,” abbreviated CF), a plain line of whole notes — so the student has only one thing to invent: the counterpoint against it. He then graded the difficulty into five species by speeding up the counterpoint against the unchanged CF: one note against one, then two, then four, then syncopated, then everything mixed. You add exactly one new problem at a time. It is scaffolding, not a style — but the voice-leading instincts it builds are the ones real composition needs.

2. The five species at a glance

Each species keeps the cantus firmus in whole notes and changes only the rhythm — and freedom — of the counterpoint.

Species Ratio (cpt : CF) What it adds
1st — note-against-note 1 : 1 Consonances only. The grammar of motion and parallels.
2nd 2 : 1 A dissonant passing tone allowed on the weak beat.
3rd 4 : 1 More dissonant figures: passing & neighbour tones, the cambiata.
4th 1 : 1, syncopated The suspension — dissonance on the strong beat.
5th — florid mixed A free blend of the first four; nearly real music.

Sections 3 and 5 below go deep on the two species you must truly own — first (the rule-set everything inherits) and fourth (the suspension, the single most important dissonance in tonal music).

3. First species: note-against-note

One whole note in the counterpoint for every whole note in the CF. With no rhythm to hide behind, every vertical interval is exposed — so first species is purely about which intervals and which motions are allowed. The rules:

  • Consonances only. Every vertical interval must be a consonance: a unison, third, fifth, sixth, or octave (and their compounds). Never a 2nd, 4th, 7th, or tritone between the voices. (On why the 4th counts as a dissonance here, see Lesson 5.)
  • Begin and end on a perfect consonance — a unison, perfect fifth, or octave (P1/P5/P8). These frame the exercise with stability.
  • Approach the final octave/unison by contrary, stepwise motion. One voice falls 2̂–1̂ while the other rises 7̂–8̂ (the leading tone) — the two lines close in on the final like a clausula.
  • No parallel perfect fifths or octaves. Two P5s (or two P8s) in a row, moving the same direction, fuse the voices into one — the cardinal sin (see Lesson 13).
  • Live in the imperfect consonances. Thirds and sixths give fullness without locking the voices together; use them for the body of the line and save perfect intervals for the frame.
  • Approach perfect consonances by contrary or oblique motion, never by similar motion (that is what breeds parallels and direct fifths/octaves).
  • One climax, no crossing. Give the counterpoint a single melodic high point, and keep the upper voice above the lower throughout — don't let them swap places.
Why “no parallel fifths,” in one sentence

A perfect fifth or octave is so blended that when two voices move in parallel fifths the ear stops hearing two lines and hears one line in hollow reinforcement — independence, the whole point of counterpoint, evaporates. Contrary motion is the opposite force: it keeps the lines pulling apart, which is exactly why the rules push you toward it, and require it for the final cadence.

4. Second and third species: dissonance on the move

Speed the counterpoint up and you finally get to use dissonance — but only passing through, on weak beats, never landing.

  • Second species (2:1) puts two counterpoint notes over each CF note. The strong (first) half-note must be consonant; the weak (second) half-note may be a passing tone — a dissonance that fills the gap of a third by stepwise motion (e.g. C–D–E), entered and left by step in the same direction. The dissonance is allowed only because it is in transit.
  • Third species (4:1) puts four counterpoint notes over each CF note, opening the door to more dissonant figures — several passing tones, neighbour tones, and the nota cambiata — still all on weak positions, still all resolved by step.

The grammar of these dissonances — passing tones, neighbour tones and the rest — is the same one catalogued in Lesson 15 (non-chord tones); species counterpoint is simply where it was first codified.

5. Fourth species: syncopation and the suspension

Fourth species keeps the 1:1 pace but syncopates the counterpoint — each note is tied across the barline, sounding on the weak beat and held into the next strong beat. That offset creates the suspension: the most important controlled dissonance in all of tonal music. A suspension has three stages, and the rule is absolute:

  • Preparation — the note arrives on a weak beat as a consonance.
  • Suspension — it is held (tied) into the strong beat, where the other voice has moved on, so it now sounds as a dissonance. The dissonance lands on the accent — that's what makes it ache.
  • Resolution — it falls down by step to a consonance on the following weak beat. If it doesn't resolve down by step, it isn't a suspension.

Above the cantus firmus the usable suspensions are 7–6, 4–3, and 9–8; below it, 2–3. The figures name the dissonant interval and the consonance it resolves to. Here is a chain of 7–6 suspensions — each resolution is itself tied over to become the next suspension:

Reading the figures

A 7–6 means “dissonant 7th on the beat, resolving down to a 6th.” 4–3 and 7–6 are the favourites above the bass because they resolve to imperfect consonances (3rds and 6ths), keeping the texture warm. The 2–3 is the one that works when the suspended voice is below the cantus. You'll meet every one of these again as the bread-and-butter suspensions of four-part writing.

6. Fifth species: florid counterpoint

Fifth species (“florid”) lifts the rhythmic restrictions and lets you blend all four earlier species at will in a single line: a whole note here, running quarters there, a suspension at the cadence, even a pair of passing eighth notes for decoration. The frame still holds — start on a perfect consonance, close with a proper cadence, every dissonance still prepared or passing — but the line is now free to breathe. It is the last rung of the ladder precisely because it looks, finally, like real music.

7. The voice-leading you actually keep

Strip away the scaffolding and a compact set of instincts survives — the same ones that govern free two-voice writing and, scaled up, four-part chorales:

  • Move mostly by step. Leaps are spice; steps are the meal. Recover from a leap by stepping back the other way.
  • Fill the middle with imperfect consonances (3rds, 6ths); reserve perfect intervals for the frame.
  • Approach perfect consonances by contrary or oblique motion. Never slide into a P5 or P8 by similar motion.
  • No parallel or direct 5ths/8ves. They erase voice independence.
  • Give each line a single climax and an arch-like shape; don't let voices cross or pile onto the same note.
  • Treat every dissonance with care — prepare it (suspension) or pass through it (passing/neighbour tone) and resolve by step.
Where this is going

Beyond the exercises lies free two-voice counterpoint: two equal, independent melodies with no fixed cantus firmus, answering and imitating each other. That is the soil from which the invention and the fugue grow — and it's exactly where the next lesson heads, by way of figured bass. The species rules don't disappear in that music; they become second nature, the grammar you no longer have to think about.
