Ear training & a sight-singing routine

Everything in this track has been about seeing music — naming intervals, spelling chords, reading Roman numerals. Ear training closes the loop: it teaches you to hear what you can already name, and to write down what you hear. This is the skill that turns theory from a paper exercise into a working sense — the difference between knowing a dominant resolves and feeling it land. This lesson is the companion to the whole Core: less new notation, more method. It hands you a structured set of skills and a short daily routine to build them, leaning on the same free trainers musicians have used for years.

  • Recognise intervals up and down by ear, anchored with song hooks.
  • Tell triad and seventh-chord qualities apart aurally.
  • Recognise scales and modes from their characteristic colour.
  • Take melodic, harmonic, and rhythmic dictation — hear it, write it.
  • Sight-sing a simple diatonic melody with movable-do solfège.
  • Run a 10–15 min/day routine on the free online trainers.

1. Why ear training, and how it actually works

Ear training is pattern recognition under a clock. You are not developing a mysterious gift — you are building a library of remembered sounds and learning to match an incoming sound to it, fast. Two facts shape every method below.

Relative pitch, not perfect pitch

Almost all practical ear training trains relative pitch: hearing a note or chord in relation to a reference (usually the key’s tonic), not in absolute terms. You do not need perfect pitch (naming an isolated note cold) to be an excellent musician — relative pitch is learnable by anyone at any age, and it is the skill that lets you transpose, improvise, and take dictation. Establish a key centre first (play a I chord or a scale), and every interval and chord becomes a scale-degree relationship you can reason about.

Short and daily beats long and rare

Ten focused minutes every day builds the ear far faster than one weekly marathon. The skill is procedural — like a language — so frequency and recency matter more than total hours in a sitting. The trainers in this lesson are built for exactly this: tiny, endless, self-checking reps.

2. Interval recognition

The first skill, and the one all the others lean on. You already know the intervals as sizes (Lesson 5); now you learn their sounds. Two methods, used together:

  • Song references. Anchor each interval to the opening leap of a tune you already know — the most common beginner method. A few classics, ascending: minor 2nd = “Jaws” theme; Major 2nd = “Happy Birthday”; Major 3rd = “Oh, when the Saints”; Perfect 4th = “Here Comes the Bride” / reveille; Perfect 5th = “Twinkle, Twinkle” (do–sol); Major 6th = “My Bonnie Lies Over the Ocean”; octave = “Somewhere Over the Rainbow.” Pick references that are yours — a hook you can summon instantly works better than a “correct” one you have to dig for.
  • Scale-degree / solfège hearing. The more powerful long-term method: hear the two notes as degrees of a key. In a key, a Perfect 5th from the tonic is do–sol; a Major 3rd is do–mi. This ties interval recognition directly to the solfège from Lesson 9 and generalises to melody.
Why direction changes everything

An ascending minor 6th and a descending minor 6th are the same interval but feel like different sounds, and most song hooks only work in one direction. Train ascending, descending, and harmonic (both notes at once) separately — the trainers let you toggle each. A harmonic interval has no “direction” but blends the two pitches, which makes close intervals (a m2 vs M2) trickier to split apart, so it is worth its own drill.

3. Chord-quality recognition

Next, hear chords as whole objects. Start with the four triad qualities from Lesson 10 and learn their characteristic colour:

Quality Built from Colour to listen for
Major M3 + m3 (root, M3, P5) Bright, stable, “resolved.”
minor m3 + M3 (root, m3, P5) Dark, sad, stable.
diminished m3 + m3 (root, m3, d5) Tense, unstable, “wants to move.”
Augmented M3 + M3 (root, M3, A5) Dreamy, suspended, ambiguous.

The reliable trick is to listen to the third first (major vs minor colour), then check the fifth (perfect = major/minor; diminished or augmented = the outer qualities). Once the four triads are secure, add seventh-chord qualities from Lesson 11 — major 7, dominant 7, minor 7, half-diminished 7, fully-diminished 7. The dominant 7th is worth singling out: its bluesy, restless sound is the engine of tonal harmony, and learning to spot it by ear pays off immediately in harmonic dictation.

Block, then arpeggiate

When a chord stumps you, ask the trainer to play it arpeggiated (one note at a time) so you can hear each interval, then as a block chord to memorise the blended sound. The goal is to recognise the block instantly — the arpeggio is the scaffolding you remove over time.

4. Scale and mode recognition

Hearing a whole scale or mode is recognising the pattern of steps as a single colour. The fastest cues:

  • Major vs minor — the third degree decides it (mi bright vs me dark), exactly as with chords.
  • The three minors — natural, harmonic, and melodic (Lesson 7) differ at degrees 6 and 7; the harmonic minor’s raised 7th gives that exotic Augmented-2nd gap between le and ti.
  • Modes (Lesson 8) — listen for the one altered degree against the parent major or minor: Dorian’s raised 6th (“minor but hopeful”), Mixolydian’s flat 7th (“major but bluesy”), Phrygian’s flat 2nd (“Spanish” darkness), Lydian’s raised 4th (“floating” brightness).

Sing the scale on solfège as you hear it — the syllable that feels “off” against the major template is usually the altered degree that names the mode.

5. Dictation: writing down what you hear

Dictation is the integrated skill — it combines interval, rhythm, and harmony recognition into transcription. It is the single best test of whether your ear and your theory are connected. There are three kinds.

Melodic dictation

Hear a short melody, write it down. A dependable procedure:

  1. Establish the key. Play or sing the tonic and the scale first, so every note has a scale-degree home.
  2. Get the rhythm first, separately. Tap or notate the rhythm before pitches — splitting the two tasks halves the load.
  3. Catch the contour and the anchor notes. Note where the melody starts and ends (usually on a stable degree like do, mi, or sol) and sketch its up/down shape.
  4. Fill in by scale degree. Sing back what you remember on solfège, identify each degree, then translate to pitch.

Harmonic dictation

Hear a chord progression, write the chords as Roman numerals (Lesson 12) and notate the bass line. The key move: track the bass first — it carries the harmonic motion — then listen for the quality of each chord above it (major/minor, and whether a seventh is present). Cadences are the landmarks: an authentic cadence (V–I) and a half cadence (ending on V) anchor the phrase so you can place the chords between them.

Rhythmic dictation

Hear a rhythm, write it. Keep the pulse with your body (tap, conduct), feel the metre (Lesson 3), and notate one beat at a time — group notes into beats rather than transcribing a long undivided stream. Counting syllables (“1 e & a”) turns the sound into a grid you can write.

6. Sight-singing: the other direction

Dictation goes sound → page; sight-singing goes page → sound. It is the most efficient ear-training tool there is, because it forces you to audiate — to hear the note in your head before you produce it. The movable-do routine from Lesson 9, condensed to a workflow:

  1. Find do. Identify the key and locate the tonic; hum it so it is in your ear.
  2. Scan the melody. Glance through for the contour, the leaps, and the cadence — sing the hard intervals silently first.
  3. Audiate, then sing. Hear the phrase internally on solfège, then sing it out with a steady pulse — do not stop to fix a wrong note mid-phrase; keep going and learn the recovery.

Two melodies to practise on, both in C major (so do = C). Sing them slowly on solfège with a steady beat, then check yourself at an instrument. Start with the scale itself — the reference frame for everything else.

Sing it before you play it

The discipline that makes this work: always sing the melody before you check it at an instrument. Singing forces audiation; playing first lets your fingers do the work your ear should be doing. A wrong note you caught by ear teaches more than a right note your hands found for you.

7. A weekly routine (10–15 min/day)

Skills do not transfer just by reading about them — they transfer by daily reps. Here is a compact routine that touches every skill above in a single short session. Cycle it most days; rotate which dictation you do so each gets attention across the week.

Minutes Drill Trainer
~3 Intervals. One set, mixing ascending and descending; note your two worst pairs. teoria ie.php or tonedear Intervals
~3 Chords. Triad qualities; add 7th qualities once triads are secure. teoria c34e.php or musictheory.net Chord
~4 One dictation. Rotate melodic / harmonic / rhythmic day to day. teoria md.php / v4.php / rd4.php
~4 Sight-sing. One short melody on solfège; the two above, then any tune you like. the scores in section 6
How to use the trainers well

The free trainers let you constrain the difficulty — start narrow and widen only as you score consistently high. Begin intervals with just the perfect intervals plus the major/minor 3rds, ascending only; add one new interval at a time. Keep a key centre playing where the trainer offers it (“in context” modes), because relative-pitch hearing is far more reliable than trying to judge raw intervals in a vacuum. Track your accuracy: the point of a session is to find the one thing you keep missing and drill that, not to rack up easy wins.
