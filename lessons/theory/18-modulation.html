Modulation

In Lesson 17 you borrowed the dominant→tonic pull and aimed it at a non-tonic chord, spotlighting it for a beat or two without ever leaving home. That was tonicization. This lesson takes the next, larger step: actually changing key mid-piece. When a new tonic is established and the music stays there — confirmed by its own cadence — you have modulated. Modulation is how a piece travels: it carves long pieces into contrasting tonal regions and creates the sense of journey-and-return that drives almost every sonata, hymn, and pop bridge.

  • State the difference between modulation and tonicization.
  • Name the closely related keys and say why they are the easy targets.
  • Build a common-chord (pivot) modulation step by step.
  • Label a pivot chord with a Roman numeral in both keys.
  • Recognise direct (phrase) and chromatic modulation, and the most common targets.

1. Modulation vs. tonicization — the one distinction

Both ideas point the ear at a new local tonic. The difference is commitment, measured in time and confirmation:

  • Tonicization is a brief visit — a secondary dominant resolves to its target, the target glows as a momentary tonic, and the music slips straight back to the home key. It lasts less than a phrase and is never sealed by a cadence in the new key.
  • Modulation is a move. A new tonic is established and then confirmed by a cadence — ideally a perfect authentic cadence (V–I) in the new key. The music settles there; the old key is, for now, gone.
Why the cadence is the deciding test

A single chromatic chord can fool the ear for a moment, but the ear only believes a key when it hears that key’s own dominant resolve to its own tonic — the same V–I that defines home everywhere (Lessons 16–17). So the practical rule analysts use is blunt: cadences establish keys. No cadence in the new key ⇒ you only tonicized. A real V–I (better, a PAC) in the new key ⇒ you modulated. The fuzzy middle ground — a tonicization stretched long enough to feel like a move — is called an extended tonicization, and reasonable analysts disagree about where to draw the line.

2. The closely related keys — the easy targets

You can modulate anywhere, but most modulations go somewhere close. Two keys are closely related when their key signatures differ by at most one accidental — one step apart on the circle of fifths. Close keys share almost all of their notes, so they share many chords, and shared chords are exactly the hinges a smooth modulation is built on.

For a major key there are five such targets, easiest to remember by Roman numeral: its dominant V, its subdominant IV, its relative minor vi, and the relative minors of IV and V — which land on ii and iii. In C major those keys are G, F, A minor, D minor, and E minor.

For a minor key the closely related set works the same way; its most natural destination is the relative major (III) — A minor goes to C major, the two sharing a key signature. We will use that fact in section 5.

3. Common-chord (pivot) modulation

The cleanest way to change key is to walk across a chord that belongs to both keys at once. Such a chord is a pivot (or common) chord. Because the listener has no reason to suspect anything yet, the pivot is heard at first as part of the old key — and only the chords after it reveal that the frame has quietly shifted. Three steps:

  1. Pick a chord diatonic in both keys. Find a triad that is built from the notes of the old key and the new key. (Between closely related keys there are several to choose from.)
  2. Reinterpret it. The same chord gets a Roman numeral in each key — its old-key meaning and its new-key meaning. This is the hinge.
  3. Cadence in the new key. Right after the pivot, drive a dominant–tonic (ideally a PAC) in the new key to confirm the move.
How to write the pivot label

Analysts stack the two Roman numerals at the pivot: the upper one names the chord in the old key, the lower one in the new key. The strongest pivots read as a pre-dominant in the new key (a ii or IV), because a pre-dominant points naturally toward the V–I that is about to confirm the arrival — the pivot doesn’t just sit in both keys, it already leans toward the new cadence.

Take C major modulating to G major (its dominant). The triad A–C–E is diatonic in both: in C it is vi, in G it is ii. So A minor is our pivot — heard as “six of C,” reheard as “two of G,” and from there a textbook ii–V–I nails down G.

Now the whole modulation as a short progression. The first bar is plainly in C (I–V). The pivot A minor arrives still sounding like C’s vi — then D major (with its F♯, the new leading tone of G) reveals the new key, and the final G major seals it. By the last chord, the ear has fully relocated to G.

Watch the bar math if you write your own: under L:1/4 in 4/4 each bar must total four quarter-units, so a block triad held as a half note is written […]2 (two chords fill a bar) and the final whole-note chord is […]4. An over- or under-full bar is an error the notation engine will not fix for you.

The pivot on the piano

The pivot triad A–C–E — all white keys, equally at home in C major (vi) and G major (ii). Nothing chromatic yet; the hinge hides in plain sight.

The pivot on the guitar

The open A-minor shape (x 0 2 2 1 0): A on the open 5th string, E and A fretted, C on the 2nd string, E on the open 1st. The same chord that pivots C into G.

4. What actually confirms the new key

The pivot opens the door; the cadence walks through it. What convinces the ear that G — not C — is now home is hearing G’s dominant (D, carrying F♯) resolve to G’s tonic. That F♯ is the crux: it is G’s leading tone, the half step that strains up into the new tonic, and it is exactly the note that does not belong to C major. One new leading tone, one V–I that uses it, and the key has changed.

The pitch that makes G major feel like home: F♯ (a black key, absent from C major) pulling up a half step into G. Introduce this leading tone and resolve it in a cadence, and you have left C behind.

5. Other ways to modulate

The pivot is the smoothest route, but not the only one.

Direct (phrase) modulation

Sometimes there is no hinge at all: one phrase ends with a cadence in the old key, and the next phrase simply begins in the new key, with no chord linking them. Because the change is unprepared, the ear feels a sudden lift — this is the “truck-driver” key change of countless pop choruses, and the abrupt new-section gambit of many classical pieces. It is sometimes called phrase or direct modulation.

Chromatic modulation

Here the hinge is itself a chromatic chord — a chord that doesn’t belong to the old key cleanly. A note is altered (chromatically inflected) at the moment of change, and that altered chord steers the ear toward the new tonic. Chromatic modulation is the tool of choice for reaching distant keys, where no ordinary diatonic pivot exists; it leans on the chromatic vocabulary you will keep building in Lessons 19–21.

The most common destinations

If you remember one rule of thumb: major keys most often modulate to their dominant (V), and minor keys most often modulate to their relative major (III). The exposition of a sonata does exactly this — a major-key first theme moves to V for the second theme; a minor-key first theme moves to its relative major. Either way, the new key is only real once its own V–I has confirmed it.
