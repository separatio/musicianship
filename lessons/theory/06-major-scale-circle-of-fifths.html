The major scale, key signatures & the circle of fifths

One pattern of steps — W–W–H–W–W–W–H — generates every major scale, in every key. From that single pattern fall the key signatures (the sharps and flats gathered at the clef) and the circle of fifths (the map that orders all twelve keys). This lesson shows the pattern, why it sounds “major,” why accidentals have to appear when you move it, and how to read any key straight off its signature.

  • Build a major scale anywhere from the W–W–H–W–W–W–H pattern.
  • Explain why G major needs F♯ and F major needs B♭.
  • Recite the order of sharps (F C G D A E B) and the order of flats (its reverse).
  • Name the major key from any key signature in seconds.
  • Use the circle of fifths to find sharps, flats, and relative minors.

1. The major-scale step pattern

A major scale is an ordered set of seven notes — one on each letter name — whose steps follow this exact succession from the tonic up to its octave:

W – W – H – W – W – W – H
(W = whole step / tone, H = half step / semitone)

Play it from C using only white keys and you get the cleanest possible demonstration: C–D–E–F–G–A–B–C. The two half steps fall exactly where the white keys touch with no black key between them — E–F and B–C — and everywhere else the step is a whole step. That is why C major needs no sharps or flats at all: the piano is already laid out in C major.

C major is the seven white keys C through B, numbered as scale degrees 1–7 (the 8th note is C again, an octave up). See how the half steps sit at 3–4 (E–F) and 7–8 (B–C), where two white keys are adjacent with no black key between them.

Two identical tetrachords

There is a tidy symmetry hiding in that pattern. Split the scale into two four-note halves — tetrachords — and each half is built W–W–H, the two joined by one whole step:

Lower tetrachord Join Upper tetrachord
C – D – E – F  (W–W–H) whole step (F–G) G – A – B – C  (W–W–H)
Why this pattern sounds “major”

The bright, settled major colour comes from where the half steps fall. The half step from 7 up to 8 (the leading tone B pulling up to C) gives the scale its strong sense of homecoming on the tonic; the half step from 3 to 4 sits inside a major third above the tonic (C–E is four half steps, the major third you met in Lesson 5). A scale with a major third above its tonic sounds major; move that half step down so the third becomes minor and the whole scale turns dark — that is the next lesson. The tetrachord trick is just a memory aid: learn one W–W–H shape and you already know both halves of every major scale.

2. Building major scales in other keys

The pattern is portable. Start on any note, keep the steps W–W–H–W–W–W–H, use each letter name exactly once, and you have that note's major scale. Because most starting notes do not give the right step sizes on the white keys alone, accidentals appear — they are forced by the pattern, not chosen.

G major needs F♯

Start on G and write the seven letters G–A–B–C–D–E–F–G. Check the steps: G–A W, A–B W, B–C H, C–D W, D–E W — so far so good. But E up to natural F is only a half step, and the pattern needs a whole step in the sixth slot. Raise it to F♯: now E–F♯ is a whole step and F♯–G is the closing half step. G major has exactly one sharp, F♯.

F major needs B♭

Now start on F: F–G–A–B–C–D–E–F. F–G W, G–A W — but A up to natural B is a whole step, and the pattern needs a half step in the third slot (the H that closes the lower tetrachord). Lower it to B♭: A–B♭ is now the half step, and B♭–C reopens the upper tetrachord with a whole step. F major has exactly one flat, B♭.

Sharp keys vs. flat keys

Notice the rule of thumb already forming: moving up a fifth from C to G added a sharp; moving down a fifth from C to F (the same as up a fourth) added a flat. A scale never mixes — a major scale uses either sharps or flats, never both, because each letter name is used once and adjusted in one direction. Section 4 turns this into the full circle.

One-octave major shape (piano)

The 1–3–5–7 of C major picked out — these are the notes a major-seventh chord is built from, and they let you see the major third (1–3) and leading tone (7) that give the scale its colour.

One-octave major shape (guitar)

A one-octave G-major scale shape, root on the low E string (6th string, 3rd fret = G). The same fingering pattern slid to any root gives that root's major scale — the fretboard makes the “portable pattern” idea literal. The 7th degree here is F♯ (4th string, 4th fret).

3. Key signatures

Writing F♯ (or B♭) on every single note would clutter the page. Instead, the sharps or flats common to a key are gathered once, right after the clef, before the time signature — this is the key signature. It silently applies to every note of that letter, in every octave, for the whole piece (until cancelled). A G-major key signature is a single F♯ at the clef; an F-major signature is a single B♭.

The order of sharps and flats

Sharps are always added to a key signature in a fixed order, and so are flats. The order is not arbitrary: each new sharp is a fifth above the last, and each new flat a fifth below — the circle of fifths again.

  • Order of sharps: F – C – G – D – A – E – B. (A classic mnemonic: Father Charles Goes Down And Ends Battle.)
  • Order of flats: the exact reverse — B – E – A – D – G – C – F. (Read the mnemonic backwards: Battle Ends And Down Goes Charles' Father.)

A key with three sharps always has the first three in the sharp order — F♯, C♯, G♯ — never a different selection. The count of accidentals plus this fixed order completely determines the signature.

Reading the key from the signature

Because the order is fixed, you can run it backwards to name the key at a glance:

Two quick rules

Sharps — last sharp is the leading tone. The final (rightmost) sharp in the signature is scale degree 7, a half step below the tonic. So the key is one half step above the last sharp. Signature ends in F♯? Up a half step is G — the key is G major. Ends in C♯? Up a half step is D — D major.

Flats — second-to-last flat names the key. With two or more flats, the second-from-last flat is the tonic. Signature B♭–E♭–A♭? The second-to-last is E♭ — the key is E♭ major. (The lone exception is one flat: that is always F major, worth memorising directly. The equivalent statement: the tonic is a perfect fourth below the last flat.)

4. The circle of fifths

Order the twelve major keys by perfect fifths and they close into a loop — the circle of fifths. It is the single most useful map in tonal music, because the same motion that orders the keys also predicts their key signatures.

  • Go up a fifth (clockwise): add one sharp. C (0) → G (1♯) → D (2♯) → A (3♯) …
  • Go down a fifth / up a fourth (counter-clockwise): add one flat. C (0) → F (1♭) → B♭ (2♭) → E♭ (3♭) …
Why a fifth adds exactly one accidental

Move a major scale up a perfect fifth and the two tetrachords shift: the old upper tetrachord (which was already W–W–H) becomes the new lower one, so six of the seven notes carry over unchanged. Only the new seventh degree — the new leading tone — has to be raised, and that single raise is the one new sharp. Symmetrically, dropping a fifth recycles the old lower tetrachord as the new upper one and lowers exactly one note, the new fourth degree, giving one new flat. The circle is just this one-note-at-a-time bookkeeping made visible.

Relative minors

Every major key shares its key signature with a relative minor — the minor key starting on the major scale's sixth degree (a minor third below the tonic). C major and A minor both have no accidentals; G major and E minor both have one sharp. Same notes, different home base; minor scales get their own lesson next.

Enharmonic keys at the bottom

Keep going around and the sharp side and flat side meet at the bottom of the circle, where the same pitch can be spelled two ways — enharmonic keys. F♯ major (6 sharps) and G♭ major (6 flats) sound identical; so do B major / C♭ major and D♭ major / C♯ major. Which spelling you choose is a matter of convenience (fewer accidentals, or fitting the surrounding music).
