Rhythm & Meter

Pitch tells you which note; rhythm tells you when. This lesson is about time — how music is measured out in a steady pulse, how single notes are made longer or shorter, and how those durations are grouped into bars by a time signature. The one idea to leave with is the split between simple and compound meter: whether the beat divides into two or into three. Get that, and 6/8 stops being a mystery.

  • Hear the beat and name the tempo in beats per minute.
  • Read the duration tree — each value half the one above it — and the matching rests.
  • Beam, dot, and tie durations, and know exactly what each does.
  • Read a time signature: what the top and bottom numbers mean.
  • Tell simple from compound meter, and know why 6/8 has two beats, not six.
  • Count triplets, syncopation, and compound rhythms out loud.

1. Beat, pulse, and tempo

Tap your foot to almost any piece of music and you have found the beat — the steady, recurring pulse that you feel underneath everything else. The beat is the ruler against which every other rhythm is measured. It is usually so regular you can set a clock by it.

How fast that pulse goes is the tempo, measured in beats per minute (BPM). 60 BPM is one beat per second — a slow walk; 120 BPM is a brisk march; 180 BPM is a sprint. A metronome simply clicks the chosen number of beats each minute so you can lock onto the pulse.

Looking ahead

Composers also name tempo with Italian words — Adagio, Allegro, Presto — and shade it with markings like ritardando. Those expression terms belong to Lesson 4 (Score-reading conventions); here we keep tempo as a plain number so all the attention stays on the rhythm.

2. The duration tree: halving all the way down

Western rhythm is built on a single, elegant rule: each note value lasts exactly half as long as the value above it — or, read the other way, every value divides into two of the next smaller one. Open Music Theory states it plainly: “Each note value can be divided into two smaller values.”

  • Whole note (semibreve) — the longest in common use.
  • Half note (minim) — two fill a whole note.
  • Quarter note (crotchet) — two fill a half note; usually the beat.
  • Eighth note (quaver) — two fill a quarter note.
  • Sixteenth note (semiquaver) — two fill an eighth note.

The notation shows the halving visually: an open notehead with no stem (whole), then an open head with a stem (half), a filled head with a stem (quarter), and one extra flag or beam for every further halving (eighth, sixteenth). The ladder below fills one whole-note's worth of time in each bar, sliced ever finer. The pitch never changes — a single repeated note — so your ear tracks only the rhythm.

Rests: silence with the same durations

Silence is measured exactly like sound. Every note value has a matching rest of identical length — a whole rest, half rest, quarter rest, eighth rest, sixteenth rest — so a bar of music is always a mix of sounded durations and silent ones that add up to a full bar.

3. Beaming: grouping to show the beat

Eighth and sixteenth notes can stand alone with little flags, but when several occur in a row they are joined with beams — the thick horizontal bars connecting their stems. Beaming is not decoration: groups are beamed to show where the beats fall, so the eye can read the pulse at a glance. Four eighth notes beamed in two pairs say “two beats, each split in two” far more clearly than four separate flagged notes would. 4. Dotted notes and ties: durations in between

The halving tree gives whole, half, quarter, eighth… but what about three beats, or a beat and a half? Two tools fill the gaps.

A dot written immediately after a notehead adds half of that note’s value to itself. So a dotted half note is a half note (2 beats) plus half of that (1 beat) = 3 beats, the length of three quarter notes. A dotted quarter equals three eighths.

Why does a dot add half?

Because the plain note values can only ever be powers of two — 1, 2, 4, 8 beats and the fractions between — there is no single symbol for a duration of 3. The dot is the fix: it multiplies the note’s length by one-and-a-half (×1.5). teoria.com puts it exactly: “A dotted note contains that note’s rhythmic value plus half of that note’s rhythmic value” — so the dotted half “equals three quarter notes.” This is the same dotted note that will become the beat in compound meter (section 6), so it is worth fixing now: dot = the note again, plus half of it.

A tie is a curved line joining two notes of the same pitch into one unbroken sound: you play the first and hold through the second without re-striking it, adding their durations together. Ties do what dots cannot — they carry a duration across a barline, letting a note sustain from one bar into the next. (A dotted half and a half-tied-to-a-quarter sound identical; the tie is needed only when the duration must cross a bar.)

5. Time signatures: organising beats into bars

The steady pulse is grouped into equal measures called bars, marked off by barlines. A time signature — the two stacked numbers at the start of the music — tells you how each bar is filled. Read it as two separate instructions:

  • Top number — how many beats are in each bar.
  • Bottom number — which note value gets one beat. It is a code: 4 = the quarter note, 2 = the half note, 8 = the eighth note.

So 4/4 is “four quarter-note beats per bar” (so common it is also called common time); 3/4 is “three quarter-note beats per bar” — the waltz; 2/4 is “two quarter-note beats per bar” — the march. The bottom number is the same in all three, so the beat feels the same; only the number of beats per bar — and therefore where the strong downbeat lands — changes.

6. Simple vs. compound meter — the key idea

Everything so far has assumed each beat splits into two even halves. That is simple meter: Open Music Theory defines it as “a meter in which the beat divides into two, and then further subdivides into four.” 4/4, 3/4, and 2/4 are all simple — clap any of their quarter-note beats and you can split each cleanly into “1-and, 2-and.”

But beats can also divide into three. That is compound meter: “meters in which the beat divides into three and then further subdivides into six.” This is the part that trips everyone up, so go slowly.

Why 6/8 has two beats, not six

Read 6/8 literally and you get “six eighth-notes per bar.” That is true — but those six eighths are not six beats. They group into two beats of three eighths each, and the note that lasts three eighths is the dotted quarter (recall section 4: a dotted quarter = three eighths). So the beat in 6/8 is a dotted quarter, and there are two of them: 6/8 is compound duple. teoria.com confirms it: “This meter has two beats; the note value with the value of one beat is the dotted quarter note… which can be divided into three eighth notes.”

This is the deep reason the beat in compound meter is always a dotted note: only a dotted value can be split into three. A plain note splits into two (simple); add the dot and it splits into three (compound). To find the number of beats in a compound signature, divide the top number by 3: 6/8 → 2 beats, 9/8 → 3 beats (compound triple), 12/8 → 4 beats (compound quadruple).

7. Tuplets: borrowing a different division

What if you want three even notes inside a beat that normally splits into two? You borrow the compound division for one beat with a tuplet. The most common is the triplet: three notes played in the time normally taken by two of the same value. Open Music Theory: “A triplet is a type of tuplet in which a beat … in simple meter is divided into three parts.” A quarter-note triplet squeezes three quarter notes into the space of two; each triplet note therefore lasts two-thirds of a normal quarter. A small 3 over the beamed group marks it.

8. Syncopation: accenting the off-beat

Normally the strong accents fall on the beat — beat 1 strongest, then the other beats. Syncopation deliberately upsets that: it places stress where you would not expect it, on weak beats or between the beats. Open Music Theory: “Syncopation happens when there are off-beat rhythmic accents, which can be created by ties, dots, rests, and/or dynamics.” It is the engine of groove in jazz, funk, Latin music, and most pop.

In the example below, the long notes land off the main beats: a short note on beat 1, then notes that sustain across beats 2 and 3, throwing the accent onto the “and” — the unmistakable lean of syncopation.

9. Counting out loud

The fastest way to internalise rhythm is to count it aloud while you clap or play. Three counting systems cover almost everything:

  • Eighths in simple meter: “1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &” — the numbers are the beats, the “and”s are the half-way splits.
  • Sixteenths in simple meter: “1 e & a 2 e & a” — four even syllables per beat.
  • Compound meter (6/8): “1 & a 2 & a” — three syllables per beat, because the beat divides into three.
