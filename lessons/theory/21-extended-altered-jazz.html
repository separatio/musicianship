Extended & altered chords, and jazz harmony

A seventh chord stops at four notes. Keep stacking thirds and the chord climbs into a second octave — the 9th, 11th and 13th. These extensions are what give jazz, gospel, neo-soul and film harmony their lush, restless colour. This lesson shows how the stack is built, which upper notes are available and which to avoid, how a dominant chord can have its extensions altered for maximum tension, how to read the shorthand on a lead sheet, and how it all comes together in the most important progression in jazz — the ii–V–I. It builds straight on the seventh chords of Lesson 11; we will not re-derive those here.

  • Stack thirds past the seventh to reach the 9th, 11th and 13th.
  • Spell and name Cmaj9, C9, Cm9, C11, C13 — and know which extension each implies.
  • Tell an available tension from an avoid tone (why the natural 11 needs to become #11).
  • Recognise an altered dominant (b9, #9, #11, b13) and the one scale that supplies all four.
  • Read lead-sheet symbols: maj7/Δ, m7, 7, m7♭5/ø, °7, sus, slash chords.
  • Hear a jazz ii–V–I and the chromatic shortcut of tritone substitution.

1. Past the seventh: 9ths, 11ths and 13ths

Triads and seventh chords are built in thirds (tertian harmony). Don't stop at the seventh — keep adding thirds and you climb into the octave above: the next third lands on the 9th, the one after on the 11th, the last on the 13th. Those three are the extensions (also called upper structures or tensions). The 13th is as far as the stack goes — by then all seven letter names of the scale are present.

The names 9, 11 and 13 are just compound intervals from Lesson 7: a 9th is a 2nd plus an octave, an 11th a 4th plus an octave, a 13th a 6th plus an octave. We name them with the big numbers because the chord literally reaches up that high — and because writing “add the 9th” signals an upper-structure colour, not a close-position cluster.

Why the names jump by twos

A third above the 7 is the 9, a third above the 9 is the 11, a third above the 11 is the 13 — odd numbers, because thirds skip a letter each time (C→E→G→B→D→F→A). The even numbers (10, 12) never appear as chord extensions: a 10th is just the 3rd an octave up, a 12th the 5th an octave up — already in the chord, nothing new to name.

In practice a 13th chord is rarely voiced with all seven notes — players drop the 5th (and often the 11th) and keep the guide tones (3rd and 7th) plus whichever extension gives the colour. The name still tells you the chord reaches that high, even when a note or two is left out.

2. Chord qualities and lead-sheet shorthand

The extension number rides on top of a chord quality taken from Lesson 11. The shorthand that does this is the chord symbol (or lead-sheet symbol), so called because it is what you read off a lead sheet — a jazz score giving only melody and chord symbols. A bare letter means a major triad; everything else is a modification of that default.

The five most common extended chords from C, spelled out:

Symbol Quality Notes from C What the extension implies
Cmaj9 (CΔ9) major 7th + 9 C E G B D major 7th (B) and natural 9th (D)
C9 dominant 7th + 9 C E G B♭ D flat 7th (B♭) + natural 9th — a dominant
Cm9 minor 7th + 9 C E♭ G B♭ D minor 3rd & 7th, natural 9th
C11 dominant + 11 C (E) G B♭ D F reaches the 11th; the 3rd is usually dropped (see §3)
C13 dominant + 13 C E G B♭ D (F) A reaches the 13th (A); 5th and often 11th omitted
Reading the shorthand

The number alone (9, 11, 13) on a plain letter means a dominant chord up to that extension — C13 is a dominant chord, not a major one. To get a major-7th colour you must write maj (or Δ): Cmaj9. To get minor, write m (or –): Cm9. So “maj/m” sets the 3rd-and-7th quality; the number sets how high the stack reaches.

On the piano

Cmaj9 laid out left to right: root C, major 3rd E, 5th G, major 7th B, and the 9th D up in the next octave.

On the guitar

A compact Cmaj9 voicing: C (5th string, 3rd fret), E (4th string, 2nd fret), B (3rd string, 4th fret), D (2nd string, 3rd fret). The 5th is dropped — common practice once the chord gets this tall.

C13 on the keyboard: the dominant core C–E–G–B♭, then the 9th D and the 13th A reaching into the upper octave. Hear how the B♭–E tritone inside still wants to resolve — extensions colour the chord but don't cancel its pull.

3. Available tones vs. avoid tones — and the #11

In theory every extension exists over every chord. In practice only some sound good; the usable ones are available tensions, and the ones that fight the chord are avoid tones. The usual culprit: a note that sits a minor 9th (an octave plus a half step) above a chord tone. That harsh rub is the thing to avoid.

The textbook case is the natural 11th over a major or dominant chord. On C, the 11th is F — and F sits a half step above the chord's major 3rd, E. Stacked an octave up, that's a minor 9th between E and F: muddy and clashing. So the natural 11 is an avoid tone over major and dominant chords, which is exactly why C13 above is voiced without its F.

The fix: raise it to #11

Sharpen the 11th and the problem evaporates. F♯ (the #11 of C) sits a whole tone above the 3rd E, not a half step, so the minor-9th rub is gone. The #11 also happens to be the colourful note of the Lydian mode, which is why Cmaj7♯11 has that bright, modern, film-score glow. Over a minor chord the natural 11 is fine (there's no major 3rd for it to clash with), so Cm11 is a perfectly happy chord.

A working rule of thumb for which tensions are available:

Chord type Available extensions Avoid
major 7th 9, #11, 13 natural 11 (clashes with 3rd)
dominant 7th 9, #11, 13 (plus the altered set — §4) natural 11 (clashes with 3rd)
minor 7th 9, 11, 13 natural 13 is touchy on ii; 9 & 11 are safe

4. Altered dominants

A dominant 7th already leans home (its inner tritone, Lesson 11). You can crank that tension to the maximum by chromatically altering its extensions — bending the 9th and 5th up or down. The four classic alterations, on G7:

  • ♭9 — G7♭9 adds A♭ (a half step above the root area), darker and more urgent.
  • ♯9 — G7♯9 adds A♯ (the “Hendrix chord” bite, a major 3rd and a minor-3rd colour at once).
  • ♯11 — same pitch as ♭5: raise/replace the 5th (D→C♯/D♭).
  • ♭13 — same pitch as ♯5: lower/raise the 5th the other way (D→E♭/D♯).

Pile all four onto one chord and the lead sheet just writes G7alt (“G seven altered”): play a dominant with its 9th and 5th fully altered, your choice of which alterations to voice. It is the sound of pure tension a heartbeat before the resolution.

Where all the alterations come from at once

There is one scale that contains the root, 3rd, ♭7 and all four altered tones (♭9, ♯9, ♯11, ♭13) and nothing else: the altered scale (also super-Locrian). It is simply the 7th mode of melodic minor — i.e. play the melodic-minor scale starting a half step above the chord's root. For G7alt that's A♭ melodic minor. One scale, every alteration; that's why jazz players reach for it over any “alt” chord.

G7♭9 on the keyboard: the dominant G–B–D–F, then the ♭9 (A♭) just above. That A♭ leans down hard toward G — the engine of the resolution in §6.

5. Reading lead-sheet chord symbols

Chord symbols grew up piecemeal as jazz developed and were never fully standardised, so the same chord can be written several ways. The essentials you must read fluently:

Symbol Name Notes from C
C major triad (the default) C E G
Cmaj7, CΔ, CΔ7 major seventh C E G B
Cm7, C–7, Cmin7 minor seventh C E♭ G B♭
C7 dominant seventh C E G B♭
Cm7♭5, Cø, Cø7 half-diminished seventh C E♭ G♭ B♭
C°7, Cdim7 fully diminished seventh C E♭ G♭ B♭♭(A)
Csus4 (or Csus) suspended 4th (3rd replaced by 4th) C F G
Csus2 suspended 2nd (3rd replaced by 2nd) C D G
C/E slash chord: C major over E in the bass E (bass) + C E G
Decoding any symbol, left to right

Read it in order: root (the letter, with any accidental) → quality (m / maj / ø / ° / sus, defaulting to major) → extension (7, 9, 11, 13) → alterations in brackets (♭9, ♯11…) → bass note after a slash. So F♯m7♭5/A = an F♯ half-diminished seventh with A in the bass. The Δ (triangle) always means major-7th; the ø (slashed circle) means half-diminished; the ° (plain circle) means fully diminished.

A slash chord (X/Y) just puts note Y in the bass — often an inversion of X (Lesson 11's inversions, now in shorthand), sometimes a genuinely different bass note for a pedal or a smooth bass line. The chord above the slash is unchanged; only the lowest sounding note is specified.

6. The jazz ii–V–I

The single most important progression in jazz is ii–V–I — in C major, Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7. It is the harmonic-progression logic of Lesson 16 (roots falling by fifth: D→G→C) with sevenths added. The voice-leading is wonderfully smooth: the guide tones (3rds and 7ths) of each chord slide by step or stay put, and the V’s tritone resolves into the tonic.

Now dress it with extensions — the jazz default is closer to Dm9–G13–Cmaj9 (or G7♭9 for extra bite). The skeleton is identical; the upper notes add colour and the voice-leading still drives home. Reaching for those extensions is exactly the §1–§4 material in its natural habitat.

Tritone substitution — one chromatic shortcut

The V can be swapped for the dominant a tritone away: in C, D♭7 replaces G7. It works because both chords share the same tritone — G7's 3rd–♭7 are B–F, and D♭7's are F–C♭(=B): the same two pitches, just swapping roles. So the part that pulls home is unchanged, but the root now slides chromatically D♭→C into the tonic. That's the silky Dm7–D♭7–Cmaj7 you hear all over jazz.
