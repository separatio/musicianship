Pitch & the chromatic system

Everything in this course is built from pitch — how high or low a note sounds. Western music carves the whole range of pitch into just twelve repeating steps, and once you can see those twelve on a keyboard and a fretboard, the rest of theory is mostly learning which of them to pick and why. This first lesson lays that foundation: what a pitch is, the twelve of them, and the two names we give them.

  • Explain what pitch is — frequency heard as highness or lowness.
  • Name the twelve chromatic pitches and the half step that separates each.
  • Read the piano keyboard as the clearest map of the twelve.
  • Use sharps, flats, and naturals — and know why one key has two names.
  • Understand octaves, octave equivalence, and scientific pitch notation (C4, A4 = 440 Hz).

1. What pitch is

When something vibrates — a string, a column of air, a vocal fold — it pushes the air around it back and forth at some number of cycles per second. That rate is its frequency, measured in hertz (Hz). Your ear hears a faster frequency as a higher pitch and a slower one as lower. Pluck a guitar string, then press it at the 12th fret and pluck again: the second note vibrates exactly twice as fast and sounds higher. That is pitch — frequency, heard.

A pitch proper needs a frequency steady and clear enough to sing back. A regular, periodic vibration gives a definite pitch; an irregular, non-periodic one gives noise — a cymbal crash, a snare hit, static. Noise has energy spread messily across many frequencies at once, so there is no single “note” to name. This course is about the pitched world: notes you can name, tune, and stack.

Why “higher” and “lower” at all?

The up/down metaphor is just a convention — nothing physically rises. We map faster frequencies to “high” because that is how Western notation, keyboards, and language settled centuries ago. What matters is the relationship: double the frequency and the pitch climbs by a fixed, recognisable amount (an octave, section 5), and that doubling is the same wherever you are in the range. Pitch is ratio, not absolute distance — a fact that quietly runs under everything in this course.

2. Twelve pitches, repeating

Western music does not use every possible frequency. It picks twelve pitches per octave, equally spaced, and then repeats that same set of twelve higher and lower across the whole range. These twelve are the chromatic pitches (“chromatic” means “coloured” — all the colours available). Climb through all twelve in order and you have played the chromatic scale; the thirteenth note is the same as the first, one octave up.

The distance from any one of the twelve to its neighbour is the smallest step the system has: a half step, also called a semitone. As Open Music Theory puts it, a half step is “the smallest interval, or distance between two notes, in Western musical notation.” Two half steps stacked make a whole step (a tone) — “the equivalent of two half steps.” That is the entire vocabulary of distance you need to start: half steps and whole steps.

Why exactly twelve?

Twelve is the smallest division of the octave that gives us good-sounding approximations of the simple frequency ratios the ear loves — the 3:2 of a fifth, the 4:3 of a fourth, the 5:4 of a third. Slice the octave into twelve equal half steps (modern equal temperament) and those consonances land close enough to please us while letting every key sound the same. Other cultures chose differently; twelve is the bargain Western music struck. You do not need the math yet — just trust that the twelve are not arbitrary, and that the half step is their common unit. The interval ratios behind all this are unpacked far later, in Lesson 5 (Intervals).

3. The keyboard: the clearest map of the twelve

Nothing shows the twelve pitches as plainly as a piano keyboard. One octave has seven white keys (named with the seven letters A–G) and five black keys tucked between some of them — seven plus five is our twelve. The black keys come in a repeating group of two then three, which is how a pianist finds their place without looking: the white key just left of the two-black-key group is always C.

Here is one octave with every one of the twelve labelled (the black keys are shown by their sharp name for now — more on their second name in section 4):

One octave, all twelve chromatic pitches. Seven white keys (C D E F G A B) and five black keys between them.

Look closely and you will spot two gaps: there is no black key between E and F, and none between B and C. Beginners assume every white pair has a black key wedged in — these two pairs break that pattern, and there is a clean reason.

Why no black key between E–F and B–C?

Because a black key exists only to sit a half step between two white keys that are a whole step apart. But E–F and B–C are already a half step apart — they are adjacent chromatic pitches with nothing between them to need a key. Open Music Theory states it directly: “Two pairs of white keys—E/F and B/C—do not have black keys in between them. This is because E–F and B–C are both half steps.” Every other white-key pair (C–D, D–E, F–G, G–A, A–B) is a whole step, so each gets a black key in the middle. The keyboard’s irregular look is really a faithful picture of where the half steps fall.

Half step vs. whole step, side by side

A half step moves to the very next key — black or white, no key skipped. A whole step skips exactly one key. The two diagrams below make the difference visible: C to C♯ (the black key immediately to the right) is a half step; C to D (skipping that black key) is a whole step.

4. Sharps, flats, naturals — and two names for one key

The five black keys (and, when needed, the white keys too) are named by altering a letter with an accidental. As musictheory.net and Open Music Theory both define them:

  • A sharp (♯) raises a note by a half step. The black key just above C is C♯.
  • A flat (♭) lowers a note by a half step. The same black key, approached from above, is D♭.
  • A natural (♮) cancels a previous sharp or flat, returning the note to its plain letter.

So the black key between C and D has two correct names: C♯ (C raised a half step) and D♭ (D lowered a half step). Two names, one key, one sound. Pitches that sound identical but are spelled differently are called enharmonic equivalents.

Why does one key get two names?

Because a note’s name carries its job, not just its sound. Spelling tells you which letter the note grew from and where it is heading — whether that black key is a raised C climbing toward D, or a lowered D leaning back toward C. The two readings sound the same on a piano but mean different things in a key or a chord. Open Music Theory: “two written notes have enharmonic equivalence if they produce the same pitch but are notated differently,” and the choice “depends on its role in harmony.” You will see why this spelling carries real musical weight once intervals arrive — that is Lesson 5’s job, not this one’s. For now: same key, two names, and the name you pick has a reason.

5. Octaves & octave equivalence

Play C, then play the C twelve half steps higher. They sound astonishingly alike — like the same note, just higher. That “same note” feeling is octave equivalence, and it has a precise physical cause: the higher C vibrates at exactly twice the frequency of the lower one. An octave is a 2:1 frequency ratio — Wikipedia defines it as “an interval between two notes, one having twice the frequency of vibration of the other.”

Because of that simple doubling, the ear fuses the two so tightly that it treats them as the same pitch class: “the human ear tends to hear both notes as being essentially ‘the same’.” This is why a man and a woman asked to sing the “same” note will naturally sing an octave apart and call it unison. It is also why we reuse the same seven letters over and over up the keyboard — every C is a C.

6. Naming the octaves: scientific pitch notation

If every C is called “C,” how do we say which C? Scientific pitch notation (SPN) attaches a number to each letter to fix its octave. The reference point is middle C = C4 — the C nearest the middle of the piano, on the line between the two staves of the grand staff. From there the numbers count up and down: C5 is the octave above, C3 the octave below.

The course’s tuning anchor lives in this system too: A4 = 440 Hz, the A just above middle C, is the modern international concert-pitch standard (ISO 16). Every other pitch is tuned in relation to it.

The one tricky rule of SPN naming

The octave number changes at C, not at A. Counting up, the number ticks over “upon an ascension from B to C” — so the note just below C4 is B3, and C4 starts a fresh octave. That means A4 and B4 sit above middle C (C4), while A3 and B3 sit just below it. Mnemonic: each numbered octave runs C up to B, then the next C bumps the number. Don’t let the letters A and B fool you into thinking they start the octave — C does.

7. The same twelve on the guitar

The keyboard separates the twelve in space; the guitar stacks them along a single string. Pluck any open string, then move up one fret at a time — each fret is one half step. Climb twelve frets and you reach the same note an octave higher, which is why the 12th fret has a double dot and often a harmonic marker: it is the octave of the open string. The chromatic scale on one string is just “every fret, in order.”

The low E string (string 6), played open then up one fret at a time. Twelve half steps from open to the 12th fret, where the note returns an octave higher — the chromatic scale on a single string.
