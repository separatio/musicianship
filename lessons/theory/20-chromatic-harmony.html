Chromatic harmony: the Neapolitan & augmented-sixth chords

So far chromaticism has meant borrowing — a secondary dominant tonicising a scale degree, a flat-six chord borrowed from the parallel minor. This lesson adds two of the most striking chromatic predominants in the common-practice vocabulary: the Neapolitan sixth and the augmented-sixth chords. Both sit in the same slot — predominant, the chord that prepares the dominant — but they colour that slot with notes from outside the key, and both resolve to V with a voice-leading logic so strong it almost plays itself.

  • Build a Neapolitan sixth (♭II6) on the lowered second degree and explain why it lives in first inversion.
  • Voice-lead ♭II6 to V — directly, or through a cadential 6/4.
  • Build the three augmented-sixth chords (Italian, French, German) around the ♭6–♯4 augmented sixth.
  • Resolve that augmented sixth outward to the octave on the dominant — and dodge the German's parallel fifths.
  • Hear why both chords feel like a held breath before the cadence.

1. The predominant slot, painted chromatic

A standard functional phrase runs tonic → predominant → dominant → tonic (T–PD–D–T). The predominant is the chord that builds tension toward the dominant — diatonically that is ii or IV. Everything in this lesson is a chromatic substitute for that predominant: a richer, more loaded way to lean into V. Because they share a function, they share a destination — both the Neapolitan and every augmented-sixth chord want to resolve to V. The chromatic notes are not decoration; they sharpen the half-step pull into the dominant.

Why these belong together

The Neapolitan and the augmented sixths are usually taught as one unit because they answer the same question — how do I make the approach to V more intense than plain ii or IV? — with the same tool: chromatically altered scale degrees that resolve by half step. The Neapolitan flattens scale degree 2; the augmented sixths raise scale degree 4 and lower scale degree 6 so they squeeze in on the dominant from both sides. Same slot, same cadential goal, different chromatic flavour.

2. The Neapolitan sixth (♭II6)

The Neapolitan is a major triad built on the lowered second scale degree (♭2, solfège ra). In C major or minor, ♭2 is D♭, so the chord is D♭ major: D♭–F–A♭. It almost always appears in first inversion — with the third, F (scale degree 4), in the bass — which is exactly why it is called the Neapolitan sixth: in first inversion the chord shows the figured-bass interval of a sixth above the bass. Spelled from the bass up in C, that is F–A♭–D♭.

Why first inversion?

Put the root D♭ in the bass and you get a heavy, root-position chord a half step above the tonic — clumsy, and it fights the tonic. In first inversion the bass is F = scale degree 4, the very note IV and ii6 already put in the bass. So ♭II6 slides into the predominant slot sounding like a chromatically darkened ii6: same bass behaviour, same function, but with that haunting ♭2 floating on top. The first inversion is what lets a wildly chromatic chord behave like a familiar predominant.

The colour comes entirely from ♭2. Compare the diatonic ii6 in C minor (D–F–A♭, bass F) with ♭II6 (D♭–F–A♭, bass F): only the top note changes, D to D♭, and the whole chord turns from ordinary to shadowed. The Neapolitan is at home in minor (where ♭6 = A♭ is already in the key) but is freely borrowed into major too.

The Neapolitan in C, voiced as it sits in first inversion: F (scale degree 4) in the bass, A♭ above it, and the characteristic D♭ (♭2) on top. Together they spell a D♭-major triad.

Resolving ♭II6 to V

The Neapolitan is a terminal predominant: its normal move is straight to the dominant. The pull comes from ♭2. When ♭II6 goes directly to V, the ♭2 (D♭) falls a chromatic step down to the leading tone (B♮, scale degree 7), not up to tonic — the leading tone is V's tendency tone, and ♭2 is poised right above it. The bass scale degree 4 (F) moves to the root of V (G or a low G), and ♭6 (A♭) descends to 5 (G).

The diminished-third detour — and the cadential 6/4 fix

Going ♭II6 → V directly creates an awkward melodic leap in one voice: ♭2 down to 7 is a diminished third (D♭ down to B in C), the signature sound of this progression and entirely permitted. If you would rather avoid it, insert a cadential 6/4 between them — ♭II6 → I 6/4 → V. The 6/4's tonic note lets ♭2 pass through 1 on its way down, smoothing the line. You met the cadential 6/4 in Lesson 14; here it doubles as a voice-leading lubricant.

3. The augmented-sixth chords

The augmented-sixth chords are a small family of chromatic predominants defined not by a root but by a single interval: the augmented sixth itself, sitting between the lowered sixth scale degree (♭6) in the bass and the raised fourth scale degree (♯4) above it. In C, ♭6 = A♭ and ♯4 = F♯, and A♭ up to F♯ is an augmented sixth (ten half steps — the very same width as the minor seventh it sounds identical to, but spelled as a sixth because A and F are six letters apart). Both of those notes are tendency tones aimed at G, the dominant root: A♭ wants to fall to G, F♯ wants to rise to G.

Why it must be an augmented sixth, not a minor seventh

A♭ and F♯ sound exactly like A♭ and G♭ — a minor seventh — on the piano. But spelling decides function (recall Lesson 5). An augmented sixth expands outward: the two notes are at the edges of the chord and pull apart by half step until they meet on the octave. A♭ falls to G, F♯ rises to G, and the chord opens onto a G octave — the dominant, doubled. Spell it as a minor seventh and the ear (and the notation) expects the opposite — a contraction inward — which is the wrong story entirely. The augmented sixth is the whole point of the chord.

The augmented sixth in C: A♭ (♭6) at the bottom and F♯ (♯4) at the top, framing the shared tonic C. These two outer notes are the engine — they will pull apart to a G octave. (Shown here as the Italian sixth, which adds only the tonic between them.)

The three national types

All three augmented-sixth chords share the same two essential notes — ♭6 and ♯4 — plus the tonic. They differ only in one added note, and tradition names each after a European nationality (the labels are historical convention, not analysis — theorists have never agreed why). In C major/minor:

Type Symbol Scale degrees Notes in C Added note
Italian sixth It+6 ♭6 · 1 · ♯4 A♭ · C · F♯ — (tonic 1 doubled)
French sixth Fr+6 ♭6 · 1 · 2 · ♯4 A♭ · C · D · F♯ scale degree 2 (D)
German sixth Gr+6 ♭6 · 1 · ♭3 · ♯4 A♭ · C · E♭ · F♯ ♭3 (E♭)

The Italian is the lean three-note core (and so it doubles the tonic to fill out four voices). The French adds scale degree 2, giving it a brighter, whole-tone tinge. The German adds ♭3 — which makes it sound, all by itself, exactly like a dominant seventh chord (A♭–C–E♭–F♯ is enharmonically A♭7), a resemblance that makes it a favourite pivot for distant modulation.

Resolving the augmented sixth outward to V

However the chord is filled in, the resolution is the same: the outer ♭6 and ♯4 expand outward by half step to the octave G — the dominant. The diagram below shows that motion; the score after it writes out an Italian sixth resolving to V.

The outward resolution. A♭ (♭6) slides down a half step to G; F♯ (♯4) slides up a half step to G; the augmented sixth opens into a G octave — the root of the dominant, doubled top and bottom.

Why the German needs a cadential 6/4

Resolve a German sixth (A♭–C–E♭–F♯) straight to a root-position V and the E♭ falling to D, set against the bass A♭ falling to G, produces parallel perfect fifths (A♭–E♭ moving to G–D) — forbidden in common-practice voice leading. The standard fix is to resolve Gr+6 → cadential 6/4 → V instead: the 6/4 inserts a tonic chord over the dominant bass, so E♭ resolves to D across the 6/4 rather than in parallel with the bass, and the fifths vanish. This is the same cadential 6/4 that smooths the Neapolitan — one device, two chromatic chords saved.

4. Two chromatic predominants, side by side

It is worth holding the two ideas together, because they are mirror strategies for the same goal. The Neapolitan leans on V from above with a single flattened note (♭2 → 7). The augmented sixths squeeze V from both sides with two altered notes pulling apart (♭6 → 5 and ♯4 → 5).

Neapolitan sixth (♭II6) Augmented sixth (It / Fr / Gr)
Function Chromatic predominant Chromatic predominant
Built on / around Major triad on ♭2 The ♭6–♯4 augmented sixth
Usual bass Scale degree 4 (first inversion) Scale degree ♭6
Key chromatic note(s) ♭2 ♭6 and ♯4
Resolves to V (♭2 → 7) V (♭6 → 5, ♯4 → 5: outward)
Cadential 6/4? Optional — smooths the dim. 3rd Required for the German (parallel 5ths)

Both are at home in minor and freely borrowed into major; both turn an ordinary approach to the cadence into a moment of heightened, almost operatic tension. Listen for them at the big cadences in Schubert, Chopin and Beethoven — the harmonic equivalent of a held breath before the dominant lands.
