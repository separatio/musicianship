Non-chord tones

A non-chord tone (NCT, also non-harmonic or embellishing tone) is a melody note that is not a member of the chord sounding underneath it. For a moment it clashes with the harmony, then it moves on. That tiny clash-and-release is where most of the life in a melodic line comes from: chord tones give you the skeleton, non-chord tones give you the muscle and motion. This lesson teaches the standard catalogue and — more usefully — the one question that classifies every one of them.

  • Classify any NCT by how it is approached and how it is left.
  • Tell passing tones, neighbors, suspensions, appoggiaturas, escape tones, anticipations, retardations, and pedal points apart.
  • Read the suspension figures (4–3, 7–6, 9–8, bass 2–3) and the prep–suspension–resolution shape.
  • Know which NCTs are accented (on the beat) and which are unaccented.

1. One question classifies them all

Every non-chord tone is the middle of a three-note gesture: a chord tone, then the NCT, then another chord tone. So you only ever need two facts to name it — how you arrive at the NCT and how you leave it — each being either by step (a 2nd) or by leap (a 3rd or more). One extra detail, accented (on a strong beat) vs. unaccented (on a weak beat), finishes the picture.

Why approach & departure, not the interval it forms?

You could try to label NCTs by the dissonance they make with the bass, but the same dissonance (say, a 4th over the root) shows up in a suspension, an appoggiatura, and a passing tone. What actually distinguishes them — what your ear hears as different — is the motion: did the note slip in by step or jump in by leap, and did it resolve by stepping on, stepping back, or leaping away? Motion is the signature. The table below is the whole lesson in one grid.

Notice how the families pair up: a passing tone and a neighbor are both step-in/step-out (they differ only in direction out); an appoggiatura and an escape tone are mirror images (leap-in/step-out vs. step-in/leap-out); a suspension and a retardation are the same shape resolving opposite ways. Learn one of each pair and the partner comes free.

2. Passing tone — step in, step on (fills a third)

A passing tone fills the gap between two chord tones a third apart by stepping straight through the note in between, moving in the same direction throughout. Below, the bass holds a C major triad while the upper voice walks C → D → E. The middle note, D, is not in C major (C-E-G) — it is the passing tone, sounded on a weak beat and gone before it can settle.

The same motion on the keyboard: C and E are chord tones (the root and third of C major); the D wedged between them is the passing tone.

And on the guitar

C (A string, 3rd fret) walking up to E (7th fret) through D (5th fret) — the same passing-tone third, now along one string.

3. Neighbor tone — step away and step back

A neighbor tone decorates a single chord tone: step away from it and immediately step back to the same note. Step up and back for an upper neighbor, step down and back for a lower neighbor. Here the chord tone E (the third of C major) blooms up to its upper neighbor F and returns. The defining feature versus a passing tone is the change of direction: a neighbor comes home.

4. Suspension — the classic accented dissonance

A suspension is the most important non-chord tone in common-practice harmony, because it is the engine of expressive tension on strong beats. A chord tone is held (or re-struck) into the next chord, where it no longer fits — it is now a dissonance on an accented beat — and then it resolves down by step to the chord tone it was blocking. Every suspension has three parts:

  • Preparation — the note is a consonant chord tone in the first chord.
  • Suspension — the harmony changes; the held note is now a dissonance, and it falls on the strong beat (this is the accented clash).
  • Resolution — the held note steps down to a chord tone of the new chord.

Suspensions are named by the intervals (above the bass) of the dissonance and its resolution. The most common are the 4–3 (a 4th resolving to a 3rd), 7–6, and 9–8; in the bass voice the figure is reversed as a 2–3 suspension. The score below is the textbook 4–3. The soprano C is prepared as a consonance over F major, then F major gives way to G major — over which C is the 4th, a dissonance on the strong beat — and C resolves down to B, the 3rd of G.

The moment of suspension on the keyboard: against a G major chord (G–B–D) the held C above sits a step above the chord’s B. That C is the dissonant 4th; it resolves a half step down to B, the chord’s third.

Suspension vs. retardation — same shape, opposite door

A retardation is a suspension that resolves up by step instead of down. The preparation and the accented dissonance are identical; only the resolution direction flips. The classic retardation is the 7–8 (a held 7th rising to the octave/root), most familiar as the leading tone dragged up to the tonic at a final cadence. If a held, accented dissonance steps down to resolve, it is a suspension; if it steps up, it is a retardation.

5. Appoggiatura — leap in, step out, and lean on it

An appoggiatura (Italian appoggiare, “to lean”) is approached by leap and left by step in the opposite direction. Like a suspension it lands as an accented dissonance on the strong beat, but instead of being gently prepared it is jumped into — which makes it the most overtly expressive NCT, the audible sigh in a melody. Below, the line leaps up a fourth from C to F on beat 3 over a C major chord (F is not a chord tone), then leans down by step to E.

6. Escape tone — step in, leap out

The escape tone (French échappée) is the appoggiatura’s mirror image: approached by step, then it leaps away in the opposite direction, usually on a weak beat. Think of a line that begins to step up, hesitates one note too far, then jumps back down into the next chord. It is unaccented and decorative rather than emphatic.

Why “incomplete neighbor” for both?

A full neighbor steps away and steps back. The appoggiatura and the escape tone each keep only half of that gesture — one side is a leap instead of a step — so older texts call them incomplete neighbors. The appoggiatura is the accented incomplete neighbor (the leap is on the way in); the escape tone is the unaccented one (the leap is on the way out). Same family, opposite emphasis.

7. Anticipation — arrive early

An anticipation is a note of the next chord sounded before that chord arrives. It is approached by step, then stays put — the same pitch — while the harmony underneath catches up to it. Because it pre-empts the beat it lands on, it is unaccented: a little eager lean forward into the downbeat. Below, the soprano slips onto C on the “and” of beat 2, while the bass is still on G major; on beat 3 the C major chord arrives and the already-sounding C is now a chord tone.

8. Pedal point — one note holds while harmony moves

A pedal point is the odd one out: instead of a single passing dissonance it is a sustained (or repeated) note — almost always in the bass — held through changing harmonies above it. For part of that span the held note belongs to the chord; for part of it, it does not, and those moments are the non-chord tones. The two classic pedals are the tonic pedal (grounding the music at the start or end of a section) and the dominant pedal (building tension before a big arrival). The name comes from the organ pedal a player literally holds down with a foot while the hands move chords above.

Accented or unaccented?

When you analyse a passage, label the metric position of each NCT, because it changes the character. Suspensions, retardations, and appoggiaturas are accented — they fall on the strong beat and create real tension. Passing tones, neighbors, escape tones, and anticipations are typically unaccented, slipping by on weak beats — though an accented passing tone or neighbor on a strong beat is perfectly possible and simply sounds more pointed.
