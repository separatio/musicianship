Putting it all together: analyzing a complete piece

Every lesson so far handed you one tool. This is where the tools meet the music. We take one short, complete, well-known theme and walk it from the first note to the last cadence — asking, at each step, the questions a working musician asks: What key is this? Where do the phrases end, and how? What are the chords, and what do they do? Which notes belong to the harmony and which only decorate it? And — the question that matters most — why does it work? No new theory here. Just the discipline of using what you already know, in order, on a real piece.

Our subject is the “Ode to Joy” theme from the finale of Beethoven’s Ninth Symphony (1824). Beethoven died in 1827; the music is firmly in the public domain, so we can engrave and dissect it freely. The theme is famous precisely because it is so plain: a stepwise tune that almost never leaves the first five notes of the scale, harmonized with the three primary chords. That plainness is a gift to the analyst — nothing is hidden.

  • Confirm the key of a piece from evidence, not assumption.
  • Map the phrase structure and name every cadence.
  • Assign a Roman numeral to each chord and read the harmonic flow.
  • Separate chord tones from non-chord tones in the melody.
  • Say, in plain words, why the music holds together.

The theme, as Beethoven wrote it

Here is the sixteen-bar tune compressed to its essential eight-bar period — the part everyone knows — engraved in D major, the key of the symphony’s finale. Read it through once before we touch it. Notice how little it moves: almost everything is a step, and the whole melody lives between D and A.

Step 1 — What key is it in?

Never assume the key from the title or the signature alone — confirm it from the music. Three pieces of evidence here all point the same way:

  • The signature: two sharps (F♯, C♯) means either D major or its relative B minor (Lesson 6).
  • The pitches used: the melody draws only on D E F♯ G A — the bottom of the D-major scale. There is no A♯ (the raised 7th that a B-minor tune would lean on), so B minor is ruled out.
  • The frame: the theme begins on F♯ (the 3rd of D) over a D-major chord and ends on D with the strongest possible cadence. The note the music comes to rest on is its tonic.
Why “confirm,” not “assume”?

A key signature is ambiguous by design — every major key shares its signature with a relative minor. The piece itself resolves the ambiguity: which note does the music treat as home? Which note ends the phrases, carries the final cadence, and sounds stable? That note is the tonic. Here every test returns D, so we can state with confidence: the theme is in D major.

The melody’s entire range on the keyboard: scale degrees 1–5 of D major (D E F♯ G A). A tune this confined forces its variety into rhythm and harmony rather than wide leaps — a lesson in economy.

Step 2 — Phrase structure and cadences

Now zoom out to the architecture (Lesson 14). The eight bars split cleanly into two four-bar phrases that begin identically and differ only at the end. That is the textbook signature of a parallel period: a balanced question-and-answer pair where the answer restates the question, then changes its ending to close.

  • Antecedent (bars 1–4) — the “question.” It rises, falls, and stops on an open, unfinished-sounding chord. Its last two bars land on the dominant: a half cadence (HC). A half cadence is any phrase ending on V, and it always sounds like a held breath.
  • Consequent (bars 5–8) — the “answer.” It repeats bars 1–4 note-for-note until the final bar, where it turns downward to the tonic. That ending is a perfect authentic cadence (PAC): V–I, both chords in root position, with the tonic note D on top. A PAC is the strongest, most final cadence there is.
The shape of a period

Antecedent ends weak (HC), consequent ends strong (PAC). The weak ending is what makes the strong one feel earned — you cannot resolve tension you never created. Beethoven builds the whole sixteen-bar tune by doing this twice, but the eight-bar period is the complete musical thought.

Step 3 — The harmony, chord by chord

Time to harmonize and label (Lessons 12 and 16). The standard harmonization of this theme is famously frugal: it uses only the three primary triads of D major — I (D), IV (G), and V (A) — plus a pre-dominant to strengthen the final cadence. Walking it bar by bar, this is the thought process — not just the label, but why each label is right:

Why so few chords?

A melody confined to scale degrees 1–5 can be supported almost entirely by I and V, because between them those two chords already contain four of the five melody notes (D, F♯, A from I; A, E from V). The fifth note, G, never needs its own chord — it appears only as a passing tone between chord tones. IV is held back entirely until bar 7, where it does real work as the pre-dominant that launches the final cadence. The harmony is sparse on purpose: with a tune this simple, richer chords would only muddy it. The genius is in the restraint.

Step 4 — Non-chord tones

Look again at the melody against those chords (Lesson 15). Several melody notes are not in the chord under them. They are non-chord tones — here, every one is a passing tone: a note reached by step and left by step in the same direction, filling the gap between two chord tones.

  • Bar 1, the G: over I (D–F♯–A), the melody goes F♯ → G → A. F♯ and A are chord tones a third apart; the G steps through the middle. A textbook unaccented passing tone.
  • Bar 2, the F♯: in the descent A → G → F♯ → E toward V, the F♯ passes between G and E.
  • Bar 3, the E: over I, D → E → F♯ fills the third from root to 3rd. Another passing tone.
Why this matters for analysis

If you mistake a passing tone for a chord tone, you invent chords that aren’t there — you might call bar 1 “D then Em” because you saw a G. The discipline is: find the chord that the structural (usually metrically strong, longer) notes outline, then explain the rest as decoration. Stepwise notes between two chord tones are passing tones. This single habit prevents most beginner over-analysis.

Step 5 — Looking for chromaticism

A complete analysis always asks: does the music leave the key? We scan for the three things Lessons 17–19 taught us to spot.

  • Secondary dominants (Lesson 17) — a chord borrowed from another key to tonicize a non-tonic chord. The tell-tale sign is an accidental: a sharp or flat foreign to the key. Scan the theme… there are none. Every pitch sits inside D major. No secondary dominants.
  • Borrowed chords / modal mixture (Lesson 19) — chords pulled from the parallel minor (which would bring B♭, or a lowered 6th). Again: no foreign accidentals, so no mixture.
  • Modulation (Lesson 18) — a lasting move to a new key, confirmed by a cadence there. The theme begins and ends in D, and both cadences are in D. No modulation.
A negative result is still a result

“Nothing chromatic happens” is a finding, not a failure. It tells you Beethoven deliberately wrote a diatonic theme — every note in the key, no exceptions. That purity is the point: this is meant to sound like a hymn the whole world could sing, and chromatic detours would work against that. Knowing what isn’t there is half of understanding what is.

Step 6 — So why does it work?

We have the labels. Now the real question — the one this whole course has been building toward. Why is this slight, almost childishly simple tune one of the most powerful melodies ever written? The analysis answers it:

  • Balance. Two equal four-bar phrases, question and answer, weak ending then strong. The form is perfectly symmetrical, and symmetry reads as inevitability.
  • Tension and release in miniature. The half cadence leaves bar 4 hanging; the PAC in bar 8 resolves it completely. In eight bars you get a full cycle of expectation and payoff — the same engine that drives a whole symphony, in miniature.
  • Singable economy. Stepwise motion inside a five-note range is the easiest thing for a human voice to sing. The passing tones smooth the line into pure scale-steps. Anyone can sing it on first hearing — which is exactly what a universal anthem needs.
  • Harmonic restraint. Three chords, saved IV for the one moment it matters. The simplicity is not poverty; it is discipline. Every element pulls the same direction.

That is the payoff of theory: it does not explain away the magic, it shows you the machinery behind the magic — and the machinery turns out to be the same handful of ideas you have been learning all along, used with taste. You now have a method you can point at any short piece: confirm the key, map the phrases and cadences, label the harmony, account for the non-chord tones, check for chromaticism, and then ask what it all adds up to.
