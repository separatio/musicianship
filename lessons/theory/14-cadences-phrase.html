Cadences & phrase structure

Music breathes in sentences. A cadence is the harmonic punctuation that ends one — the chord progression that tells the ear “this thought is closing,” with a finality that ranges from a hard full stop to a hanging comma. Once you can hear and name cadences, you can hear how phrases combine into bigger shapes: the period and the sentence. This lesson is about why some endings sound final and others sound like a question — it is all in the harmony and the top voice.

  • Name the five cadence types by their defining harmony.
  • Explain why a PAC is the strongest close and a half cadence the most open.
  • Hear the difference between “full stop,” “comma,” and “surprise.”
  • Build a period from an antecedent and a consequent phrase.
  • Recognise a sentence: presentation, then continuation driving to a cadence.

1. A cadence is harmonic punctuation

A cadence is the two-chord (sometimes three-chord) formula that closes a phrase. Just as a sentence can end in a period, a question mark, or a dash, a phrase can end with closure that is complete, partial, suspended, or deliberately thwarted. The amount of closure is not a matter of taste — it is fixed by three things:

  • Which chords end the phrase (V–I? ends on V? IV–I? V–vi?).
  • Whether the chords are in root position or inverted.
  • Where the soprano (top) voice lands — on the tonic, or on the 3rd or 5th.

All examples below are in C major (the Phrygian half cadence in A minor), written as plain block chords so the harmony is naked and easy to read. The bottom note of each chord is the bass; the top note is the soprano.

2. The authentic cadences: V–I

An authentic cadence is the dominant resolving to the tonic — V–I (or V7–I). It is the engine of tonal closure, because the dominant contains the leading tone (scale degree 7), which strains upward a half step into the tonic, and often the 7th, which pulls down. When both tendencies resolve, the ear hears “home.” How completely it hears home splits the authentic cadence into two grades.

Perfect authentic cadence (PAC) — the full stop

A cadence is a perfect authentic cadence when all three of the strict conditions are met:

  • The progression is V–I (or V7–I).
  • Both chords are in root position (root in the bass).
  • The soprano ends on the tonic (scale degree 1).

This is the strongest cadence in tonal music — a hard full stop.

Why root position + soprano-on-tonic = maximal closure

Two things make a chord sound settled: hearing its root in the bass (the most stable inversion, because the bass states the chord’s fundamental), and hearing the tonic note on top, where the ear tracks the melody most closely. A PAC delivers both — root in the bass and tonic in the soprano — so every layer of the texture agrees that the music has arrived. Weaken either one and you weaken the close; that is exactly what the next grade does.

Imperfect authentic cadence (IAC) — a softer landing

An imperfect authentic cadence is still V–I, but it fails at least one PAC condition, so it lands more gently. The three common ways to be “imperfect”:

  • Soprano not on the tonic: root-position V–I, but the top voice ends on the 3rd or 5th of the tonic chord.
  • Inverted: one or both chords are in inversion (root not in the bass).
  • Leading-tone IAC: vii°6–I substitutes a leading-tone chord for V.

3. The half cadence: ends ON V

A half cadence (HC) is any phrase ending that stops on the dominant — ?–V, where the approach can be anything (I–V, ii–V, IV–V, vi–V). Because the phrase ends on an unresolved, tension-bearing chord, it sounds unfinished — a comma, a question that wants an answer.

Why ending on V feels open

The dominant is the chord of maximal harmonic tension: it holds the leading tone, which the ear hears as about to move. Park the phrase there and you freeze that “about to move” feeling. Nothing is wrong — it is a perfectly good ending — but it cannot feel final, because the very chord it lands on is defined by its pull elsewhere. That is why half cadences typically end an antecedent phrase: they pose the question that a later phrase will answer.

The Phrygian half cadence

One half cadence is special enough to have its own name. The Phrygian half cadence is iv6–V in minor: the subdominant in first inversion (so scale degree 6 is in the bass) steps down a half step to the dominant’s root (scale degree 5), while an upper voice rises up by step in the opposite direction. That converging stepwise contrary motion gives it an archaic, modal colour — the sound of the Phrygian mode’s half-step from above. It is a half cadence, so it still ends on V.

4. The plagal cadence: IV–I, the “Amen”

A plagal cadence is IV–I — the subdominant to the tonic. It is the “Amen” you sing at the end of a hymn. It does close on the tonic, so it sounds final, but with a softer, more settled finality than a PAC. The reason is in the bass and the missing leading tone.

Why “Amen” is gentle, not dramatic

IV contains no leading tone — there is no scale-degree-7 straining up into the tonic, so there is no sharp pull to resolve. The bass also moves by a gentler step (4→1, a falling fifth or rising fourth) rather than the dominant’s charged 5→1. So the plagal cadence confirms an arrival rather than dramatically achieving one — which is exactly why it so often comes after a PAC has already done the real closing work, as a soft “Amen” tag.

5. The deceptive cadence: V–vi, the surprise

A deceptive (or interrupted) cadence is V–vi: the dominant sets up the ear to expect the tonic, and instead delivers the submediant. The leading tone still resolves up a step — but to scale degree 6 inside vi, not to the tonic. The result is a vivid “not yet” that keeps the music moving.

Why vi is the perfect decoy

vi shares two of its three notes with the tonic chord (in C: vi is A–C–E, I is C–E–G — they share C and E). So the ear is half-fooled: it gets most of the tonic chord and the leading tone’s upward resolution, but the bass lands on A instead of C, and the “wrong” root snaps the expectation. Close enough to satisfy the voice-leading, different enough to surprise — a composer’s favourite way to extend a phrase right when you thought it was over.

Here is that surprise on the keyboard. The dominant triad (G–B–D) primes you for C–E–G; the deceptive resolution lands on A–C–E instead. Notice the two shared keys — C and E belong to both the expected I and the actual vi.

6. The five cadences at a glance

Read this top to bottom as a ladder of closure, from the hardest full stop to the most open question.

Cadence Harmony Closure Feels like
Perfect authentic (PAC) V–I, both root position, soprano on tonic (1) Strongest Full stop
Imperfect authentic (IAC) V–I but inverted, or soprano on 3/5, or vii°6–I Strong, softer Semicolon
Plagal IV–I Final, gentle “Amen”
Half (HC) ends on V (any approach); Phrygian HC = iv6–V in minor Open Comma / question
Deceptive V–vi Thwarted “Not yet”

7. From motive to phrase

Cadences punctuate phrases, so we need the units that build up to one. The smallest is the motive: a short, memorable pattern of pitches and/or rhythm — the “da-da-da-DUM” of Beethoven’s Fifth is one motive. Motives combine into a phrase: a relatively complete musical thought with a sense of trajectory toward a goal — and that goal, in tonal music, is almost always a cadence. The cadence is what tells you a phrase has ended.

8. The period: question and answer

A period is two phrases working as a pair, like a question and its answer:

  • The antecedent phrase ends on a weaker cadence — usually a half cadence. It poses the question and leaves the door open.
  • The consequent phrase answers it and ends on a stronger cadence — usually a PAC — closing the thought.

The defining feature is the cadential pairing: a weak, open ending answered by a strong, closed one. Often the two phrases begin with the same material (a parallel period), which makes the contrast in endings the whole point — same opening, different close.

9. The sentence: state, repeat, drive home

A sentence is the other classic phrase-level form. Where a period is two balanced phrases, a sentence is a single phrase with an internal logic of statement, repetition, and acceleration. It has two functional parts:

  • Presentation: a basic idea (a short motive or gesture), immediately repeated — often transposed to fit the new harmony. State it, then say it again.
  • Continuation: fragmentation (the basic idea broken into smaller, faster pieces) that builds momentum and drives to a cadence — typically a PAC or HC at the end.
Why a sentence feels like it’s accelerating

Fragmentation is the trick. Once a two-bar idea has been stated and repeated, breaking it into one-bar then half-bar pieces makes events arrive faster and faster, even though the pulse never changes. The ear reads that quickening as a build toward something — and the cadence is what it builds toward. A period balances; a sentence accumulates. The opening of Mozart’s Piano Sonata K. 545 and the theme of Beethoven’s Op. 2 No. 1 are textbook sentences.

Period or sentence, the cadence is the load-bearing event: it is what turns a string of motives into a complete musical statement. Master the five cadences and you have the grammar of how music starts, pauses, and stops.
