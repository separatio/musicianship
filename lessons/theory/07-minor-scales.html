Minor scales: natural, harmonic & melodic

“Minor” is not one scale but a small family. The natural minor sets the key signature; then two working variants — harmonic and melodic minor — bend a note or two to solve specific musical problems. Once you see why each variant exists, the accidentals stop being arbitrary and start being inevitable.

  • Build a natural minor from its step pattern W-H-W-W-H-W-W.
  • Tell relative minor (shared key signature) from parallel minor (shared tonic) — and never confuse them.
  • Raise the 7th for harmonic minor and explain the leading tone it buys you.
  • Spell the augmented 2nd between scale-degrees 6 and 7.
  • Raise 6 and 7 ascending, drop them descending — melodic minor — and say why voice-leading wants exactly that.

1. Natural minor — the Aeolian pattern

The natural minor scale (also called the Aeolian mode — more on modes in Lesson 8) is built from this sequence of whole (W) and half (H) steps:

W – H – W – W – H – W – W

Start on A and follow it — A (+W) B (+H) C (+W) D (+W) E (+H) F (+W) G (+W) A — and every note is natural. A natural minor is the one minor scale that uses only the white keys, which is exactly why it is the scale we learn first.

A natural minor on the keyboard, A4 to A5 — seven white keys and back to A. No black keys touched: that is the signature of this scale.

2. Relative vs. parallel minor

There are two completely different ways a minor scale can “relate to” a major scale, and beginners mix them up constantly. Keep them straight and the rest of harmony gets easier.

Relative minor — same notes, different home

The relative minor shares the major scale's key signature — the exact same set of notes — but starts on a different note: scale degree 6 of the major. C major's sixth degree is A, so A minor is the relative minor of C major. Both use no sharps and no flats; you are just re-centring the same seven white keys on A instead of C.

Shortcut: the relative minor sits a minor third (three half steps) below the major tonic. Down from C three half steps lands on A. Run the white keys from A and you have A natural minor — the same scale we just built.

Parallel minor — same home, different notes

The parallel minor shares the major scale's tonic but not its notes. C major and C minor both centre on C, yet C minor lowers the 3rd, 6th, and 7th degrees by a half step (E→E♭, A→A♭, B→B♭). That trio of flattened degrees is precisely what turns a major key into its parallel minor.

Why two relationships, not one

They answer different questions. Relative asks “which major key shares my key signature?” — useful for reading and for borrowing chords. Parallel asks “what happens to this tonic when I switch to minor?” — useful for hearing the colour change on the spot, because the bass note stays put while the 3rd, 6th, and 7th drop. A minor relates to C major (shared signature) and is the parallel minor of A major (shared tonic). Same scale, two different majors in its life.

Shares with major… Built by… Example
Relative key signature (same notes) starting on degree 6 of the major A minor ↔ C major
Parallel tonic (same starting note) lowering degrees 3, 6, 7 of the major A minor ↔ A major

3. Harmonic minor — raising the 7th

Natural minor has a problem when it comes time to harmonise it. Its 7th degree (G in A minor) sits a whole step below the tonic. A note that far away has no urgency — it doesn't lead anywhere. Major scales have a leading tone a half step under the tonic (B→C in C major) that pulls hard toward home; natural minor doesn't.

The fix is surgical: raise the 7th degree by a half step. In A minor, G becomes G♯, now just a half step under A. That is the harmonic minor scale.

Why “harmonic”? It fixes the dominant chord

The name is literal: the change is made for the sake of harmony. The chord built on the 5th degree (the dominant, V) is the engine that drives music home to the tonic — but it only pulls strongly if it is a major chord containing the leading tone. In natural A minor the V chord is E–G–B, a minor chord, with a limp G. Raise G to G♯ and the dominant becomes E–G♯–B, a major chord whose G♯ strains upward to A. That single raised note is what gives minor-key music its strong cadences. (Full treatment in the harmony lessons.)

Raising only the 7th leaves a gap. Between the unaltered 6th (F) and the raised 7th (G♯) there are now three half steps — an augmented second. It is the same distance as a minor third in sound, but spelled as a second because F and G are adjacent letters. That wide, exotic-sounding leap is the fingerprint of harmonic minor.

Spot the augmented 2nd

In any harmonic minor scale the augmented 2nd always sits between scale-degrees 6 and 7 — F to G♯ in A minor. It spans three half steps (F–F♯–G–G♯). If you hum the scale and hit a step that feels like an awkward leap rather than a smooth move, that's it. Melodic minor exists largely to smooth this leap away.

The 6th (F) and the harmonic-minor raised 7th. The natural 7th would be G (the white key labelled “♮7”); raising it a half step gives G♯ (the black key labelled “♯7”). Note how far the 6th (F) is from the raised 7th (G♯) — that gap is the augmented second.

4. Melodic minor — raise going up, revert coming down

Harmonic minor gives a strong leading tone but leaves that jagged augmented 2nd in the middle of the scale — awkward to sing and to play as a smooth line. Melodic minor solves the melody problem with a two-part rule:

  • Ascending: raise both the 6th and the 7th degrees. In A minor: F→F♯ and G→G♯, giving A B C D E F♯ G♯ A.
  • Descending: revert to natural minor — no raised notes. Coming down from A: A G F E D C B A.

So melodic minor is the only common scale whose ascending and descending forms differ.

Why raise 6 too, and why only going up

This is pure voice-leading. Going up toward the tonic, you want a smooth half-step staircase into A: F♯ then G♯ then A, each a half step apart — no augmented-2nd jolt. Raising the 7th alone (harmonic minor) would leave F sitting a wide augmented 2nd below G♯, so melodic minor lifts the 6th as well to fill the gap. Descending, there is no tonic to lead up into — the line is heading away from A, down toward the dominant — so the leading tone earns its keep no longer, and the notes relax back to natural minor. The scale literally changes shape depending on which way the melody travels.

The three forms side by side

Same tonic, same first five notes — the action is all in degrees 6 and 7:

Form Degree 6 Degree 7 A-minor spelling (ascending)
Natural F (♮) G (♮) A B C D E F G A
Harmonic F (♮) G♯ (raised) A B C D E F G♯ A
Melodic (up) F♯ (raised) G♯ (raised) A B C D E F♯ G♯ A
Melodic (down) F (♮) G (♮) A G F E D C B A
