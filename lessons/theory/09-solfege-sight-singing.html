Solfège & sight-singing foundations

Solfège is a set of sung syllables — do re mi fa sol la ti — that gives every note of a scale a name you can sing. It turns the silent shapes on the staff into sound before an instrument touches them. This is the bridge from seeing music to hearing it in your head, and it is the single most useful skill for sight-singing and ear training. The point of this lesson is not memorising syllables — it is learning to audiate: to hear a melody in your mind from the page alone.

  • Map movable-do solfège to scale degrees 1–7 of any major key.
  • Choose between la-based and do-based minor — and know why each exists.
  • Name every scale degree: tonic, supertonic … leading tone (and subtonic).
  • Know what Curwen hand signs are and why a physical sign helps the ear.
  • Start sight-singing a simple diatonic melody, syllable by syllable.

Before this lesson

  • Lesson 5 — Intervals. Solfège is really a way of singing intervals from a known home note; you should be able to measure a step and a skip first.

1. Movable do: syllables for scale degrees

In the movable-do system, do is always the tonic — scale degree 1 — of whatever major key you are in. The seven syllables climb the major scale in order:

The word movable is the whole idea: the syllables track the function, not the letter. In C major, do is C; in G major, do is G; in E♭ major, do is E♭. A tune always sol-fas on the same syllables no matter what key it is in — “do mi sol” is the bright outline of the tonic chord in every key at once.

Why movable do is the sight-singer's tool

A melody is a pattern of relationships, not a list of absolute pitches. Because movable do labels each note by its pull toward home — ti always leans up into do, fa always sags down toward mi — the syllables carry the sound of the function with them. Learn how “sol – do” feels once and you can sing it in any key, with no perfect pitch required. That transfer is exactly what sight-singing needs.

Solfège and the plain numbers 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 are interchangeable — many musicians sing numbers instead. Use whichever sticks; this lesson shows solfège because the open vowels (doh, ray, mee) are easier to sing in tune at speed.

The first three syllables on the keyboard: do–re–mi = C–D–E. Two whole steps up from home — the opening of countless tunes, and the easiest shape to anchor in your ear first.

2. Chromatic syllables (briefly)

Notes outside the scale get their own syllables by bending the vowel. Raised degrees take an “-i” sound; lowered degrees take an “-e” (or “-a” for the second). You do not need to drill these yet — they matter once you sing chromatic music — but it is worth seeing the logic:

  • Raised: di (♯1), ri (♯2), fi (♯4), si (♯5), li (♯6).
  • Lowered: ra (♭2), me (♭3), se (♭5), le (♭6), te (♭7).

So C-major's do raised a half step is di; the note a half step above fa is fi. Keep this light for now — the diatonic seven are what you sing every day.

3. Minor: two honest conventions

Minor is where solfège splits into two camps, and both are correct. The question is simply: which note does do land on?

La-based minor (relative-to-major)

Here do stays on the relative major's tonic, so the minor key starts on la. A natural minor scale is sung la ti do re mi fa sol la — the very same syllables as the major scale, just begun from the sixth syllable. Nothing changes vowel; the half steps still fall at mi–fa and ti–do, exactly where they sit in the relative major.

Do-based minor (parallel)

Here do is always the tonic, in major and minor. To get the darker minor scale you lower three degrees and bend their vowels: a natural minor scale becomes do re me fa sol le te do — the 3rd, 6th and 7th drop to me, le, te. The minor colour is then audible in the syllables themselves.

Which one should you pick?

Both are taught at top conservatories; pick one and be consistent. La-based keeps every syllable's vowel fixed, so the half-step landmarks mi–fa and ti–do never move — many find it easier for tonal, common-practice music and it dovetails with the relative-major / relative-minor link from Lesson 6. Do-based keeps do nailed to the tonic, so scale-degree 1 is always do in any key or mode — which is clearer when you compare a parallel major and minor, or move between modes. The cost of do-based is the extra altered syllables; the cost of la-based is that do no longer means “home” in minor. There is no universally right answer — only a house style.

4. Fixed do — the other school

There is a second world entirely: fixed do, where do is always the note C, re always D, and so on, regardless of key. The syllables are just French/Italian note-names. Fixed do is standard across much of continental Europe and trains absolute pitch recognition, but it does not encode scale-degree function the way movable do does — in a fixed-do world, “do” in C major and “do” in G major are the same pitch but completely different functions. For relative, function-first sight-singing — our goal here — we use movable do for the rest of this lesson.

5. The scale-degree names

Alongside numbers and syllables, each degree of the scale has a traditional name. These names describe each note's relationship to the tonic, and you will meet them constantly in harmony — the “dominant” chord, the “subdominant,” the “leading-tone” resolution.

DegreeSyllableNameWhy that name
1doTonicHome; the note everything resolves to.
2reSupertonicSuper- = above; a step above the tonic.
3miMediantMidway between tonic and dominant.
4faSubdominantA fifth below the tonic (mirror of the dominant).
5solDominantA fifth above; the strongest pull back home.
6laSubmediantMidway between tonic and subdominant; a third below the tonic.
7tiLeading toneA half step below the tonic; it leads up to it.
Leading tone vs. subtonic

The name of the seventh degree depends on its distance from the tonic. A half step below — as in every major scale — is the leading tone (ti): it strains upward into do. A whole step below — as in the natural-minor scale, or a lowered 7th — is the subtonic (te): the pull home is much weaker. Same degree number, different name, because the gap to home is different.

6. Curwen hand signs

Curwen hand signs — devised by John Curwen in 19th-century England and later adapted by Zoltán Kodály — give each solfège syllable a distinct shape made with the hand, placed in space from low (around the waist) to high (around the eyes). Sing do as a closed fist at chest height; mi is a flat hand level with the floor; ti points up with a finger, ready to resolve into the do above it.

Why a hand sign helps the ear

Pitch is invisible. The hand sign makes it physical and spatial: a rising melody literally rises through the air, and the tension of ti (a hand straining upward) maps onto the tension you hear. Tying sound to a movement and a location gives the brain an extra handle to recall the pitch by — which is why hand signs reliably improve intonation in early training. You are not learning gestures for their own sake; you are giving an abstract sound a body to live in. Try every score on this page with the signs and the syllables will stick faster.

7. How to actually start sight-singing

Sight-singing is reading a melody and singing it correctly the first time, with no instrument to lean on. Here is a reliable first method — slow and deliberate beats fast and wrong:

  1. Find do. Read the key signature, name the tonic, and get that one pitch in your ear (a piano or a tuning app is fine before you start — the singing itself stays unaided).
  2. Sing the scale up and down on solfège, with hand signs, to lock do and its neighbours in place.
  3. Scan the melody for shape — where it steps, where it skips, where it returns home — and label each note with its syllable or number.
  4. Audiate first, then sing. Hear the phrase in your head before a sound leaves your mouth. This inner-hearing step is the skill; singing is just confirming it.
  5. Sing stepwise lines confidently, check leaps against do. For a skip, silently relate the target back to the tonic chord (do mi sol) so you land it in tune.

Start with melodies that move mostly by step and stay in one key — like the one below. Audiate it, then sing it on solfège.

Where this leads

Sight-singing and ear training are two sides of one coin: solfège lets you sing what you see, and the same syllables let you notate what you hear. You will turn this around — hearing a melody and writing it down — in Lesson 26 (Ear training & dictation). Until then, the practice drill is short and daily: sing the scale, sing one new melody, audiate before every phrase.
