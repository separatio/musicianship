Musical form: phrase to sonata

Form is architecture in time. Everything earlier in this course works at the scale of a beat, a bar, or a phrase; form is what happens when you zoom out and ask how whole sections of a piece are laid out, in what order, and — crucially — in what keys. The forms below are not arbitrary moulds. Each one is a particular way of staging a single dramatic idea: leave home, and come back. Departure from the tonic creates tension; return to it gives release. That is the engine, and once you hear it you hear it everywhere.

  • Map binary, rounded binary, ternary, rondo, and variations by section and key.
  • Tell rounded binary from ternary — the trap that catches everyone.
  • Lay out sonata form: exposition, development, recapitulation.
  • Explain why form is the large-scale story of tension and resolution.

1. The building blocks (a one-paragraph recap)

Lesson 14 already gave you the small-scale grammar, so we will not re-teach it: a motive is the smallest memorable cell; motives combine into a phrase; two balanced phrases form a period (an antecedent that asks and a consequent that answers), while a single phrase that spins itself out forms a sentence (presentation, then continuation driving to a cadence). Form, this lesson's subject, is the next level up: how groups of phrases assemble into sections, and how those sections are arranged. If phrases are sentences, the forms below are paragraphs and chapters.

Why label sections with letters and keys

Analysts diagram form with capital letters — A, B, C — for distinct musical material, and a prime mark (A′) for a section that returns altered. But the letters alone are only half the story. The other half is the key of each section. A B section that merely sounds different is weak; a B section in a new key is a genuine departure that the return of A then resolves. Always read a form as two parallel rows: material (the letters) and key area (the harmony). The forms differ mostly in how those two rows line up.

2. Binary form (AB) and rounded binary

Binary form has two parts, called reprises because each is normally repeated. The first reprise opens in the tonic and travels to a related key — the dominant in a major-key piece, or the relative major in a minor-key piece — ending with a cadence in that new key. The second reprise sets out from there and works its way back to close in the tonic. So the deep structure is tonal: away, then home.

SectionKeyMaterial
‖: A :‖ (first reprise)I → V (major) / i → III (minor)Opening idea, modulating
‖: B :‖ (second reprise)V → I (back to tonic)Continuation, returns home

Rounded binary is the most important variant: in the second reprise, the opening material of A comes back in the home key before the end. The standard diagram is ‖: A :‖: B A′ :‖ — the little “round trip” back to the A music is what “rounds” the form off. This is the backbone of countless minuets, scherzos, and theme statements.

3. Ternary form (ABA)

Ternary form is three self-contained sections in the pattern A–B–A. The outer A sections are complete, closed units (each ends with its own cadence in the tonic); the middle B section contrasts — usually in a different, related key, with its own distinct theme and its own cadence — and then A returns literally, often marked in the score with da capo (“from the top”) rather than written out again.

SectionKeyMaterial
AI (closes in tonic)Main theme, complete and self-contained
BA related key (e.g. V, vi, or the parallel/relative)Contrasting theme, its own cadence
AI (home)Literal return of the main theme

Here is the shape in miniature: a tonic A idea, a contrasting B that leans toward G, and the same A returning unchanged.

Rounded binary vs. ternary — the classic trap

Both end with a return of A, so on paper they look alike. The test is the middle. In rounded binary the B is short, harmonically open and dependent — it spins the A material over dominant harmony and can't stand alone, so the whole thing is really one continuous two-reprise shape. In ternary the B is a self-sufficient section: its own theme, its own key, its own cadence, able to stand on its own, and the A return is full and literal. Rule of thumb: if the B section could be lifted out and played as a complete little piece, you have ternary; if it only makes sense as a bridge back to A, you have rounded binary.

4. Rondo (ABACA…)

A rondo keeps bringing back a single tune. The recurring section is the refrain (A), always heard in the tonic; between its returns come contrasting episodes (B, C, …) in other keys. The most common layouts are the five-part rondo (ABACA) and the seven-part / sonata rondo (ABACABA). The refrain functions like a chorus: no matter how far an episode wanders, the home tune and the home key keep coming back to ground you.

B (episode 1)V or relative keyFirst contrasting material
A (refrain)IMain tune returns
C (episode 2)A further, often more distant keySecond, stronger contrast

Extend it with another B episode and a final refrain and you get ABACABA; when the A and first B together behave like a sonata exposition that is later recapitulated, the result is the hybrid sonata-rondo.

5. Theme and variations

In theme and variations you state a complete theme — typically a short, closed tune (often itself in binary or rounded binary) — and then present a series of variations, each one a re-imagining of that same theme. What changes is the surface: the melody gets ornamented, the rhythm re-cast, the harmony re-coloured, the texture thickened or thinned, the mode flipped from major to minor. What stays is the skeleton — usually the theme's phrase lengths and underlying harmonic plan — so the ear keeps recognising the same bones under new clothes.

Variation is not development

They sound similar but work oppositely. Variation presents the whole theme again, intact in structure, just dressed differently — one complete statement at a time. Development (you'll meet it in sonata form below) takes fragments of a theme and tosses them through many keys and combinations, deliberately breaking the theme apart. Variation reassures; development destabilises.

6. Sonata form

Sonata form (or sonata-allegro) is the great large-scale drama of the Classical and Romantic eras — the typical shape of a symphony's, concerto's, or sonata's first movement. It has three big sections, and at heart it is a vast rounded binary: depart from the tonic, destabilise, then return everything home. What makes it powerful is that the conflict is tonal — it is staged as a clash of keys that the ending must resolve.

Exposition — stating the conflict

The exposition lays out two contrasting theme groups in two different keys:

  • First theme (P) — the primary theme, firmly in the tonic, establishing home.
  • Transition (TR) — a modulating bridge that dismantles the tonic and drives toward the new key.
  • Second theme (S) — the secondary theme, in a new key: the dominant (V) for a major-key movement, or the relative major (III) (sometimes the minor dominant, v) for a minor-key one. Often more lyrical, to contrast the first theme.
  • Closing (C) — cadential material confirming and celebrating the new key.

The exposition is the “problem”: the music now stands in the wrong key, and the second theme is stranded away from home.

Development — the instability

The development is where the music is most restless. It takes motives fragmented from the exposition's themes and runs them through rapid modulation and sequence, touching many keys and avoiding the tonic. This is the section of greatest tonal, harmonic, and rhythmic instability — the knot pulled tight — usually ending poised on the home dominant, so the ear is desperate for resolution.

Recapitulation — resolving it

The recapitulation brings back the exposition's material in order, but with one decisive change: both themes now sound in the tonic. The second theme, which first appeared in the dominant (or relative major), is transposed home. That single act — the wayward second theme finally pulled into the tonic — is the resolution of the whole movement: the tonal conflict set up in the exposition is closed.

An optional introduction may precede the exposition and an optional coda may follow the recapitulation, but the three-part exposition–development–recapitulation core is the form.

7. Why form matters

Step back and the whole catalogue tells one story. Binary goes out and comes back. Ternary states, contrasts, and returns. Rondo keeps re-grounding you in the refrain between adventures. Variations hold the structure fixed and recolour the surface. Sonata form raises the stakes highest: it makes the very key the protagonist, banishes the second theme to a foreign key, destabilises everything, and only resolves when that theme is dragged home.

Form is tension and resolution at the largest scale

Cadences (Lesson 14) create tension and release across a phrase; modulation (Lesson 18) does it across a section. Form does the same thing across an entire movement. The departure from the tonic is the tension; the structured return to it is the resolution. That is why analysing form is not bookkeeping — it is tracing the single biggest arc of dramatic feeling in the piece, the one you sense even without naming it. Everything in this course has been building the tools to hear it: in the next lesson you'll point all of them at one complete short work.
