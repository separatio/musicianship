Chord voicings & inversions at the keyboard

You already know what a triad is — a root, a third, and a fifth stacked in thirds (Theory Lesson 10). This lesson is about playing it: getting three notes under one hand, then re-ordering those same three notes into inversions so that one chord flows into the next with the smallest possible hand movement. That re-ordering is the engine behind every smooth-sounding chord progression, and the piano is the one instrument where you can literally see it happen.

  • Play a triad as a single hand-shape (root position, fingers 1-3-5).
  • Build the first and second inversions and feel how each re-shapes the hand.
  • Use inversions for voice-leading: keep common tones, move the nearest notes.
  • Play I–IV–V–I with tiny hand movements instead of jumps.
  • Tell an open voicing from a closed one, and spread a chord across two hands.

1. A triad under one hand

In C major the tonic triad is C-E-G — the root, a major third up to E, a minor third up to G. At the keyboard you do not play those notes one at a time; you press all three together with one hand. The natural fingering in the right hand is 1-3-5 (thumb on C, middle finger on E, little finger on G); in the left hand it is 5-3-1 from the bottom up. The shape skips a key each time, so the hand sits open and relaxed, never cramped.

This block — the three chord tones sounded together with the root at the bottom — is the chord’s root position.

C-E-G in root position. The labels are the chord members (1 = root, 3 = third, 5 = fifth) — which double as the right-hand fingers that play them.

2. The three positions

An inversion is the same three notes with a different one in the bass. The rule is purely about which chord member is lowest; the chord’s root never changes, only its arrangement does.

  • Root position — the root (C) is lowest: C-E-G. Figured bass leaves this unmarked (a plain 5/3).
  • First inversion — the third (E) is lowest: E-G-C. Move the bottom C of root position up an octave and you are there. Figured bass writes a 6.
  • Second inversion — the fifth (G) is lowest: G-C-E. Move the bottom E of first inversion up an octave. Figured bass writes 6/4.
Why “move the bottom note to the top” works

A triad is built in thirds, so its three notes are evenly spread inside an octave. Lift the lowest note up by a full octave and it lands just above the old top note — you have rotated the stack by one notch without adding or removing anything. Three notes means exactly three rotations: root position, first inversion, second inversion, and the next rotation is back to root position an octave higher. The hand learns three shapes and owns the chord everywhere on the keyboard.

First inversion (E-G-C)

The root C has jumped to the top. The hand now spans E up to C — fingers 1-2-5 sit comfortably, because E-G is a small gap and G-C a wider one.

Second inversion (G-C-E)

Now the fifth G is lowest and the root C sits in the middle. The wide gap is at the bottom (G-C), the small one on top (C-E) — a mirror of first inversion under the fingers.

Say the bass note out loud as you play each shape — “root, third, fifth” — until your eye reads the lowest note and your hand finds the rest automatically. (For the figured-bass numbers themselves, see Theory Lesson 12; here we only need to play the three positions.)

3. Why inversions matter: voice-leading

Inversions are not decoration. Their real job is voice-leading — connecting one chord to the next so smoothly that each note moves the shortest possible distance, or does not move at all. The two guiding habits are simple:

  • Keep common tones — any note shared by two chords stays put in the same finger.
  • Move the rest by step — every other note slides to the nearest note of the new chord, never leaping when a step will do.

Played all in root position, I-IV-V-I in C (C-E-G → F-A-C → G-B-D → C-E-G) forces the whole hand to jump up and down the keyboard — it sounds blocky and amateurish. Re-voice the IV and V with inversions and the hand barely moves:

Chord Root-position triad Smooth voicing (played) How each note got there
I C-E-G C-E-G (root position) Starting shape.
IV F-A-C C-F-A (2nd inversion) C is shared — it stays; E→F and G→A each move up a step.
V G-B-D B-D-G (1st inversion) No shared tone; C→B, F→G, A→G — all by step.
I C-E-G C-E-G (root position) G is shared — it stays; B→C and D→E each move up a step.
Why this is the whole point of the keyboard

Every note in that smooth version moves by at most one step, and a common tone is held wherever one exists. The hand stays in roughly one place and the chords seem to melt into each other instead of jumping. This is exactly the “move each voice the shortest distance” principle from four-part writing (Theory Lesson 13) — but at the piano you don’t have to imagine four singers; you can see and feel all the voices under two hands at once.

Walk that progression note by note on three keyboards:

I → IV (C stays)

IV voiced as C-F-A. The shared C holds; E and G each nudge up one white key to F and A.

IV → V (all step)

V voiced as B-D-G. No common tone here, so C→B, F→G, A→G — every note moves, but only by a step.

V → I (G stays)

Home again on C-E-G. The shared G holds; B and D step up to C and E. The hand is right back where it started.

4. Open vs closed voicings

Everything so far has been closed (close-position) voicing: the three notes packed as tightly as possible, all inside a single octave, sitting under one hand. A open (open-position) voicing spreads the same notes out across more than an octave — typically by dropping one of the middle notes down an octave, or by handing the root to the left hand far below the rest.

The notes — and therefore the chord’s name and function — are identical. Only the spacing changes, and with it the colour: closed voicings sound compact and warm, open voicings sound bigger, airier, and more resonant, because the low root rings underneath with room to breathe.

Two hands, one chord

The simplest open voicing at the piano: put the root low in the left hand and the rest of the triad in the right. The left hand anchors the harmony in the bass while the right hand is free to play the chord, an inversion, or a melody on top. This left-root / right-chord split is the foundation of nearly all keyboard accompaniment — and the reason a pianist can lay down a full harmony and a tune at the same time.

5. The piano as the place to see harmony

On a guitar the same chord can live in five different shapes scattered across the neck; on the staff harmony is stacked vertically but abstract. The keyboard is the one surface where pitch runs strictly left = low, right = high in a single unbroken line. An interval is a fixed visual distance; a triad is a fixed shape; an inversion is that shape rotated; a smooth progression is a hand that hardly moves. Harmony stops being a list of rules and becomes something you can watch.

That is why so much of this course returns to the keyboard diagram, and why the next lesson is devoted entirely to using the piano as a theory visualizer — seeing scales, intervals, and chords at a glance.
