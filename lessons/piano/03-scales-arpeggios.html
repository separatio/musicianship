Piano Application · Lesson 3

Scales & arpeggios at the keyboard

You already know what the C-major scale is — seven letters, no sharps or flats. This lesson is about the other half of the job: getting those notes under your fingers smoothly, with a fingering that never runs out of fingers. The whole trick is two small moves — the thumb passing under and a finger crossing over — and once you understand why they exist, every scale and arpeggio in the book becomes a variation on the same idea.

  • Play the one-octave C-major scale with the standard fingering, both hands.
  • Understand the thumb-under and cross-over moves — and why a five-finger hand needs them.
  • Put the hands together, and use contrary motion as a learning shortcut.
  • Finger the C-major arpeggio (the triad spread out) cleanly, both hands.
  • Practise scales and arpeggios so they get faster and stay even.

1. Why scales need a special fingering

A hand has five fingers; a scale has eight notes to the octave (and keeps going past that). If you tried to play a scale with one finger per key in order — 1 2 3 4 5 — you would hit the fifth note and have nothing left. You would have to lift the whole hand and jump it sideways, and every jump is an audible bump: a tiny gap, an uneven note.

Scale fingering solves this by recycling the thumb. Partway up, the thumb slips under the longer fingers to a new key so the hand can keep travelling without lifting off. Going down, a longer finger crosses back over the thumb. The hand flows along the keys like a caterpillar instead of hopping like a frog — and the sound stays seamless and legato.

Why these two moves and no others

The thumb is short and sits below the palm, so it can tuck underneath the other fingers without the hand twisting. The long fingers (mostly the 3rd) can reach over a parked thumb just as cleanly. No other pair of fingers can pass each other without the wrist contorting. That single anatomical fact — short thumb, long middle finger — is the reason essentially every scale fingering in Western keyboard technique is built on thumb-under and 3-over (or, in wider scales, 4-over).

2. The right-hand C-major scale

Ascending, the right hand plays 1 – 2 – 3 – 1 – 2 – 3 – 4 – 5. Thumb on C, 2 on D, 3 on E — and now the thumb-under: the thumb passes beneath the 3rd finger to land on F. From F it is a plain 1 2 3 4 5 the rest of the way up (F G A B C), finishing with the 5th finger on the top C. Coming down, you reverse it: 5 4 3 2 1, and after the thumb plays F the 3rd finger crosses over the thumb onto E, then 2 on D, 1 on C.

The C-major scale highlighted across one octave (C4 up to C5). The labels here are scale degrees (1–8), not fingers — all white keys, which is exactly why C major is the scale every pianist learns first.

3. The left-hand C-major scale

The left hand uses the same idea in mirror image. Ascending, it plays 5 – 4 – 3 – 2 – 1 – 3 – 2 – 1: the 5th finger (the pinky) starts on the low C, the hand walks up 5 4 3 2 1 to G under the thumb, and then the 3rd finger crosses over the thumb onto A, with 2 on B and the thumb finishing on the top C. Because the left hand climbs with its weakest finger first, the cross-over does the work here that the thumb-under did in the right hand — same mechanic, opposite direction.

4. Hands together — and contrary motion

Played the obvious way — both hands rising and falling together, in parallel motion — the two fingerings do not line up. When the right thumb is tucking under to take F, the left hand is doing something else entirely; the thumbs land on different beats. That mismatch is what makes parallel scales feel awkward at first, and it is normal. Practise each hand alone until its fingering is automatic, then combine.

The contrary-motion shortcut

There is an easier way in to put the hands together. Start with both thumbs on middle C and move the hands in opposite directions — right hand up, left hand down. Now the fingering is identical in both hands at every instant: 1 2 3 1 2 3 4 5 in both. The thumbs cross under at the same moment, the fingers cross over at the same moment — the two hands are perfect mirror images. It feels coordinated almost immediately, which makes contrary motion a great first taste of hands-together playing before you tackle the trickier parallel version.

5. Arpeggios — the triad, spread out

An arpeggio is a chord played one note at a time instead of all at once (the word is from the Italian arpeggiare, “to play like a harp”). The C-major arpeggio is just the C-major triad — C E G — laid out in time and carried up to the next C. The gaps between the notes are wider than in a scale (thirds, not steps), so the hand stretches more between thumb-unders, but the mechanic is exactly the same one you just learned.

For a single octave that stops on the top C, the right hand plays 1 – 2 – 3 – 5 (thumb C, 2 on E, 3 on G, 5 on the top C); the left hand mirrors it with 5 – 3 – 2 – 1 (pinky C, 3 on E, 2 on G, thumb on the top C). If you keep going past one octave, the 5th-finger bookend disappears and the right hand pivots on a repeating 1 2 3 (thumb-under after the 3rd finger every time), the left hand on 2 1 / 3-over — the same recycling-thumb principle as the scale, just spaced a third apart.

The C-major arpeggio on the keyboard: the chord tones C (root), E (3rd), G (5th), and the octave C. Compared with the scale diagram above, you can see the arpeggio skips the in-between notes — it is the chord’s skeleton.

6. How to practise so it actually improves

Scales and arpeggios reward patient, slow practice far more than fast repetition. The goal is not speed for its own sake — it is evenness: every note the same length and the same weight, with no bump at the thumb-under. Speed is just evenness played faster.

  • Hands separately first. Get each fingering automatic on its own before combining. Most of the difficulty in hands-together playing is one hand’s fingering not yet being secure.
  • Start slowly, listen for the bump. The thumb-under is where evenness breaks down. Play slowly enough that you can hear and smooth out the join, then gradually increase the tempo.
  • Use a metronome. Set it slow, play one note per click, and only move the tempo up when the scale is clean and even at the current speed. (We will pair scales with the practice track in a later lesson.)
  • Try contrary motion to break in hands-together playing before the harder parallel version, then come back to parallel.
