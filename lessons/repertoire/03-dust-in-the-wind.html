Example Piece: Travis Picking & a Descending Line

Our example piece is the famous late-1970s acoustic study in the style of “Dust in the Wind.” Its composer built the guitar part as, in his own words, “a finger exercise for learning fingerpicking” — which makes it a perfect lens for two ideas at once: the alternating-thumb Travis-picking technique in the right hand, and a gentle descending line in the left that turns one held chord into a whole phrase. We will analyse how and why it works, not transcribe it.

  • Explain the alternating-thumb Travis pattern and why it sounds “full.”
  • Hear the key as C major leaning toward its relative A minor.
  • Read a descending stepwise line as a line cliché and name each sonority with Roman numerals.
  • Connect the moving voice to non-chord tones and smooth voice leading.
  • Take the generic pattern to any chord, then find a properly licensed tab to learn the piece itself.

1. The key: C major, with one foot in A minor

The piece sits in C major — no sharps or flats, home chord C. But it spends much of its time on, or pulling toward, A minor, which is C major's relative minor: the same seven notes, a different home (the major scale and its relative minor share a key signature). In Roman-numeral terms, A minor is the vi chord of C. That C ↔ Am tension — bright tonic versus its wistful relative — is the whole emotional engine of the tune, and it is created almost entirely by one note moving down a scale.

2. Travis picking: the alternating thumb

Travis picking (named for the country guitarist Merle Travis) is the most common fingerstyle accompaniment pattern. The idea is a division of labour between the hand's two halves:

  • The thumb (p) keeps a steady, metronomic alternating bass — bouncing between two low strings (usually the root and the fifth of the chord) on every beat, like a one-person walking bass line.
  • The fingers (i, m, a) pick melody and chord tones on the treble strings between the thumb's beats, landing on the off-beats to create syncopation.
Why it sounds like two players

Because the thumb and fingers are rhythmically independent: the thumb holds down a rock-steady pulse while the fingers float syncopated notes against it. Your ear separates the steady low part from the floating high part and hears two voices — an implied bass and a melody — from one guitar. The alternating (not repeated) bass is the trick: hopping root–fifth–root–fifth outlines the chord low down and keeps the pulse moving, so the texture never sits still.

Here is a generic Travis cell over a C chord — one bar, stripped to its skeleton. The thumb alternates the C bass (root) and G (fifth) on the four beats; treble notes fall between them. This is the engine, not the piece: once it is automatic, you slide it under any chord.

The thumb's two bass notes (piano view)

The alternating bass is just root–fifth low down: C and G. Everything the fingers add sits above this steady two-note foundation.

The C chord the pattern rides on (guitar view)

An open C shape. The thumb (p) plays the C bass on the 5th string (3rd fret) and alternates to a lower fifth; the fingers i / m / a pick the treble strings above. See Guitar 5 for the p i m a hand map.

3. The descending line: a “line cliché”

Now the left hand. The signature sound of the intro is not a burst of new chords — it is essentially one chord with a single voice walking down a scale through it. Theorists call this a line cliché: you hold a chord and move one note (here the top voice) by step, so each step creates a new, richer sonority while everything else stays put.

Over a held C in the bass, the top voice descends stepwise C – B – A – G. Watch what each passing note does to the chord:

Top voice Sonority over C Roman numeral (in C) What changed
C C major I Plain tonic — home.
B C major 7 Imaj7 The 7th (B) adds a soft, hanging colour.
A C6 (= A minor 7) I (add 6) → vi The 6th (A) leans the chord toward A minor.
G C (back to a chord tone) / G in bass I / V6 The line resolves; the phrase can turn or repeat.

That single falling line is why the music feels like it is gently descending the whole time, and why it keeps brushing against A minor: the moment the top voice hits A, you are hearing an A-minor seventh chord hiding inside C.

The descending top voice on the keyboard: C5 → B4 → A4 → G4, four white keys walking down by step. On guitar this whole motion usually happens on the B string, one finger moving while the chord shape stays.

4. The voice leading: why one moving note is enough

Why does such a simple device sound so finished? Because of voice leading: when one voice moves by step and the others hold, the ear hears a smooth, inevitable line rather than a jolt of new chords. The held C–E–G is the common-tone glue; the falling B–A is heard as non-chord-tone colour passing through the C triad before it lands. Specifically, the B and the A behave like a chain of stepwise embellishments over a stationary harmony — the same passing/neighbour logic you met in Theory 15, just frozen into the chord names.

Descending soprano or descending bass — same family

Our example walks the top voice down (C–B–A–G). Its famous cousin walks the bass down instead — think C – C/B – C/A(Am) – C/G, or the chromatic C – Cmaj7 – C7 – F line where the bass falls C–B–B♭–A. Both are the same idea: hold a harmony and let one outer voice step down, generating a new chord per step. Recognising the pattern in either voice means you can spot it in dozens of songs — it is a workhorse of harmonic progression.

5. Putting the two halves together

Now picture both hands at once. The thumb runs its steady alternating C–G bass (Section 2) while the fingers, instead of repeating a fixed chord, let their top note inch down the line cliché (Section 3). Steady pulse below, slow descent above — that is the entire effect of the piece, and it is built from two ideas you can now name and reuse. Practise them separately first: the thumb pattern until it is mindless, then the descending top voice over a held shape; only then combine.
