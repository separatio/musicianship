Example piece: a minor-key fingerstyle study

Our first repertoire breakdown takes a famous, gentle, sad fingerstyle piece — the kind in the style of “Sadness and Sorrow” — and uses it as a lens. We will not transcribe it; instead we ask why it sounds the way it does and which theory it quietly exercises. By the end you should be able to look at any small minor-key fingerstyle tune and name what is going on under the hood.

1. The key, and why it sounds sad

Published licensed editions of this piece print it in E minor — a friendly key on the guitar, because its tonic chord uses three open strings. The choice of a minor key is the first and biggest reason the music reads as melancholy, and the reason is concrete, not mystical.

A minor scale differs from its parallel major in three lowered degrees — flat 3, flat 6, flat 7 — but the decisive one is the third. The interval from the tonic up to the third is a minor third (3 half steps) instead of a major third (4 half steps). That single half-step-narrower third is the “darker” colour you hear; the whole major/minor mood rides on it (you measured exactly this in Lesson 5, Intervals).

Why the flat third reads as “sad”

Two threads point the same way. Acoustically, the minor third is a less consonant, “darker” interval than the major third — we hear smaller intervals as less bright — so a chord built on a minor third sounds softer and more shadowed. Perceptually, listeners (including some with no exposure to Western music) reliably hear minor as the sadder of the two, and theorists tie that to minor melodies tending to sit lower and feel less stable than we expect — features that echo the prosody of sad speech. Cultural conditioning amplifies the effect, but it is not the whole story.

The second reason is motion: pieces like this lean on descending lines — the melody and the inner voices drift downward, and falling pitch is one of music’s oldest cues for sighing, settling, grief. Minor mode supplies the colour; descending contour supplies the gravity. Together they do most of the emotional work before a single ornament is added.

The same E–G–B on the keyboard. E is the root, G the minor third (note how close it sits below G♯, the major third), B the fifth.

2. The progression, as block chords

Strip the arpeggios and ornaments away and a piece like this is, at heart, a loop of diatonic chords in E minor. Here is a representative progression of the type these tunes use — built entirely from the chords the key gives us, labelled with Roman numerals so the function is plain. *The fifth chord is the key’s pivot. From natural minor it is minor (Bm, v). Raise the seventh degree of the scale (D → D♯) — the move that defines harmonic minor — and it becomes a major V (B major), giving a strong leading-tone pull back to i. Sad fingerstyle pieces use this raised-seventh V at cadences for exactly that tug home.

A representative loop in the spirit of the piece — i – VI – III – VII – iv – V – i — written as plain block chords:

Why this loop feels melancholy and “endless”

Three of these chords are major (VI, III, VII), yet the loop still sounds sad — because they are heard in orbit around a minor tonic. The flat-side chords (III, VI, VII) are the relative major’s family borrowed back into minor; they brighten briefly, then gravity pulls back to i. And because i is the resting point of a minor triad, every “home” arrival re-confirms the dark third. The descending root motion you can trace through several of these chords (E…C…then A…) reinforces the falling feeling from section 1.

How those block chords get prettied into the actual tune is the subject of Lesson 15, Non-chord tones: passing tones, neighbour tones and suspensions thread between the chord tones to make the singing melody. The chords are the skeleton; the non-chord tones are the flesh.

3. The fingerstyle approach: PIMA arpeggios

A piece like this is rarely strummed. Instead each chord is arpeggiated — broken into one note at a time — with the right-hand fingers, so the harmony shimmers rather than thumps. This is the PIMA system you met in Guitar Lesson 5. The letters come from Spanish:

  • p — pulgar, the thumb — plays the bass strings (6, 5, 4).
  • i — índice, the index.
  • m — medio, the middle.
  • a — anular, the ring finger.

Convention assigns i, m, a to the three treble strings (3, 2, 1) while p takes the bass. A staple pattern is exactly that: thumb plucks the bass note, then i–m–a roll up the top three strings. Repeat it over each chord of the progression and the block chords from section 2 turn into a flowing, sustained texture — the bass note anchoring while the upper voices arpeggiate.

Here is a generic PIMA arpeggio over the E-minor tonic chord — a pattern, not the song’s riff:

The chord shape (guitar)

Open Em: low E (string 6) and the open G, B, high E strings ring, with the 5th (B) on string 5 fret 2 and the root (E) on string 4 fret 2. The thumb (p) catches the low E; i–m–a sweep the top three open strings.

The chord tones (piano)

The same four pitches a pianist would arpeggiate: E–B (root & fifth, the bass anchor) then E–G up top. Same harmony, different hands.

4. So which theory does this piece exercise?

Almost everything you can hear in this small tune traces back to a lesson you have already done. That is the point of a repertoire breakdown — to show the theory in the wild:

What you hear The theory behind it
The overall dark, sad colour Minor scales — the flat third (and flat 6 / flat 7).
The chords of the loop and their names Triads — diatonic chord qualities in minor (i, iv minor; III, VI, VII major; ii° diminished).
The strong pull home at cadences (B major V) Harmonic minor — the raised seventh that turns v into a major V.
The singing melody weaving over the chords Non-chord tones — passing, neighbour & suspension notes between chord tones.
The flowing, plucked texture PIMA picking — right-hand arpeggiation of each chord.
How to study any piece this way

Find the key first (look at the key signature and the chord it keeps resting on). Reduce the texture to block chords and number them with Roman numerals. Notice the contour — is it falling? Then identify the right-hand pattern. Once you can describe a piece in those four moves, you can learn it faster and remember it longer, because you understand it instead of just memorising finger positions.
