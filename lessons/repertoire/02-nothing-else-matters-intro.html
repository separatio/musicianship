Example piece: a fingerpicked minor intro

One of the most recognisable clean-tone guitar intros ever recorded — the opening of Metallica’s “Nothing Else Matters” — is, underneath the fame, a small masterclass in open-string fingerstyle in a minor key. We will treat it as the example piece and use it as a lens, not a tab: the goal is to understand why it sounds the way it does, so you can build your own intros on the same bones. We analyse the key, the open strings, the picking, and the harmony — and point you to a licensed tab for the actual notes.

1. The key: E minor, and why the guitar loves it

The intro lives in E minor. That key is not an accident of taste — it is the key the guitar is physically built for. In standard tuning the six open strings are E A D G B E, and four of those open strings (the two E’s, the G and the B) already spell an E minor chord. You can strum an Em with no left hand at all on those strings. That is exactly why so much of the classical-guitar and metal repertoire is written in E minor: the instrument resonates there for free.

E minor (natural minor) uses the notes E – F♯ – G – A – B – C – D, a single-sharp key signature (F♯). The minor third (E up to G) is what makes it sound dark rather than bright; that one interval, three half steps instead of four, is the whole emotional difference. We unpack this fully in the minor-scales lesson — here we just put it to work.

Why open strings change everything

On a piano every note is “stopped” the instant you lift the key. On a guitar an open string keeps vibrating with no finger to damp it, so it rings under whatever you play next. A fingerpicked intro in E minor exploits this: because the open strings belong to the Em chord, they can ring through the picking and blur the notes into a shimmering wash instead of separate plucks. Play the same pattern in a key with no friendly open strings (say E♭ minor) and you lose that ring entirely — you would have to fret and damp everything by hand.

Em from open strings (guitar)

Open E minor. Only two notes are fretted (2nd fret, strings 5 and 4, stopped by the index and middle fingers); strings 6, 3, 2 and 1 ring open and already belong to the chord. That is the open-string resonance the intro is built on.

The Em triad (piano)

The same three notes spelled on the keyboard: E (root), G (the minor third — the dark colour), B (the fifth). One octave from E4 to D5.

2. Arpeggiating a chord: the picking pattern

The intro never strums. Instead it arpeggiates — it sounds the notes of a chord one at a time, in a repeating order, so the chord unfolds horizontally as a little melody rather than landing as a block. (Arpeggios are the chord-as-line idea from the triads lesson; here you hear them played, not just stacked.) Because the chord is an open Em, the picked notes and the ringing open strings are the same harmony, so nothing clashes — the pattern just keeps re-sounding the Em chord from the bottom up.

Below is a generic open-position p–i–m–a arpeggio over an Em shape — the kind of pattern this style is built from, not the song’s actual riff. The thumb (p) takes a bass string; i m a sweep up three higher strings in a steady relay. Practise this to get the feel; reach for the licensed tab when you want the exact notes and rhythm.

Why arpeggiate instead of strum?

A strummed chord gives you the harmony all at once and then decays. An arpeggio spreads those same notes across time, so each pluck is audible and the open strings accumulate — by the end of a bar several notes are ringing together that you struck separately. The result is fuller and more singing than a strum, and it gives a clean electric or nylon-string tone room to bloom. It is also why the intro feels intricate despite using, harmonically, just a handful of plain chords.

3. Thumb and fingers: two independent voices

The engine room of this technique is right-hand independence: the thumb (p) and the fingers (i m a) operate as two separate voices, almost like two hands on a piano. The thumb owns the bass — the low E, A and D strings — laying down a steady root or pedal tone. The fingers own the treble — strings 3, 2 and 1 — carrying the moving arpeggio above it. This split is the standard PIMA division of labour from the technique lesson; what makes a fingerpicked intro work is doing both at once without either one stiffening the other.

Mapped to the strings, the default assignment is the relay you already know — each finger lives over its own string so nothing has to jump:

Finger Voice Usual string(s) Job in the intro
p (thumb)Bass6, 5, 4 (low E, A, D)Sound the chord root / low pedal note.
i (index)Treble3 (G)Lower voice of the arpeggio.
m (middle)Treble2 (B)Inner voice of the arpeggio.
a (ring)Treble1 (high E)Top voice — the note the ear hears as “melody.”

The open Em with the right-hand fingers labelled on the strings they pluck: thumb (p) on the low E (string 6), then i m a on the open G, B and high E (strings 3–2–1). Thumb = bass voice, fingers = treble voice.

Building the independence

Get the thumb steady first. Play just p on the low E in slow quarter-notes until it keeps time without you watching it — then add i m a on top. The thumb becomes a metronome you carry inside the hand, freeing the fingers to phrase the arpeggio musically above it. Most early stumbles in this style are the thumb speeding up or hesitating whenever the fingers get busy; isolate it and that disappears.

4. The harmonic skeleton

Strip the arpeggios away and the intro’s harmony is plain: a short loop of diatonic chords drawn from E minor. Naming chords by Roman numeral — their scale degree in the key, uppercase for major and lowercase for minor — lets us describe the function without copying anyone’s notes. (Roman numerals and diatonic chords are the triads lesson’s territory.) The diatonic chords of E minor are:

A representative minor-key loop in this style leans on the tonic and the two bright major chords a step below and above it — a i – VI – VII motion (Em – C – D), one of the most common progressions in all of minor-key popular music. Why VI and VII feel like a lift

In a minor key the chords on the sixth and seventh degrees (VI and VII) are major, while the tonic is minor. So i → VI → VII walks from a dark chord up through two bright ones and back — a small swell of light inside the minor mood, then a return home. The VII (D major) sits a whole step below the tonic and pulls strongly back up to Em, which is why the loop feels like it “resolves” even without a dominant chord. That rise-and-fall is most of the intro’s emotional arc, built from three ordinary triads.

5. Make it yours

You now have the recipe behind the example piece without needing a single note of it: pick a minor key with friendly open strings (E minor or A minor on guitar), choose a short diatonic loop (i–VI–VII is a safe, beautiful default), set a steady thumb on the bass, and let i m a arpeggiate the chord above while the open strings ring. That is a complete, original fingerpicked intro — same skeleton, your own flesh.
