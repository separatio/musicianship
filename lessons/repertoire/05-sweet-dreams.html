Example piece: a minor ostinato & vamp

Some songs barely change harmony at all. They take a short loop of chords, set a repeating figure spinning on top, and let repetition itself become the hook. Our example piece — a 1983 synth-pop landmark built on a Roland SH-101 bass line, in the style of “Sweet Dreams (Are Made of This)” — is the textbook case: a four-bar minor vamp and a relentless ostinato carry the whole track. This lesson uses it as a lens for how a looped minor progression and a repeated figure generate groove and dark mood. We analyse the harmony and the shape of the figure only; we do not transcribe the song.

  • Hear a four-bar minor vamp as a loop, not a journey.

  • Explain why an ostinato drives the groove.

1. What a “vamp” is

A vamp is a short chord pattern repeated as a backdrop — a loop the music sits on while a melody, a solo, or (in pop) a whole song plays out above it. Where a typical phrase moves somewhere — building tension toward a cadence — a vamp deliberately refuses to. It circles. The interest comes not from harmonic travel but from everything layered on top: the groove, the production, the vocal.

Our example piece is a vamp song. Its harmony is essentially one four-bar loop, repeated for the length of the track. So the question a theorist asks is simple: what is in the loop, and why does it sound the way it does?

2. The key: C minor

The example piece is in C minor. That single fact sets the mood before a chord is even named — the minor mode is the source of the song's brooding, insomniac colour. C natural minor (the Aeolian mode) gives us the raw materials:

C – D – E♭ – F – G – A♭ – B♭ – C. The three flats (B♭, E♭, A♭) are exactly the key signature of C minor, and the lowered 3rd (E♭) is what makes the tonic chord minor — the darkness lives in that one note. (Full treatment in Minor scales.)

3. The loop: a i–♭VI–V minor vamp

The repeating progression is three chords, looped over four bars: Cm – A♭ – G, with the Cm returning to close the loop. In Roman numerals for C minor that is i – ♭VI – V. Two of those three chords come straight out of the natural minor scale; one is borrowed, and it is the most interesting chord in the song.

Chord Roman numeral Notes Where it comes from
Cm i C–E♭–G tonic — built on scale degree 1 of C minor
A♭ major ♭VI A♭–C–E♭ diatonic in natural minor (built on ♭6̂)
G major V G–B♮–D borrowed — needs B♮, the raised leading tone
Why is the V chord major in a minor key?

Build a triad on the 5th degree of natural C minor and you get G–B♭–D — a minor v, weak and droopy. To get the bright, tense G major the song uses, you must raise the 7th degree of the scale from B♭ to B♮. That B♮ is the leading tone: a half step below the tonic C, it pulls hard back to home. Raising 7̂ to harden the dominant is the whole reason the harmonic minor scale exists. So the loop quietly switches scales: it lives in natural minor for the i and ♭VI, then borrows the leading tone for the V. (See Minor scales for natural vs. harmonic minor.)

That borrowed major V is also a small act of modal mixture — reaching outside the prevailing natural-minor collection for a chord with a stronger pull. The same machinery, used more adventurously, colours countless minor-key songs. (Modal mixture takes this much further.)

Here is the loop on both instruments — the tonic Cm triad, the chord the whole vamp orbits.

On the piano — the i chord (Cm)

C minor = C (root), E♭ (the lowered 3rd — the dark note, a black key), and G (5th). That E♭ is what separates this from the brighter C major.

On the guitar — the i chord (Cm)

A compact Cm voicing: C on the A string (5th string, 3rd fret), E♭ on the D string (4th string, 1st fret), and the open G string (marked O) as the 5th — root, ♭3, 5.

4. Why a borrowed V over a ♭VII would still “work”

Plenty of minor vamps swap our V for a ♭VII instead — giving the very common i–♭VI–♭VII loop (in C minor: Cm–A♭–B♭). That version stays entirely inside natural minor: no raised leading tone, no borrowing. It sounds more modal, more rock, slightly less “classical.” Compare the two:

Loop Third chord Scale used Flavour
i – ♭VI – V G major (B♮) natural + harmonic minor stronger pull home; a touch of tension/drama
i – ♭VI – ♭VII B♭ major (B♭) natural minor (Aeolian) only modal, open, “rock” — no leading tone

Knowing this fork is the practical pay-off: when you hear a minor vamp, the single most telling question is does the chord before the return raise the 7th (V) or keep it flat (♭VII)? That one note tells you whether you are in a borrowing-tinged or a purely modal world. (Modes & pentatonic covers the Aeolian sound this trades against.)

5. The ostinato: repetition as the engine

Ostinato is Italian for “obstinate” — a short figure that persistently repeats. In our example piece, a synth-bass figure loops continuously beneath the vamp; in pop and rock such a figure is also called a riff. The repetition is the point. It establishes a groove the listener locks into and anticipates, then lets attention drift to whatever changes above it — the vocal, a new texture, the production.

Why does relentless repetition feel good, not boring?

A repeated figure removes harmonic surprise, which frees the ear to attend to feel — micro-timing, accent, timbre — and to the few things that do change. Each return of the loop confirms a prediction the listener has made, and confirmed prediction is pleasurable; meanwhile the unchanging floor makes any new layer read as an event. A ground bass in a 17th-century Baroque basso ostinato works on exactly this principle, and so does a synth loop in 1983: a fixed foundation underneath developing variation.

6. The intervallic shape of a riff (generically)

We will not write the song's actual figure, but we can talk about how riffs are built — the kind of analysis you should do on any hook by ear. A memorable riff usually has a clear intervallic shape: a recognisable contour of leaps and steps. Some common minor-key building blocks:

  • A rising minor-third leap (e.g. C up to E♭, 3 half steps) — the signature “minor” colour as a gesture, instantly dark.
  • A drop to the 5th (root down to the 5th below, or up to the 5th above) — stable, anchoring, very common in bass riffs.
  • Stepwise fill between those leaps, walking through scale tones (often the natural-minor or minor-pentatonic notes) to connect the corners of the shape.

Our generic arpeggio in section 2 is exactly this skeleton: a root, a leap to the 5th, the minor 3rd, all from the Cm chord — chord tones outlining the harmony in time. When you study the real riff from a licensed score, ask: what is its highest and lowest note, which intervals are leaps, and which chord tone does it lean on? That shape — not the exact pitches — is what makes a riff yours to learn from. (Interval names are in Intervals; the minor pentatonic that riffs love is in Modes & pentatonic.)

7. Where the dark mood actually comes from

Pulling it together, the brooding character is the sum of small, nameable causes — not vague “sadness”:

  • The minor tonic — the E♭ in Cm, a lowered 3rd, is the root of the colour.
  • The ♭VI (A♭) — a major chord a half step above the dominant's root, lending a heavy, plush darkness as the loop dips.
  • The borrowed V with its leading tone — the lone raised note (B♮) injects tension and a pull home, a flash of brightness inside the gloom.
  • The ostinato — repetition turns all of the above into a trance: an inescapable, circling mood rather than a passing chord change.

That is the lesson of the vamp: with only three chords and one repeated figure, the harmony is almost static — and the song is entirely about what you do with that stillness.
