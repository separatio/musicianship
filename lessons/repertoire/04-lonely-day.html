Example piece: power chords, arpeggios & build

This lesson uses a well-known minor-key rock ballad — in the style of “Lonely Day” — as a lens, not a transcription. We will not reproduce its melody, riff, or tab. Instead we strip the song down to the three ideas that make it work: a simple chord loop, the power chord (root + perfect fifth, no third), and the way the same chords change character depending on whether you arpeggiate or strum them — which is exactly how a quiet verse grows into a loud chorus.   • Hear a minor-key loop as functions (i–VI–III–VII), not just chord names.
  • Build a power chord from first principles as a root plus a perfect fifth.
  • Know why a power chord is neither major nor minor — and why that matters.
  • Turn one chord into a verse (arpeggio) or a chorus (full strum) at will.
  • Read the form of the piece as a dynamic arc: build, peak, release.

1. The key, and why we transpose it

The recording sits in G♯ minor — the guitars are tuned down so the open low string is a low G♯, which is why it sounds so heavy. G♯ minor is a thicket of sharps to read, though, so for analysis we move everything up a half step to A minor — the no-sharps, no-flats key, and the key the most common standard-tuning play-alongs use. Nothing about the functions changes when you transpose; only the letter names do. (If you need to revisit why minor keys behave the way they do, that lives in Theory 7 — Minor scales.)

Why analyse in a different key than the record?

Roman numerals describe relationships, not pitches — a i chord is the home minor chord whether that is A minor or G♯ minor. Picking the key that is easiest to read (A minor) lets you see the pattern with no accidentals in the way, then transpose the finished understanding back to wherever you want to play it. This is the entire point of functional analysis.

2. The progression as block chords

The harmony is deliberately plain: a four-chord loop drawn entirely from the A natural-minor scale (A B C D E F G). Built in root-position triads it is i – VI – III – VII: A minor, F major, C major, G major. Three of those four are major chords sitting inside a minor key — the bright VI, III and VII against the dark home i is what gives the loop its bittersweet pull. This is the classic Aeolian (natural-minor) rock loop.

Read the bass motion and you can hear why it feels like it is always falling home: A → F is down a third, then C and G keep stepping, and the loop swings back to A. Functionally the VII (G major) here is a subtonic — a whole step below the tonic — not a leading tone, which is the natural-minor sound rather than a dominant pull. Whether a phrase feels finished or left hanging is a question of cadence: landing the loop back on i feels like an arrival, while ending on VII or III leaves it open and asking for more.

Modal mixture, briefly

When a minor-key song reaches for a chord that is not in the plain natural-minor scale — say a major IV instead of minor iv, or a major V to get a real leading tone — that borrowing is modal mixture, and it has its own home in Theory 19. Our loop here stays diatomic to natural minor, so we are getting the bittersweet colour purely from the major VI/III/VII, not from mixture. Worth knowing which effect you are hearing.

3. The power chord: a root and a perfect fifth

When the guitars get heavy, they almost never play those full triads. They play power chords — and a power chord is just two notes: the root and the note a perfect fifth above it (often with the octave doubled on top). That perfect fifth is exactly the interval you metered in Theory 5: seven half steps, the most stable interval after the octave.

The defining move is what the power chord leaves out: the third. The third is the note that decides major vs minor. Drop it and the chord becomes neither — harmonically ambiguous, able to sit over a major or a minor melody without clashing. That open, hollow stability is also why power chords stay clean under heavy distortion: the root–fifth frequency ratio is very close to the simple just ratio 3:2, so the overtones reinforce rather than fight each other, while a full third through a distorted amp turns to mud.

On the guitar — the generic fifth shape

One movable shape, shown as A5 at the 5th fret: root on the low E string (6th string), the perfect fifth two frets up on the next string (A string, 7th fret), and the octave root above it. Slide the whole shape up or down the neck and you have every power chord — the position-shifting you already practised.

On the piano — the same two pitches

A (root) up to E (its perfect fifth) — the identical interval, no third between them. Pianists rarely call it a “power chord,” but an open fifth in the left hand does the same job: stable, open, and neutral about major or minor.

Why “5” and not “maj” or “min”

A power chord is written A5, E5, and so on — the “5” tells you it is the bare fifth with the third removed. Because there is no third, you can stack a row of them and the ear hears raw weight and motion rather than a key colour. That is why the same power-chord riff can pivot between feeling major and minor depending only on the melody or bass underneath it. The pentatonic and blues scales you met in Theory 8 exploit the same ambiguity: leave out the notes that commit you, and you stay free to lean either way.

4. Same chords, different energy: arpeggio vs strum

Here is the lesson the song teaches better than almost any other: you can change a section's entire feeling without changing a single chord. Take the i chord. Spread its notes out one at a time — an arpeggio — and you get a quiet, spacious, almost fragile verse. Hit all the notes together as a full strum, loud, and you get a chorus that lands like a wall.

The two parameters you are trading are texture (how many notes sound at once — thin arpeggio vs thick block) and dynamics (how loud). Notation marks dynamics with the symbols you met in Theory 4 — Score-reading conventions: p (soft) under the verse, a crescendo hairpin into the chorus, f (loud) when it arrives. The arpeggio naturally sounds softer even at the same volume, because fewer strings ring at once — so texture and dynamics reinforce each other.

Try it on one chord

Take a single A minor chord and play it four ways in a row: (1) arpeggiated softly, fingerstyle; (2) arpeggiated but speeding up; (3) strummed quietly; (4) strummed hard with the power-chord shape. You have just built a whole song's dynamic arc out of one chord. That is the trick.

5. Form: a dynamic arc, not just a chord chart

Zoom out and the piece is a textbook verse–chorus form. The verse is low-intensity: clean tone, arpeggios, a thin texture, soft dynamics. The chorus is the peak: distortion, power chords, full strums, loud. The job of the verse is not just to deliver words — it is to make the chorus feel like an arrival by being everything the chorus is not. Drama in music is the management of contrast over time.

The general machinery of how composers shape sections — statement, contrast, return, climax — is the subject of Theory 24 — Form & analysis. What the example piece adds is a concrete, audible case: the same four chords run under both sections, and only the texture and dynamics change to separate verse from chorus.

Section Harmony Texture Dynamics Effect
Verse i–VI–III–VII (same loop) arpeggiated, clean, thin soft (p) intimate, building tension
(transition) same loop strumming begins, drums enter crescendo rising — promises a payoff
Chorus same loop, as power chords full strums / power chords, thick loud (f) release, the emotional peak
Why the loud part needs the quiet part

Loudness is relative. A chorus is only “big” compared to the verse before it — play the whole song at full volume and the chorus stops landing, because there is nothing for it to tower over. This is why so much rock keeps the verse deliberately restrained: the engineer of the emotional hit is the contrast, and contrast costs you nothing but patience. Hold the energy back so you have somewhere to go.
